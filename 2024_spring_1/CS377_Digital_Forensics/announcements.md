This week (Week 5) we are going to discuss hashing.  Simply put, a hash is a digital fingerprint and is unique to each file, drive, or system that is hashed.  You’ll learn that hashes come in different types such as MD5, SHA1, and a slew of others.  Regardless of the hash you use, the hash for a file, drive, or system should never change once the acquisition has taken place.  If it does, it means that something has changed on the media.  This is how you ensure the integrity of the evidence.

## Week 2 Recap / Week 3 Intro
*Posted: 2024-01-22T10:00:10Z*

Class,

Great job this past week.  This past week we discussed data acquisition.  Data acquisitions are at the heart of forensics.  When seizing digital evidence, it is critical that a forensic acquisition (bit-for-bit) copy of the media happen.  Rarely would you ever conduct a forensic examination of the original media.  Rather, you will use your forensic copy of the evidence to conduct your exam.  As you learned, acquisitions come in two flavors, static and live.  Most of the time you will do static acquisitions; however, there may be times when a live acquisition is necessary.  From past experience, live acquisitions usually involved business machines such as servers which were unable to be taken offline without the business suffering a financial or operational loss.

This week (week 3), we will expand our learning and will discuss Windows based forensics and related tools.  We will dive deep into the Windows disk structure, file system, registry, and other related areas.  Hands down, Windows is one of the most popular operating systems in the world and will be a common Operating System (OS) that you encounter in most forensic exams.

## Week 2 Mid-Week Announcement
*Posted: 2024-01-17T10:00:00Z*

Class,

As you have learned, a digital forensic acquisition is the process of creating a bit for bit copy of data on a storage device in a forensically sound manner. Once a forensic acquisition has been performed the acquired forensic image needs to be authenticated.

We are going to look at two types of forensic acquisitions – a logical acquisition and a physical acquisition.

In the case of a logical acquisition, what we are doing is essentially a copy of the files. You will obtain information similar to that which you would obtain if you were to open the digital media and copy the files that you can see from the device to your computer’s documents folder. Doing that, however, is not by itself forensically sound. If a file on the digital media has been deleted you won't be able to get it from a logical acquisition.

A physical acquisition is going to result in a bit for bit copy of not only the files that are present on the device but also files that have been deleted that have not been overwritten already.

## Week 1 Recap / Week 2 Intro
*Posted: 2024-01-15T10:00:00Z*

Class,

Great job this past week.  Week 1 is always the hardest ;-)  This past week, we discussed what digital forensics is, the differences between public and private sector investigations, the importance of maintaining professional standards, and requirements for data recovery.  You learned what a bit-stream copy is and the importance from a forensic perspective.  When it comes to forensic acquisitions, you want to ensure you always get everything on the system.  The only way to do that is by getting a bit-stream copy which is a bit-by-bit copy.  But getting the forensic copy isn’t enough.  You should always ensure you also get the hash value of the original media and the forensic copy.  MD5 and SHA1 hashes are acceptable.
