While researching a topic for our discussion based on the unit, I discovered that CDP is susceptible to spoofing. In a CDP spoofing attack, a threat actor can manipulate the Cisco Discovery Protocol (CDP) to circumvent network management systems and exploit Cisco IP phones (Attacking CDP, 2024). By injecting spoofed CDP frames, attackers trick management software into believing non-existent Cisco devices are present, prompting communication between neighbors. Through this, the threat actor can execute a DNS attack, potentially causing the device to crash (Cisco Discovery Protocol, 2020). Attackers can also discover SNMP community names and potentially exploit other Cisco devices. Similarly, IP phones can be misled into connecting to attacker-controlled VLANs and rogue DHCP servers, which makes man-in-the-middle attacks possible (Attacking CDP, 2024). SNMP community names play an important role in network management and monitoring, serving as a form of authentication and access control. Cisco recommends disabling CDP on interfaces that connect to non-Cisco (user stations) devices, as there is no encryption in the CDP packets (Cisco Discovery Protocol, 2020).
