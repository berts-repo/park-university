This week we will look at analyzing visual data. Visual data often seems reliable simply because it looks professional, but the way a graph, chart, or other form of visual organization is presented can create either an accurate representation of data or a skewed or inaccurate one. For example, let's think of a chart that is meant to show how much something has jumped in percentage or amount. Let's take a look at the following graph: 

 

Based on the purely visual aspect of this graph, you would think that welfare had jumped up multiple times from where it was in 2009. If you take a look at the numbers, though, you see that the count started at 94,000,00 and ended at 100,000,00. While these are both big numbers, the last number is not multiple times larger than the initial one, as one might gather by merely glancing at the chart (as many people do). If you start to look carefully at graphs in news, you might find many that mis-represent data--often not maliciously, but through a lack of clarity about the baseline number/percentage or incorrect choice of visualization to represent the data.

## Welcome to Week 3! 
*Posted: 2023-10-30T17:12:11Z*

Class, welcome to week 3!

This week, we start getting into the more advance world of rhetoric. On the surface, rhetoric simply refers to the how of an argument--does the author use evidence based on number to make a point, or do they use an emotional appeal? Does the author use their own expertise as the primary driver or something else? These questions and others will help you identify how the author puts together their argument and if those methods make sense for the topic or if they are manipulative tactics. Even beyond the how, we will start to investigate the soundness of evidence--not all research is created equal.

This week we will make our first steps toward a rhetorical analysis. The biggest thing to remember here is that when you are analyzing something, it is not about if agree or disagree or like or dislike--that would be an opinion piece. Rhetoric asks how something is argued and if those methods are sound. Your job is to take a first stab at it. Notice that this is a draft, so expect that the suggestions I give you should be used to revise the work later :)

This week you will be giving me the name of the professional in the field that you are interviewing. Before you begin to formulate the questions you want to ask your interviewee, review the rubric for the final assignment--your job is to ask about writing in the field and figure out how professionals use writing. ## Welcome to Week 1! 
*Posted: 2023-10-16T03:29:53Z*

Hello, all and welcome! This week, we will be laying the foundation for the entire class by learning how to slow down and carefully read academic texts and have an accurate summary. Often, we are taught to read for basic meaning and keywords. When you are in high school, this can be useful, as it helps you grasp the most important points. Once you are in college, though, you'll need to have a more nuanced understanding of a text, particularly around what argument the author is making, what the underlying assumptions are, and if the argument is based on facts or opinions (or in most cases, both!).

One thing I want you to begin to think about as this course starts is how this course differs from other writing courses. In many writing classes, you are asked to focus on a particular topic, this class asks you to think about and write about how authors in your field write. This is an important distinction, so I want to give an example. In a typical class, you might be asked to write an essay about a topic in psychology, for example. In this class, you would be asked to write about how authors in psychology write about case studies, for example, or how authors in the field handle introductions to research. Basically, you are begin asked to step back and look not just at the topic, but how authors in your field write about various topics.

In addition, I'd suggest you look at the syllabus to get a sense of the required work for each week and any major assignments. Review due dates for initial  DQ posts as well as follow ups. ##  Welcome to EN306!
*Posted: 2023-10-16T03:29:11Z*

Class, welcome to Professional Writing in the Discipline! In this class, we will be going beyond general writing advice to focusing in on writing in your particular field or discipline. Although there are many specific writing rules that will apply across disciplines (such as a need for appropriate use of grammar, punctuation, and spelling) other issues might vary. For example, fields such as science might require far more use of logic-focused writing than a field such as marketing.  Some fields might require very different formatting than others, or different ways of citing information. This course will help you identify the specific writing skills, resource identification, citation and style, as well as rhetorical expectations of your field.

In your first week, please check out the syllabus as well as the introductions section, where I'd love for you to post a bit about yourself and your goals as a student. I'd also encourage you to use this class as an opportunity to really expand your knowledge about of an aspect of your field that interests you and as a way to get familiar with recognizing different kinds of resources, know how to use them properly, and get more proficient in reading different types of texts in your field.
