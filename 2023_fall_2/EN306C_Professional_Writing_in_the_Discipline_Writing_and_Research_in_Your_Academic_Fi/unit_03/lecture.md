# Unit 3: Lecture

Rhetorical Analysis Assignment

In this unit, you are going to learn what rhetoric is, how to analyze and use rhetoric to better understand your discipline’s writing conventions and how to use rhetorical concepts to improve your own writing.  The content in this unit’s lecture will help you prepare your first draft of your rhetorical analysis essay that is due at the end of the unit, so be sure to read and view all the materials linked here. 

First, you need to know what rhetoric is.  Rhetoric is basically the field that studies persuasion, and all writing is persuasive to some degree.  To become a more rhetorically effective (more persuasive) writer in your field of study, it can be useful to analyze some writing in your discipline to discover the range of effective persuasive strategies used by experienced writers in your discipline. 

To get a more detailed understanding of rhetoric and some useful concepts, read the assigned chapters (links are on the unit overview page) from Writing Commons that will define rhetoric and explain in more detail the 4 basic rhetorical appeals: Ethos, Pathos, Logos, and Kairos.

How to Write Good Interview Questions

Start with more general questions, move towards specific ones.

A general question: What expectations are there for how to write an article in psychology?

A more specific question: What role does the personal play in writing in your field?

Even more specific: To what extent is the use of 1st person acceptable, and if so, in what situations? Do authors mention themselves or their prior research? How do authors build their credibility and authority in their writing?

Avoid yes/no questions

Do writers in your field use a standard format?

Vs. 

What methods of organization can writers in your field use? Where do those formats come from? How do people learn them?

Come from different angles

Ask not only about their experience writing and researching for the field; also consider that their knowledge about writing has also been informed by and informs:

Their experience as a student of the discipline

Their experience as a teacher of the discipline

Their possible experience editing a journal, reviewing other scholars’ work, etc.

Use the texts we have read to formulate questions

You might develop questions that address the claims our readings have made:  e.g. Source x claims…do you think that’s true for your field?  Remember that all of our sources focus on particular conventions of writing in multiple fields that you could ask about.

Be curious

If you don’t understand an answer, ask for clarification

If they say something intriguing, ask follow up questions

Ask questions you really want answered as a student of that discipline

Have a plan, but be flexible

Sometimes the information you think you want is not as interesting as what your interviewee brings up

Look for opportunities to elicit information you hadn’t considered beforehand

Be listening for statements/information you didn't expect

A few more tips

Make sure to take good notes (if you want to record the interview, ask for permission first)

Be sure to say thank you for their time

You may ask if they’d be willing to answer follow-up questions by email (however, emphasize that you wouldn’t take up much of their time, and that you just want to be sure you accurately represent their interview with you)

Make sure you re-read the assignment sheets for the interview write-up and for the final researched analysis assignment, and that you have prepared questions that cover all the areas you are expected to address in the interview write-up and that might help you begin to formulate an idea for your final researched analysis essay.
