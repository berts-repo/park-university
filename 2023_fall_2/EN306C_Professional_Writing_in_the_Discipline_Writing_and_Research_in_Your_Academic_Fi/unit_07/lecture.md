Using Sources Effectively

A major part of any researched project is using sources to help you build your argument and then integrating your sources smoothly into your own writing.  Your discussion for this unit will give you the chance to experiment with different methods of integrating quotes and paraphrases.  

Before you work on the discussion post, read the Summarizing, Paraphrasing,  and Quoting chapters of Writing Commons (links on unit overview), paying special attention to the sections on dropped quotes, paraphrasing in your own words, how to choose paraphrasing or quoting, and how to tell your readers when you are citing, paraphrasing, or summarizing.  Also review this  [link: https://canvas.park.edu/courses/77415/files/10536444/download?verifier=iv5zJDgoRhwztWTgH2nfNVJ2vE3bYy4vnY3iJne2&wrap=1] PowerPoint on Integrating Quotations. 

Watch  [link: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FNR0XjXc5WU] this video for a discussion and more examples of proper paraphrasing.  Paraphrasing in particular can be tricky because it is easy to reproduce something too close to the language of the original or to not make it clear to the reader where paraphrased material begins and ends (which borders on plagiarism). 

You may also want to review in-text citation rules for the citation style in your discipline.   [link: https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/purdue_owl.html] Purdue’s OWL has a great guide to using in-text citations for each of the major citation style. 

Examples: 

Once you have reviewed the unit materials on integrating sources, also take a look at the following examples that will show you a variety of ways a single quotation could be integrated or paraphrased. Note the different choices that can be made about signal phrases and sentence structure.  Also, notice how the writer marks the boundaries between the quoted and paraphrased materials and their own arguments. 

Source:

Article: “Gossip as Social Control: Informal Sanctions on Ethical Violations in Scientific Workplaces.”

Authors:  Vaidyanathan, Brandon, Khalsa, Simranjit and Ecklund, Elaine Howard

Publication:  Social Security Bulletin. 2016, Vol. 63 Issue 4, p. 554-572.

Passage from the original:

“Existing research on science ethics is focused primarily on three types of misconduct—fabrication,

falsification, and plagiarism in the research process” (555).

APA style used with a paraphrase 

In “Gossip as Social Control: Informal Sanctions on Ethical Violations in Scientific Workplaces,” Brandon Vaidyanathan et al. (2016) show that research on scientific misconduct tends to concentrate on scientists who make up or misrepresent their data (p. 555).   While Vaidyanathan et al. explore gossip as scientific misconduct, I argue that there is a much more widespread ethical issue that deserves more research: exploitation of graduate students. 

MLA style used with paraphrase

 In “Gossip as Social Control: Informal Sanctions on Ethical Violations in Scientific Workplaces,” Brandon Vaidyanathan et al. criticize research on scientific misconduct for concentrating only on scientists who make up or misrepresent their data or scientists who plagiarize someone else’s work (555).   While Vaidyanathan et al. identify gossip as scientific misconduct, I argue that there is a much more widespread ethical issue that deserves more research: exploitation of graduate students.

APA style with a direct quote:

According to Brandon Vaidyanathan et al. (2016), “[e]xisting research on science ethics is focused primarily on three types of misconduct—fabrication, falsification, and plagiarism in the research process” (p. 555).   This suggests that some common kinds of scientific misconduct, such as exploitation of graduate students, are allowed to flourish unchecked. 

MLA style with direct quote:

Brandon Vaidyanathan et al. argue that “[e]xisting research on science ethics is focused primarily on three types of misconduct—fabrication, falsification, and plagiarism in the research process” (555).  This suggests that some kinds of scientific misconduct, such as exploitation of graduate students, are allowed to flourish unchecked.  This is particularly problematic, as exploitation of graduate student labor is far more common than plagiarism or falsification of data. 

Direct quote, with alterations, in MLA style

“Existing research on science ethics focuse[s] primarily on…fabrication, falsification, and plagiarism” according to Vaidyanathan et al. (555).  This means that the least common types of misconduct get the most attention, while researchers turn a blind eye to more common forms of misconduct, such as exploitation of graduate students.
