I will be using the secondary source from Leeson, How Histories Begin: A Note on the Writing of Openings, with the passage:

“The final type of introduction—the reflective opening—consists, as its name suggests, of general statements about history or historiography.”

To use this in direct quotation in my own text I would use the statement: Author implements a reflective opening, which Leeson (2011), "The final type of introduction—the reflective opening—consists, as its name suggests, of general statements about history or historiography." This would be considered a “short quotation” as it meets Jennifer Janechek’s et al. definitions which qualifies as a short question; by referring to a definition, and because it is analyzing a specific quote, and keeps the authors style and tone (Janecheck et al., 2023). Using a direct quotation allows the original text to speak for itself, maintaining the author's intended tone.

Paraphrasing involves rewording the source material into your own words while retaining the original meaning. We could say that a reflective opening in a piece of writing often includes the history or the study of historical writing.

A combination of both quoting and paraphrasing can provide clarity where needed. I could introduce the idea by paraphrasing and then use a direct quote for specific emphasis. For example: In discussing various types of introductions, one common form is the reflective opening, which is characterized by its broad observations about history or historiography. As the Leeson et al. (2011) states, "The final type of introduction—the reflective opening—consists, as its name suggests, of general statements about history or historiography." This method allows the writer to frame the source material in a way that aligns with their own narrative.
