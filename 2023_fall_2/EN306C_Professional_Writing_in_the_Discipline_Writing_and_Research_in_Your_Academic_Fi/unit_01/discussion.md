In "Does Reality Backbite? Physical, Verbal, and Relational Aggression in Reality Television Programs" by Coyne et al., the six academic writing moves described by Teresa Thonney are implemented as follows:

Responding to Other Writers: The authors engage with previous studies on media and aggression, creating the context of their research.

Stating the Value and Plan: The importance of their study by addressing the gap in research on types of aggression in reality TV.

Acknowledging Potential Disagreement: The authors cautiously interpret their findings, acknowledging limits and the potential for different interpretations.

Adopting an Authoritative Voice: They confidently present their methodology and findings, as experts in the field.

Using Academic and Discipline-Specific Vocabulary: The paper is filled with technical terms relevant to media studies and psychology, such as "relational aggression" and "content analysis."
