# Unit 1: Lecture

Tips for reading and summarizing complex academic essays 

Phase 1: Reading and Understanding

Before you can accurately summarize a source, you have to read it carefully and understand it in all its specificity.  Until now, you may have learned many techniques for reading quickly, catching the gist, or in other words, skimming to get a basic overview of an article.  Many standardized tests, for example, test skimming skills rather than deep reading.  However, academic writing often demands the opposite style of reading – reading closely and deeply to understand not just the main point, but all the finer points, evidence, and reasoning that helped the author arrive at their main point.  As you begin this course, you will learn some techniques for reading and breaking down academic articles that will help you not only complete your first assignment, but that will also help you in the rest of the course as well as your other courses at Park.  There are several effective methods of reading closely.  Your assigned readings from Writing Commons describes some methods such as active reading, annotating the text, distinguishing main claims from sub-claims; the following material describes some others.  Practice each of these until you find a method that works for you and for the particular task, and keep practicing these techniques as you move through the units in this course.  

Find the thesis: 

A thesis may be a one sentence statement and it may not be – complicated arguments may take several sentences.   Some writers may start with a clearly stated thesis and then show you the evidence they used to arrive at the thesis.  In this case, you may find the thesis statement just after the introduction.  However, some writers may work by starting with an issue, problem, or question, and accumulate and examine evidence which is then pieced together to finally reveal the full thesis near the end of the essay.  In some cases, a writer may work with an implied thesis, in which the entire thesis is not stated fully in any one place, but is assumed to be pieced together by a careful and (usually) informed reader. 

Tips for finding the thesis: 

Pay attention to repetitions of key phrases, ideas, arguments: repetition usually signals importance

Look in positions of emphasis: beginnings or endings of essays, sections, or paragraphs. 

List out all the major arguments: What larger argument do they all add up to?

Examine the organization:

Break the essay into parts: Where does the essay seem to break into pieces or change directions?  Try to paraphrase the main argument in each section (not just the topic, also what is argued about the topic).  What is the relationship between each part? What is the relationship between each part and the thesis?

You can also try constructing a sentence outline which gives a one sentence summary of the point of each paragraph along with a statement of what purpose the paragraph serves in the essay (i.e. Is it introducing an issue or problem? Examining evidence that provides support for a claim? Summarizing a secondary source that will be applied to the evidence in the next paragraph? Etc.)

Target difficult words, phrases, and passages:

Look up any unfamiliar words, phrases, key people or theories that are mentioned

Use context to figure out meaning

Try to paraphrase in your own words and check your paraphrase against the rest of the essay (If the passage means x, does this make sense with the rest of the essay?)

Pay attention to repetitions (both exact repetitions and synonyms or restatements) – if a word or phrase is used in multiple places, look at each of those places to see if there’s some similarity in how the word or phrase is used

Understand the author, audience, and purpose: 

Author:

To what extent is this author authoritative about this topic?

Why they are interested in this topic?

From what point of view are they viewing the topic? What things are they simply assuming?

Audience:

What can you tell from the essay about who the audience is and what they know?

Where was this published and what types of audiences would read it?

What clues does the author give you about the audience being addressed? 

What is the audience assumed to know and what does this tell us about the audience?

Purpose(s):

Why has the author written this essay?  What are they trying to achieve?

What can you tell about what has already been said about the topic?

What knowledge is the author attempting to add to the conversation and why?  Why is this important for others to know? 

Phase 2: Writing a Good Summary 

Once you have broken down the article and really understand its pieces and parts, you can now reconstruct your understanding of the article in a summary.  Keep in mind that summaries fulfill many purposes in academic writing, and although we are going to practice a balanced, neutral, and comprehensive summary in this unit, in practice, you will find summaries are not always balanced or comprehensive, or sometimes, they are tinged with an evaluation (this is good/bad, positive/negative).  This is because summaries serve specific persuasive purposes.  It might be the case that the author’s purpose is to evaluate a theory and thus their summary might be colored with negatively-loaded language.  Similarly, an author’s purpose may only require summarizing a part of an article (the part that they are interested in).  Your purpose in the summary assignment is to show your ability to read and understand an entire article on its own terms, which is why you are being asked to be balanced and neutral for the time being. 

Here are some tips for good summary writing.  Following the list is an annotated sample summary that shows these tips in action.  Make sure to read the summary and the annotated comments for examples of how to use these tips in practice. 

Argument: Start with the main argument and/or purpose no matter where it actually appears in the piece you are summarizing.  Your organizational choices should be based on how it helps a reader understand your summary, not on how the original author organized the essay. The best organization may not match the order in which they present arguments. 

Author/context: It is conventional to mention the author’s entire name and the title of the piece being summarized in the first sentence.  In some fields, the date of publication will also be important.  After the first mention, it is conventional to refer to people by their last name only (never the first name only).

Tone: Make sure to maintain a neutral tone and choice of words if that is the objective of the assignment.  If the assignment is pure summary, avoid inserting your own personal beliefs and opinions and avoid judging the arguments being made. 

Attribution: Use attributive tags to make sure a reader is able to clearly judge which ideas and arguments belong to the source. This is especially important when using summary to accomplish some other task (to provide the basis for a critique, for example).  Your reader needs to be able to tell the difference between what the source says and what you say.  You can also use descriptive verbs in your tags to help quickly capture the tone and purpose of the author.  For example, Smith argues that…Jones critiques ideas which…Brown compares…

Connections: Try to capture not only what the author argues, but the relationships between those arguments and the purpose they served in the original argument.  You can often give readers this sense very quickly through your choice of transitions and conjunctions.

Compression: Compress multiple ideas into one sentence using complex, compound sentence structures, or subordination (be careful, however, not to create grammatical errors when you do so). 

Examples: In longer summaries, provide some examples that show how the author supported his or her claims – you needn’t reproduce all of their evidence, but give one or two of the best pieces of evidence. 

Paraphrasing: Make sure when you paraphrase that you do put the ideas in your own words and in your own sentence structures (simply replacing with synonyms word for word is actually considered plagiarism, since the writer is borrowing the writer’s original sentence structure).  For the summary, don’t use any quoted material, but keep in mind that in practice, academics sometimes blend quotes and paraphrases when summarizing texts (however, they always demonstrate that they understand what the quotes they use mean in the original text).

Balance: For the unit 1 assignment, make sure your summary is balanced (it doesn’t spend disproportionate amounts of time on one particular section of the original text). However, keep in mind, that depending on your purpose in summarizing, in practice writers often focus their summary on parts that are most relevant to their purpose.  However, typically in doing so, they are careful not to take those parts out of context. 

Comprehensiveness: Cover the main points the author made to support the thesis – If there is a chain of reasoning to arrive at the “proof” of the thesis, make sure you represent each link in that chain (i.e. the author starts with point a, which is used to build to point b, which helps persuade an audience of point c, which then supports the entire thesis).

What are disciplines? What is academic writing? What writing conventions are common to most academic disciplines?

To effectively learn about writing in your discipline, you need to know what a discipline is, what writing conventions are common to most academic disciplines, and what writing conventions are specific to your discipline.  Your assigned reading on  [link: https://courses.lumenlearning.com/suny-jeffersoncc-styleguide/chapter/exploring-academic-disciplines/] disciplines in the College Writing Handbook offers you one way to understand disciplines as discourse communities with specific expectations for “vocabularies, styles, and modes of communication.”  For example, the style of research and writing in biochemistry is markedly different than that used by art historians.  Experts in these disciplines are members of the discourse community by virtue of their familiarity with the field, its expectations, methodologies, and style of communication.  Disciplines have developed over time; for an overview of how disciplines have developed and changed, see this  [link: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discipline_(academia)] Wikipedia article on disciplines and this  [link: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outline_of_academic_disciplines] list for one way to categorize and distinguish various disciplines.  Can you find your area of study on this list? How is it categorized? 

However, disciplines are not always so easily separated and distinguished from one another.  Many disciplines share common traditions that may make them similar to one another; for example, many of the natural sciences such as biology and chemistry, share an emphasis on empirical methods (the scientific method), an emphasis on quantitative data, and a specific style of writing (shorter articles, no first person, IMRAD format).  At a finer level, however, writing in biochemistry and biology are not exactly the same and will differ in ways that experts in each field will recognize.  Becoming a part of the discourse community in your field means learning the writing conventions that are expected by academia, by your disciplinary area, by your specific discipline, and perhaps even a more specific sub-discipline.  For example, to become an effective writer in biochemistry, you have to learn the writing conventions expected by most of academia, those expected in the sciences in general, those expected by chemistry, and more specifically those expected in the specific subfield of biochemistry. 

One way of grouping disciplines is to use a fairly traditional model of separating disciplines into areas with common traditions; for example, fields that belong to the humanities are understood to share some common expectations, theories, methods, and history, while the social sciences are different in some important ways; likewise, the hard sciences operate differently from the humanities and the social sciences.  However, there are disciplines that appear to stand outside of or even between these traditional boundaries.  For example, disciplines built around specific professions such as business or nursing may draw methods from both the sciences and the social sciences, but will be classified by many schools as neither of those categories. 

Even more complex is the trend towards inter and cross-disciplinary fields of study that may combine multiple methods or traditions into a new area of study.  For example, gender studies is a cross-disciplinary field that includes scholars in many fields drawing from many disciplinary backgrounds (English, history, communication, sociology, psychology, linguistics, and even some of the sciences).  Some of you may be interdisciplinary majors yourselves, and thus your college major may be situated between two different disciplines.

While this sounds complicated, it is much easier to learn when you have some specific tools that help you examine and articulate these conventions; this course is designed to give you those tools.  In this unit, we are going to begin with the discourse community of academia, the widest set of expectations you will be working with as a scholar in your field.  Your assigned article this week, Teresa Thonney’s “ [link: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/258999848_Teaching_the_Conventions_of_Academic_Discourse_Teaching_English_in_the_Two-Year_College_384_2011_347-62] Teaching the Conventions of Academic Discourse,” will discuss 6 basic expectations she believes are shared by all disciplines.  Your discussion in this unit will give you a chance to practice identifying these conventions in a piece of academic writing. 

In future units, you will get the tools you need to begin to distinguish your discipline from others as well.  You will end the course with a research project of your own design in which you investigate some specific writing convention in your field of study.  Included below is the final assignment in this course.  Review this assignment carefully, and begin brainstorming ideas for your final project as we work through the material in the first 4 units.  In Unit 4, you will be writing a formal proposal and bibliography for your final project, so don’t wait to think about the final project until then.  The readings, discussions, and assignments leading up to unit 4 are useful for discovering or kicking around ideas that you might develop into your final project, so just keep this in mind as you work on these early assignments.  Finally, don’t forget that your instructor is great resource for talking through ideas for your final project!

Unit 8 Assignment:  Final draft of the Researched Analysis (Core Assessment) 

Introduction

In this unit, you will submit your final 7-9 page researched analysis essay on some aspect of your chosen discipline’s discourse. 

Directions

Your final essay in this class is a 7-9 page researched analysis of some aspect of your chosen discipline’s discourse.  There are a number of ways you could come at this assignment. For example, you could choose to compare different kinds of scholarly journals in your field in order to make claims about how they might differ in their preferred writing styles.  You might compare a scholar’s public writing in more popular venues to the writing they would do to scholars in their own field. You might choose to investigate some aspect of the writing that seems confusing or perplexing to you, or that seems most central to understanding how the disciplinary discourse works. You could focus your research on a writing issue that seems to be controversial or contested in the field you are investigating (for example, some fields aren’t settled on the uses of first person narratives in scholarly discourse). You could even choose to look at how the discourse in your chosen field has changed over time by looking at older journals versus newer ones.

Of course, there are a number of different angles I haven’t even thought of that could certainly make good projects.  The articles we have read can help you decide on an angle and a methodology for collecting and analyzing your data.  For example, following Leeson’s lead, you might compare introductory strategies in a set of articles chosen from a journal in your discipline.  Or you might use Golafshani’s categories for cataloguing the uses of first person reference to analyze a discipline or journal he does not investigate. 

For every project, you will be doing an analysis of some primary sources that you have chosen (examples of the scholarly discourse, such as articles, journals, etc.) and supplementing and developing that analysis with secondary sources (that is, other sources that talk about writing in that field, including discipline-specific writing textbooks, interviews with experts, as well as research on writing in that field). You may use sources drawn from our course materials, but you must also do research to find and use sources you found yourself, including at least one scholarly, peer-reviewed publication that does not come from our course materials. 

The development of this final essay will be supported by a number of ancillary assignments, including the expert interview, a project proposal, and an annotated bibliography.

When your instructor evaluates your essay, they will be looking for

A clear, specific, and analytically focused thesis

A well-developed argument with sound logical reasoning and support for the argument

Effective use and integration of evidence from primary and secondary sources

Effective organization

Effective introduction and conclusion

Effective style, punctuation, and grammar

Citations correctly formatted according the citation style preferred by your field

Evidence of revision from the first to the final draft
