# Unit 8: Lecture

Revising, Editing, Proofreading

Welcome to Unit 8!  In this unit, we will focus entirely on revising, editing, and proofreading your final essay.    Your readings, this lecture, and UNC’s Writing Center webpage will give you the tools to effectively revise your draft, edit for grammatical errors, and proofread for typos. 

Revising:

Revision is the process of making higher-level, meaning-making changes to your draft.  Generally, you should begin with revising as there is no point editing sections of your draft that may end up completely rewritten.

You have several resources at your disposal for helping you revise your document. 

Use the feedback you got from your peers and instructor last week and tackle the biggest or most important issues first.

Use what you learned from this week's reading in Writing Commons on revision to help you systematically revise your document.

Editing:  

Editing is the process of making sentence-level changes to improve grammar.  To edit, you need to be familiar with some of the more common errors, and you should have a sense of the kinds of errors you typically make.

The Writing Commons chapter on editing strategies is a good place to start to identify common grammatical errors and how to fix them.

Review your own patterns of error: Review comments on your work throughout the term.  What kinds of errors has your instructor marked before?  Look up the errors on  [link: https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/purdue_owl.html] Purdue OWL and review the error and how to fix it (navigate to general writing in the index on the left, then choose mechanics, grammar, or punctuation for more information on each).   Then, go through your document looking just for those errors. 

Proofreading: 

Proofreading is the process of doing a final pass-through of a document looking for any typos or other lingering issues not caught at the editing stage.  Professional editors have a number of tried-and-tested proofreading strategies to help catch errors your brain would ordinarily pass over. 

One useful trick is to print the document out and edit on a hard copy, as you will catch more errors.

Professional proofers also start with the last sentence of the document and work backwards through the document. This helps disrupts the logical flow of the content, which helps your brain not skip over errors. 

Use these tips to effectively revise, edit, and proofread to polish your final essay due Sunday.
