Disciplinary Forms of Organization

You will be reading two articles in this unit that examine how specific academic disciplines organize their texts and then reflect on the organizational conventions used in your own discipline.    As  [link: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC442179/] Solacci and Pereira’s article shows us, disciplinary forms of organization evolve over time in response to changing needs of a discipline, the availability of particular technologies (such as printing and digital word processing), and the influence of related disciplines.  For example, Solacci and Pereira observe that physics has already adopted the IMRAD formula by the 1950s which may have influenced other scientific fields to adopt the same form.  They also explain that editors of scientific journals began to insist on IMRAD format because it facilitated the process of peer review and because it made reading scientific articles for specific pieces of information easier (Solacci and Pereira call this “modular reading” as opposed to linear reading in which a reader reads from beginning to end).  It is important to recognize that these disciplinary conventions about organization are intimately linked to the way intellectual work gets done in a particular discipline: it reflects the way knowledge is made, the methods used by the discipline, and the reading needs of the disciplinary audience.  For example, IMRAD works for many scientific fields because it is a good representation of the scientific method in which you start with a hypothesis (Introduction), develop an experiment (Methods), run the experiment and collect data (Results), analyze the data (Analysis), and then discuss how the data confirms or rejects your hypothesis (Discussion).  You might also note that since replicability is an important value in scientific disciplines, following a set format also allows scientists to more easily reproduce an experiment to reproduce its findings. 

Non-scientific disciplines, however, have a different set of methodologies and values, and thus they do not necessarily find a set format like IMRAD useful for representing their work.  Disciplines in the humanities, for example, have evolved a different set of needs and organizational strategies.  While many humanities fields do not have a set format, that does not mean that anything goes.  For example,  [link: http://www.societyforhistoryeducation.org/pdfs/Leeson.pdf] Leeson’s article analyzes introductory strategies specific to the discipline of history; he discovers that although there is no standard format as there is in many scientific disciplines, there are 5 main strategies historians regularly use to introduce their articles.  This means that history too has evolved a common set of conventions and expectations that meet their needs.

This becomes even more complicated when you consider scholars who are working in cross- or inter-disciplinary fields.  For example, a historian who does work in women’s studies may find writing conventions and expectations differ from those used by military historians.  Similarly, political science has scholars who may take quantitative and/or qualitative approaches, and each approach has a different strategy for organizing information.  

As you read Solacci and Pereira’s article, consider these questions:

Does your discipline have a set format (IMRAD or something similar)?

If not, can you identify some regular patterns in how articles are organized?

Why might your discipline find this organization strategy useful? What needs of readers in the discipline might it satisfy?

How are the values and methods of the discipline reflected in its organizational strategies?

As you read Leeson’s article, you might consider these questions:

What introductory strategies are typically used in your discipline? Are any of them similar to those Leeson describes or do writers in your discipline fulfill different purposes when introducing an article?

Similarly, can you identify concluding strategies in articles in your field? What are writers in your discipline trying to accomplish in a conclusion? 

As you consider these questions, try to go beyond the general idea that they just introduce the topic or that they sum up the article, as academic writers tend to have more specific purposes and strategies in their introductions and conclusions, as Leeson shows us. 

You also may find this chapter of the  [link: https://courses.lumenlearning.com/suny-jeffersoncc-styleguide/chapter/introductions-and-conclusions/] College Writing Handbook useful for articulating strategies for introductions and conclusions and for composing your own introductions and conclusion for your final essay.

The handbook notes some common introductory moves:

Provide background about a topic

Locate readers in a specific time and/or place.

Start with a compelling quotation or statistic, or a particularly interesting piece of your evidence

Include an ethical appeal, with which you (explicitly or implicitly) show that you’ve done your homework and are credible (remember an ethical appeal is an appeal based on your authority, trustworthiness, and credibility, not an appeal to ethical thinking).

Articulate a main claim/thesis.

Lay out the stakes for the piece of writing—that is, why the reader should bother reading on.

Here are a few more introductory strategies you may see in academic writing:

Lay out a perspective on the topic the writer intends to challenge in some way.

Summarize the work already done on a topic to set up what the author will be contributing to the academic conversation that has not already been said or examined carefully.

The handbook also lays out some common concluding strategies:

Summarize the argument (especially in longer pieces of writing)

“Bookend” a story that started in the introduction

Include an emotional appeal, with which you (explicitly or implicitly) connect the “logic” of the argument to a more passionate reason intended to sway the reader

Issue a call to action

Some other strategies you may see academic writers use in conclusions

Answer the “So What?” question (why do the results of the author’s analysis matter? Why should we care about it? What are the further implications of the author’s claim?)

Call for more research in a specific area (the analysis may reveal a gap in the research that the writer cannot fulfill in the present article, but that sets the groundwork for further research).
