In the field of cybersecurity, I noticed that articles often follow a structured format, although it doesn’t necessarily follow the IMRaD (Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion) format described in this week’s reading, that Luciana Solacci and Mauricio Pereira describe. In my post I aim to look at the differences between formatting requirements, for different peer-reviewed journal related to my field of cybersecurity. I also am going to target how the authors employ different “introductory” techniques, described by Leeson.

When looking up the submission requirements for a couple of peer-reviewed journals, Journal of Cybersecurity, and the International Journal of Information Security, I notice some formatting difference: the use of citation had different requirements in the two journals, and the total words aloud in the text, along with some other differences. Generally, cybersecurity journals follow the IMRaD format, and implement the fallowing structure: abstract, keywords, affiliation, introduction, methodology, literary review (especially when proposing new methods), results, discussion, conclusion, and references. Typically, the articles put more emphasis on methodology and results, especially when discussing new technologies, vulnerabilities, or defensive mechanisms.

When analyzing the introduction in different articles related to cybersecurity, they all employ different combinations of openings, but focus on “locative” and “motive” structure (Leeson, 2011). I have often found that the articles I have been analyzing also integrate “argumentative” and commonly “reflective” openings in conjunction with the locative and motive openings (Leeson, 2011). In my article for analysis, Smart Approach for Botnet Detection Based on Network Traffic Analysis, Obeidat and Yaqbeh’s introduction starts by giving a reflective opening, focusing on the discussion of what “botnet” is and the common communication protocols used to detect this threat. Obeidat and Yawbeh then combine the “motive opening” to identify their proposal and how it joins the conversation.

My exploration into the formatting and introductory styles of cybersecurity articles reveals a nuanced structure. While journals in this field generally follow the IMRaD structure, they demonstrate unique variations, particularly in citation styles and word limits. Additionally, the incorporation of different introductory techniques, as highlighted by Leeson (2011), illustrates the approaches authors use to engage the reader.
