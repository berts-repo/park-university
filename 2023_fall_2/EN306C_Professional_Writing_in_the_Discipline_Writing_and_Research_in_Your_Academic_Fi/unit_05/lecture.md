Visual Rhetoric in Academic Writing

Just like many forms of writing in the 21st century, academic writing increasingly uses visual elements to persuade an audience.  For example, you might consider how some of the articles we have already read used visuals or visual elements to develop or enhance their arguments.  For example, if we look at the article you summarized for the Unit 1 assignment, you can see that Coyne et al. used a number of tables to quickly summarize their statistical data in a more readable from that allows the reader to more easily compare reality versus reality television shows and different types of violence.  Figure 1 below shows one of their tables summarizing their statistical data.  The table is designed to emphasize the total incidences of violence per hour, which supports the author’s main point well.  However, consider this: What if the authors wanted to emphasize a slightly different point that their data might support, perhaps that reality TV has lower incidences per hour of physical violence than non-reality TV?  How would they need to design this table differently to make that point come more clearly into view?

If you are not well-versed in statistics, this section might be quite daunting for you, which is one reason why the table is so useful: it allows the data to be accessible to scholars who have less familiarity with formal statistics.  You might wonder then why Coyne et al. include this section at all, but the information they include here is very important to scholars who are well-versed in statistics as it allows them to examine whether the data presented is credible in terms of statistical methods and significance. 

Presenting numerical data is not the only function visuals or visual elements can serve in an academic article, and different disciplines use different types of visuals in ways that suit their discipline.  An art historian or critic might include a visual of a painting they are analyzing so the reader can see the details that are being analyzed.  A geography information systems (GIS) specialist might include maps that illustrate the location, relationship, or density of particular geographic features in a given location.  An engineering professor might include diagrams that illustrate a fabrication process.  Of course, some disciplines use more visual than others: a discipline that is very text-oriented, for example some areas of literary study, may use no images at all, while highly visual disciplines like graphic design may use many visuals. 

Beyond actual images, however, there are also visual elements of document design that enhance the reading experience, and most disciplines use these features to some degree, though the ways they use them may be highly specialized to the discipline.  For example, as Bahls et al. point out in their analysis of mathematical writing, visual organizers, such as headings, indentations, bullets, lists, and whitespace are used to help readers identify relationships between different sections of the article and to more easily skim the article when looking for particular pieces of information.

Depending on your final research topic, you may find it helpful to consider whether a data visualization would help explain or enhance your points, and you should also consider whether using some visual organizers might enhance your reader’s understanding of your essay. 

Visuals and visual organizers can help you:

Make important points stand out

Emphasize material that might be lost in paragraph form

Present complex material (for example dense numerical data) more compactly or clearly

To choose the right visuals, you should do the following:

Identify the Purpose of the Visual

                What persuasive work is the visual meant to do?

                What goal does the visual serve?

                What argument or information does the visual need to convey?

Identify the audience

                How much technical knowledge do they have?

                What are their expectations for visuals?

                What kinds of visuals are they likely to be familiar with?

Choose the right visual to tell the story

                What kind of relationship are you trying to show?

What kind of visual is best suited to your purpose and data? 

Your assigned reading from Writing Commons on data visualizations mentions a range of possible visualization types from tables and charts to photographs or diagrams, and gives more detailed guidelines for creating visualizations of each type.   Each type of visualization has a specific purpose, a type of information or relationship it is designed to show especially well.  To pick the right type of visual, you have to think critically about what possible visualization styles are available and what purpose those visualizations serve.  The following website shows even more possibilities for visualization types (roll over each type for a pop-up example of each one):   [link: http://www.visual-literacy.org/periodic_table/periodic_table.html] Periodic Table of Visualization Methods

Making Visuals

You don’t need to be a graphic designer to make visualizations, nor do you need fancy or expensive software.  The operating system and office programs that you already use have simple tools you can use to make graphics.  For example, Microsoft Word and Excel can make tables, charts, and graphs.

You can find tutorials on the web that show you how to use these functions in your particular edition of MS Office.  For example, here is a quick tutorial on how to insert charts in Word:
