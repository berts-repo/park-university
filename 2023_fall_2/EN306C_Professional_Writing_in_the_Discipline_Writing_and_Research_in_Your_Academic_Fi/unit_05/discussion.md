**Posted:** 2023-11-17T06:06:27Z

Class,

In my research article, Smart Approach for Botnet Detection Based on Network Traffic Analysis, the authors Obeidat and Yaqbeh make use of many different types of visual rhetoric that is persistent throughout the entire text. The authors make use of 9 different figures, which include models, methodologies, and formulas, as well as 5 tables that include various elements. The text also includes many different types of lists and uses white space around formulas, to make the methodologies easy to read, compare, and evaluate as the technical information is being disseminated.

In the second figure of Obeidat and Yaqbeh’s article, which gives a visual representation of the proposed methodology that the authors are trying to employ; the authors break their text into headings, subheadings as well as an ordered list to explain their proposal. The figure represents the process that their ordered list explains, giving a visual outline of what the authors are trying to portray, as well as outlining the rest of their research paper. This is a great example of the use of “repetition” in Katherine McGee, Review of Best Practices in Data Visualization (2023). The figure also uses light colors that are easy to read with the “contrast” of the white background, which continues to follow McGee’s context of best practices for visual rhetoric (2023).

Further into the proposed methodology section the author makes use of the last two objectives of McGee’s general tips for visual rhetoric: aligning text in a readable manner, as well as the proximity of different visual rhetoric elements. The way that Obeidat and Yaqbeh aligned their figures, it helps prove the reliability of their research proposal, by representing the data through multiple iterations. I do notice something interesting after reading Teun De Rycker article: the author is taking advantage of correlation of their explanation and not the causation (De Rycker, 2001). The author uses a bar graph with a dataset containing 4500 packets, but the packets containing threats variable compared to the detected threats, only ranges from roughly 200 to 400. This leaves a visual representation that their detection rate is equal to the infected packet rate.

 

Refrences:

De Rycker, T. (2001). Analyzing tables, graphs, and charts: A four-step approach. Business Communication Quarterly, 64(4), 72–80. https://doi.org/10.1177/108056990106400408
