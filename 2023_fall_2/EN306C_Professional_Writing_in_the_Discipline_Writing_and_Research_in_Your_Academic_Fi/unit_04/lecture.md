What are primary and secondary sources and how are they used in different fields? 

Many people only learn the difference between a primary and secondary source in the context of historical study, which leads many students to be confused when they are asked to analyze primary sources or use secondary sources in other fields of study. 

You may have heard a teacher say that primary sources are those that are original sources from the time period, and they might have cited diaries or letters from the 180s as examples of primary sources.  While these are in fact primary sources for the field of history, this definition does not sufficiently explain what primary sources are and how they are selected or analyzed in other fields.

For our purposes, we are going to define primary sources as those the scholar is centrally analyzing in a study, and secondary sources are those written by other scholars or thinkers that are used by the author to analyze their primary materials. 

For example, a historian who wants to analyze the ways women’s groups in the 1920’s impacted local education reform would probably head to local archives to locate the manuscripts and records left by particular women’s groups.  They might find letters, scrapbooks, photographs, and clippings of news articles, and these would become the primary materials they analyze for their study.  These would be her primary sources for the study.  Secondary sources might be books written by other scholars on the history of women’s clubs, articles about specific women’s organizations, such as a women’s Shakespeare society, research on educational reform, etc.  These kinds of sources, which the writer would use to help her analyze the material she found in the archives, are her secondary sources. 

Let’s look at an example that is a bit further away from history studies.  Let’s image a scholar who wants to study how high school students at a local high school revise their writing.  To study this, she is going to collect copies of student papers before and after revision, and she is planning to survey students about their revision process.  She is also planning to get books from the library on the writing process written by scholars in writing studies, articles that have done research on student revision, curricular materials that claim to have effective methods for teaching revision practices to students, as well as some theoretical articles that examine the implications of different definitions of revision. 

Her primary sources – those she is centrally analyzing – are the papers and surveys she gathered from the local high school students.  Her secondary sources are the works written by scholars and teachers who have studied or taught writing and revision skills; she will use these sources to help her analyze the local high school students’ papers. 

One more example: the rhetorical analysis you are currently working on also has a primary source – it is the article you are analyzing.  If you bring in a claim from one of the articles we have read so far, you are then using a secondary source to help you analyze a primary source. 

The important thing to notice here is that what makes a primary or secondary source is not what the source is, it is what you are doing with that source intellectually that makes it a primary or secondary sources.  An academic article could be used as a secondary source in one context, but as a primary source in another. For example, if we were to use Coyne et al.’s article to help us analyze American Idol, we would be using it as a secondary source (our primary source would be episodes of the TV show).  However, if we analyze the rhetorical strategies in Coyne et al.’s article, it is now being used as a primary source. 

This distinction is very important to understand in order to properly propose your final project in your unit 4 assignment.  Your final assignment for this course will also have a set of primary sources and secondary sources.  In your proposal, you will need to say in your proposal what primary sources you will be analyzing in your final project and for what specific features, and you will then need to annotate at least 3 secondary sources that you think might be useful in analyzing your primary sources. 

Example project: 

Here is an example of a project a student might do that would fit our assignment: Kelly is in the field of psychology, and she has become interested in how case studies function rhetorically in her field.  She wonders how studies that have such limited number of participants build their credibility (how can a case study that focuses on a single person or a handful of people be credible compared to studies with 100s of participants, for example?).  Kelly proposes to collect a set of primary materials – a set of case studies that were published in the top 3 psychology journals in the last 3 years – she has located 10 such case studies.  She proposes to analyze those 10 articles to catalogue the ways the writers of case studies construct the credibility or explanatory power of studies with limited participants.  Those case studies are her primary sources.

Now Kelly has to figure out what secondary sources she needs to find that will help her analyze the case studies she has selected.  She already knows that Golafshani’s article that we read in unit 2 offers some ideas she can use to analyze her chosen primary sources: the ideas about qualitative methods and values of trustworthiness, generalizability, triangulation, and use of multiple methods are all things she could look for as she analyzes her chosen case studies.  Golafshani’s article then is one of several secondary sources she will annotate (summarize and evaluate) in her annotated bibliography. 

However, Kelly would like a perspective on case studies specifically and/or writing in psychology in particular to give her other things to consider or look for.  After doing some library research, she comes up with a few more sources all of which have something different to offer Kelly in how they would help her analyze her primary sources. 

She chooses this article:  Flyvbjerg, Bent. "Five Misunderstandings About Case-Study Research" Qualitative Inquiry, vol. 12, no. 2, 2006, pp. 219-245.

She likes that the article talks about why case studies have scientific validity and explanatory power even though they focus on smaller numbers of subjects.  She thinks this will help her better understand why case studies even exist in the first place. 

She also chooses this article:

Lee, Eunjung & Mishna, Faye & Brennenstuhl, Sarah. “How to Critically Evaluate Case Studies in Social Work.”  Research in Social Work Practice, vol. 19, issue 10, 2009.

This article is important because it shows her a method for evaluating whether a case study is credible or not based on several different variables.  Although the article is about the field of social work, Kelly can see that the concepts seem to also apply well to case studies in psychology. 

Finally, she also grabs this book:

Alexander L. George, Andrew Bennett, Sean M. Lynn-Jones, Steven E. Miller.  Case Studies and Theory Development in the Social Sciences, MIT Press, 2005. 

Kelly chooses this book because it catalogues the ongoing debate among psychologists about case studies and how they fit into or complement other kinds of studies in psychology. One of the chapter directly addresses how researchers can make case studies more credible or explanatory.  She also notices that the bibliography in this book has a bunch of other relevant sources she wants to track down and read after the proposal and bibliography is submitted (because the proposal and annotated bibliography is just the beginning of her research process and she wants to write the best possible project, Kelly will continue looking at sources even after turning in the unit 4 assignment).

How to evaluate sources

Once you have located some sources, you need to evaluate them.  Many students have been taught to evaluate sources on a yes/no, biased/unbiased scale.  However, sources are way more complicated than that, and there is realistically no such thing as wholly unbiased source.  All sources, at the very least, have a point of view that represents the author’s shaping and selection of information that is designed to accomplish their objective.  However, that does not mean that all sources are equally valid either, nor does it mean that all sources can be used in the same ways or for the same purposes.  Some sources might be perfectly useable for some purposes, but absolutely not useful or credible for others, so you need to think carefully about how you might use a source as you consider whether or not it can credibly serve your purposes.  Evaluating sources means that you need to think critically about what a source has to offer, whether it is useful for a specific purpose, and whether it is credible for the specific purpose you would like to use it for.

Scholarly vs. Non-Scholarly Sources

Your assignment this week requires that you find at least 1 scholarly, peer-reviewed source to annotate in your proposal/bibliography.  You might be wondering: How can you tell if a source is scholarly and peer-reviewed?

What is a scholarly source?

Scholarly sources are those that are written by and for scholars.  Basically, it is the writing of experts who study topics in particular disciplines and produce writing for other experts in that discipline. 

Why do we use scholarly sources?

Scholarly sources represent sustained and serous study about particular topics; the claims made by scholars are generally well-evidenced, and academic writing tends to be very detailed and complex which makes it very useful for gathering lots of reputable information.

Scholarly sources also carry a lot of credibility because the bar to become a scholar and to publish work is set very high.  One of those bars scholars have to jump is the peer-review process. Most reputable academic presses and journals have a peer review policy that dictates that any work submitted will be sent out to other experts in the field for review before it can be published.  This ensures a level of quality control that is absent in popular sources.  Some peer-review is even conducted blind, meaning that the authors and/or reviewers’ names are not revealed, which is supposed to remove a measure of bias.  While peer-review is not perfect, it is a better control mechanism than is present for other kinds of sources. 

How can I tell if a source is scholarly or peer-reviewed?

This guide can help you determine if a source is scholarly.  If you can't decide from these hints, ask a librarian or your instructor for help. You can also verify whether a journal is really peer-reviewed by finding the journal’s website which will usually describe their peer-review policy in the about page or in instructions to authors.  Some databases also allow you to limit your results to peer-reviewed material; however, beware relying on this too heavily without checking yourself as the results rely on the indexer knowing whether the publications are actually peer-reviewed, and sometimes, they do get it wrong.

Does this mean that only scholarly sources are credible or useful?  

No, it just means that they are often more detailed and somewhat more reliable in terms of their claims, evidence, and expertise; scholarly sources can be used to back certain kinds of claims you could not support with a less detailed or more popular source.

However, sources published in other types of venues can still be useful in your research.  For example, a textbook that aims to teach students how to write in the social sciences is not aimed at other experts in the discipline, and textbooks usually are not peer-reviewed; however, a textbook may still offer some useful ideas that you could use to analyze writing in one of the social science fields.  It will likely be written at a lower level than peer-reviewed research and it may not have as many nuanced or detailed claims as peer-reviewed research, however. 

Bibliographic Citation

Now that you have located and evaluated your sources, you need to cite them using a bibliographic style that is appropriate to your field.  You will want to cite each secondary sources using the end-text reference style (in other words, the style that is used on the references or works cited page). 

Use the citation guides in the  [link: https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/research_and_citation/resources.html] Purdue OWL to look up and formulate the proper citation for each of your secondary sources.  Note: you may be tempted to use a citation generator, but be warned: they are only correct about 50% of the time.  You do need to be able to use a citation handbook to properly prepare citations to be an effective writer in your discipline.
