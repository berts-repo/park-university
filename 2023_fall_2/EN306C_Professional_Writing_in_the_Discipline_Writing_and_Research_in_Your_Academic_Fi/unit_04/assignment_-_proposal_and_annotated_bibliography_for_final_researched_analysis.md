Introduction

To begin developing your final researched analysis project, you are going to write a 300-500 word proposal (about 1-2 pages) and an annotated bibliography containing at least 3 sources for the final project you’d like to do for this class.

Directions

In the academic world, proposals serve a very important purpose.  Scholars write proposals to get papers accepted to conferences, to get articles accepted into publications, or to get a book contract signed with a publishing company.  Proposals are also useful in getting grant funding, or convincing an institution to grant release time or a sabbatical leave.  In all of these cases, proposal writers have to carefully consider the audience to whom they are writing the proposal and make specific rhetorical choices in order to demonstrate that they are prepared to do the project being proposed, that they have a specifically defined project, and that the project is significant, relevant, useful, or timely in some way. 

Proposal

To begin developing your final researched analysis project, you are going to write a 300-500 word proposal (about 1-2 pages) for the final project you’d like to do for this class.  As the writer, it is your job to convince your instructor to approve your project.  In your proposal, you should explain what discipline you are working in, what angle(s) of the disciplinary discourse you will investigate and what primary materials you are considering looking at.  Remember, primary materials are those you are analyzing, so in this case, you need to tell your instructor what your sample of disciplinary discourse will consist of. (For example, are you analyzing all the introductions in one volume of a journal using Leeson’s methodology? Are you picking an article from each of the top journals in your field and using Hylands’s methodology to measure their use of first person?).  You should also tell you instructor what research questions you are proposing asking, and/or what kinds of research resources you are interested in looking for. 

You should also discuss why you are interested in the topic/angle you have chosen.  Is it because this particular feature of writing in the field is fundamentally important to understanding how writing in the field works? Perplexing or confusing to you? Controversial in the field? 

Keep in mind that a proposal of this sort doesn’t necessarily ask you to know what you will argue about your topic; rather the purpose is to show that you have developed some interest in the project, that you have begun to narrow and define what you’ll be investigating, and that you have done some thinking and planning about your project. 

Annotated Bibliography

Along with the proposal, you will also turn in an annotated bibliography of 3 secondary sources that you think you’ll be able to use to help you develop your analysis of your disciplinary discourse.  (Remember, secondary sources are those that will help you analyze your primary sources - your sample of disciplinary discourse you identified in the proposal). There are a range of options here: 

Scholarly analyses of writing in specific fields (or even broader categories like the sciences, social sciences, and humanities) that will be helpful, analyses of disciplinary differences, or analyses of academic writing in general (like Thonney or Hyland).

Don’t forget to examine the reference pages for the articles we have read: you may find useful sources in other scholars’ bibliographies!

As well, in many fields, there are textbooks about writing in that field, which - although they are directed at students rather than other scholars - may still tell you something about what is important, valued, and necessary when writing for other disciplines.

Articles or books that target particular aspects of writing, for example, on the use of personal narrative in academic writing, or the challenge of arguing for the validity of a case study in psychology.

Additional Requirements

You may (but do not have to) use one article from our set of class readings for this assignment

You must also include at least one scholarly-peer-reviewed source that you located beyond our course readings.

In the annotated bibliography, list each source in the citation format appropriate to your field (if you still don’t know what that is, you should ask your interviewee).  Under each source, write 2 paragraphs (annotation):

In the first paragraph, summarize the source as specifically as possible. Make sure to give an overview of the source (what is the main point?) and identify particular claims that will be useful in developing your own analysis of the primary materials.

In the second paragraph, explain, as specifically as possible, why that source is useful/significant to your project, or what you think it will help you do in the paper. Keep in mind: You may use sources with which you don't necessarily agree.  For example, you could identify claims you want to question or work against, as well as those you may agree with.
