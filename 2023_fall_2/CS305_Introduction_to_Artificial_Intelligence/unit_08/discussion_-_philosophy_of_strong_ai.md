After taking the course I have taken a small step backwards in how close I think artificial intelligence is to human-like consciousness. According to a journal article in the Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, “strong AI must be able to demonstrate sentience, emotional intelligence, imagination, effective command of other machines or robots, and self-referring and self-reflecting qualities” (Ng & Leung, 2020).

What I see to be the issue and what the book explains is the trade-off between thinking and acting. Agents have a hard time applying uncertainty and rationality. In theory I can see a weighted system, maybe like how language models systems weight words to complete a sentence, a strong AI could have an agent designated to weighing moral computations. However, the complexity of moral decision-making makes this more of a challenging task. The issue arises in formulating agents to work continually with other agents. When dealing with multiple agents they can be cooperative or competitive, and they can sometimes act unpredictably or in a way that does not contribute to the overall goals.

The challenge also lies in making AI agents interact efficiently, but also in ensuring that their actions align with ethical standards and societal norms. The data that is being used to determine if something is rational or moral would depend on training sets, and the datasets could be curated, and are curated. Agents and systems would not only have to make these emotional intelligent decisions from the offline data, but for them to be considered strong AI they would need to be time-sensitive, weighing when and when to not to base a decision from the data, but would also need to be sensitive to real-time data.
