Late Assignment Policy

Assignments are expected to be completed on time.  Late assignments will be accepted at the discretion of the instructor. 

The following is a list of valid reasons for submitting late work:

A medical emergency or a serious acute illness. All medical emergencies and illnesses must be verified by a note on letterhead by an M.D., D.O., P.A., or R.N. The course instructor will not normally accept a note from other health professionals (e.g., Ph.D., MSW, D.C., Physical Therapist) because their professional functions rarely involve medical emergencies or acute illnesses. The course instructor will accept late work for students who can provide evidence of a verified medical emergency (but not acute illness) involving a child, spouse, parent, sibling, or grandparent.

An Accident or Police Emergency. An accident report or note on letterhead from an appropriate law enforcement officer is required to accept late work due to accidents or police emergencies.

Unforeseen Jury or Witness Duty. A note on letterhead from a judge or attorney is required to accept late work due to jury or witness duty.

Unforeseen Military Deployment or Activation. A note on official letterhead from your commanding officer is required.

Funerals for Immediate Family Member (e.g., parents, siblings, grandparents, aunts/uncles, first cousins). A copy of the obituary or a note from a minister or funeral director is required.

Quiz

Quizzes are for both you and the instructor to check whether you have a good grasp of the unit's readings. Use the feedback to close knowledge gaps and better learn the basics of the material. To ensure you get early and prompt feedback, quizzes are automatically graded, and set up to be due by the end of each Thursday (11:59 pm CT). 

Each quiz can be taken up to five times by its due date and the highest score will be recorded. 

No late submissions are allowed on quizzes.

Discussions

There are discussions in Units 1 through 8. Each of the postings should be about 1-2 paragraphs long.

Due Date(s)

Post your initial response by Thursday, 11:59 pm, CT.

Post your peer response(s) by Sunday, 11:59 pm, CT.

Units 1-7: Respond to 2 or more classmates by Sunday, 11:59 pm, CT.

Unit 8: Respond to 1 or more classmates by Sunday, 11:59 pm, CT.

Guidelines

Initial posting

Whenever possible, pick a question that has not been answered, or has an incorrect/incomplete answer. 

NOTE: You cannot just say the correct answer is xxx. You must also explain why your answer is correct or on the flip side why some other answers are wrong.  Your explanation also cannot be “this is how xx works” or “this is the result I get when I run this program on my computer”.  Rather it needs to be specific such as explain the result of each line of the code. When appropriate you should also cite corresponding concepts from the book chapter or online resources. Use correct spelling and grammar.

Here is an example question and a sample initial posting:

Q0. If you declare an array double list[] = {3.4, 2.0, 3.5, 5.5}, what's the highest index in this array?

Q0. The answer is 3. Here the length of the array is determined by the number of initial values in the braces.  There are 4 elements in the declaration thus the size of this array is 4. Since the index starts at 0, the highest index is 3 (4 minus 1).

Reference:

Array Initialization during Declaration, ch8, p532.

Follow-up posting

Each follow-up post should follow the same guidelines used for the initial post.

Do not just say “you’re right/wrong” or “agree/disagree”.  Support your feedback with reasonable explanation(s).  Your follow-up post should continue or expand the discussion, not just repeat the other student’s statement.  You may have a different answer, or a similar answer with different/clearer reasoning.

The answer/feedback in your posting doesn't have to be correct, but needs to be specific and logically coherent.

Programming Homework

Submission Requirements

Sometimes, you are required to paste a copy of your source code into your homework document. Set font to Courier New to have indentation shown clearly. 

If you choose to submit your work in one compressed file (you don’t have to), use only .zip.

Check the completeness of your homework and programming style against the rubric before turning it in.

Coding Style

Programming homework should follow proper C++ coding style, the preferred style of industry software.  Two C++ coding style documents are referenced for this course:

The CSIS department  [link: https://canvas.park.edu/courses/73627/files/9959108?verifier=1VAxlHn8GvA9NwV34bR7s4DwQ4XQavlolBNs22ed&wrap=1] “C++ coding conventions” document.

 [link: https://google.github.io/styleguide/cppguide.html] Google’s C++ style guide

Minimal C++ coding style requirements (CSIS department “C++ coding conventions” document)

#3. file prologue

#7. Proper and consistent placement of braces

#8. Proper and consistent indentation

#13. Naming conventions

(based on the idea of #17) Documentation for each function (except main): a brief statement explaining the purpose of the function and proper comments documenting each parameter and the returned value, if any.

Pre- and Post- condition comments

Proper comments in code body. See the Comments section (Variable comments and implementation comments) of  [link: https://google.github.io/styleguide/cppguide.html#Comments] Google’s C++ style guide.
