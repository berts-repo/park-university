Defining Terrorism

Last unit we talked about the difficulties in defining what we mean when we talk about media. Definitions of terrorism are also problematic.

There’s a famous saying often repeated, “one man’s terrorist is another man’s freedom fighter.” Defining who is the terrorist often depends on which side you are on; critics of that viewpoint often cite such thinking as evoking a moral relativist stance that just further muddies the waters. As still others have pointed out, the difference between a freedom fighter and a terrorist is the freedom fighter saves lives.

Again, if you are looking for a precise definition you may be frustrated.

All of which has made reporting on incidents of terrorism difficult for news organizations. To appear unbiased and objective, many international news organizations ban the word terrorist from their guidebooks.

Furthermore, it doesn’t help that U.S. government agencies – the state department, the FBI, the department of homeland security – do not have an agreed upon definition.

Neither does the U.N.

Nonetheless, for the purposes of our discussions in this course, there are factors which most scholars and experts do concede are common traits:

Terrorists operate in small units of groups usually fewer than ten

They use improvised devices

Their targets are attack state symbols, political opponents and the public

They are prepared to murder noncombatants to further their objectives

They see terrorism as a tactic to effect political change

Terrorism or the study of terrorism is not a discipline. Those who study its root causes and history come from a variety of academic disciplines including media scholars, many of whom are former newspaper and broadcast journalists.

It makes sense to address at this point in the course to ask whether anyone has figured out why someone would commit these horrific acts, in other words, the personal motivation or psychological make up or profile. Indeed, after a terrorist attack, one of the most troubling questions media commentators ask is “Why?”

Psychologists say there is no specific terrorist personality or profile. Rather, because terrorists tend to subordinate their individual identities to a terrorist collective identity that is fighting for political change, researchers contend that theories associated with group psychology and sociocultural context are more appropriate.
