# Unit 8: Lecture

Social Media and Terrorism

For the purposes of finalizing our discussion of media and terrorism, we end this course with a consideration of the dramatic change social media and cell phone/web technologies have wrought in not much more than a decade, a topic that surely could be a course in itself.

Take a look this  [link: http://www.pewinternet.org/2014/03/11/world-wide-web-timeline/] Pew Research Center web history time line. Facebook was founded in 2004. Twitter launched in 2006. Throughout the first decade of the 21st century cell phone devices became increasingly linked to web interactivity. The mass media of communications has undergone dramatic changes as well in part due to the global reach of the Internet. Most news organizations – including print newspapers -- have web pages, Facebook pages and Twitter followers.

What is the impact of social media and mobile phone/web technologies on terrorism and terrorism coverage? It’s a story with multiple dimensions and an array of colorful characters.  

First, images that stream in from social media users have changed how news coverage of terrorism events are reported by mainstream news media, as the following video illustrates using the Orlando Pulse nightclub attack as a case study:

 [link: https://www.c-span.org/video/?c4633686/terrorism-communication-new-media%C2%A0] Orlando Pulse Nightclub Attack

Another change brought on by social media: Anyone with a cell phone and link to the Internet can create and publish – with photos and/or video – "news" of a terrorist event. These “citizen journalists” bypass the legacy media gatekeepers such as editors who fact-check content before publication and ask questions about fairness and neutrality in coverage. Like their more professional counterparts, the work of citizen journalists is protected by the First Amendment. Also like the pros, the quality of their work varies. Their work may or may not add a valuable dimension to terrorism coverage. Unlike doctors or lawyers or even teachers, journalists are not licensed professionals.

In this video, Rachel Sterne, former CEO and founder of GroundReport.com, discusses the work of citizen journalists on the coverage of the Mumbai terrorists.

It is worth noting at the end of this course that the story of how social media is affecting terrorism and terrorism coverage is ongoing. In fact, when Brigette Nacos, the political scientist quoted often in your course textbook and in the online material for this course, updated her findings on the relationship between media and terrorism, social media or “digital media” was prominently added to the mix.

 

It is also worth noting that how we define terrorism also is not in stasis, and that Nacos devoted the last chapter of Mass Mediated Terrorism: Mainstream and Digital Media in Terrorism and Counterterrorism, published in 2016, to a discussion of the growing threat of right-wing extremists, which the Department of Homeland Security had been warning about since 2009. Another report released a few years later by the  [link: https://www.newamerica.org/] New America foundation found that post 9/11 nearly twice as many Americans were killed on American soil by white supremacists and antigovernment extremists than Jihadists. But the study didn’t get much coverage by the mainstream media until the killing of nine members of the Emmanuel African Methodist Episcopal Church in Charleston, South Carolina in 2015. Nacos wrote: “This is a perfect example of the mainstream media’s power to set not only the media agenda … this case demonstrates furthermore the ability of the media to frame the news simply by reporting more, less, or not at all on developments, events, or threats of violence.” In the media, the attack led to discussions about whether the murderer should be called a terrorist. Nacos wrote, “Only time will tell whether this was the beginning of finding common ground on the perennial definitional issues in the realm of non-state political violence.”
