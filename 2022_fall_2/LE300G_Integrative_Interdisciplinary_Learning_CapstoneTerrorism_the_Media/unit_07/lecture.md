# Unit 7: Lecture

Journalism and Patriotism

Speaking truth to power or being the bearer of bad news often makes journalists unpopular. But publishing stories on government wrong doing can appear particularly unpatriotic just after a terrorist attack when nationalistic sentiment is high.

Surveys conducted in the months after 9/11 indicated journalists were less likely to perform their adversarial role when interviewing officials about whether to retaliate. Audiences preferred a more patriotic press. Some news anchors even donned flag lapel pins at the risk of appearing less professional, less objective. Critics now say the watchdog press let the people down. It should have been more aggressive when interviewing government officials, including the president and retired generals allegedly representing their own views and not the Pentagon’s – (see this unit’s Discussion topic).

A common criticism is that most media outlets cover terrorism as an event and not as an ongoing issue required in-depth coverage.

In-depth reporting is within the purview of investigative journalism. Most reporting is piecemeal. It covers an event. What just happened. It tells us the facts. Who, what, when and where but rarely why. Investigative journalism is a form of journalism that lives up to the founders’ ideal vision of a press worthy of designation as the Fourth Estate in a democracy. But it is expensive, labor intensive work and not the norm. Most news staffs, especially these days – remember what you learned about the current state of the business side of journalism from watching Page One: Inside The New York Times – can’t afford to let reporters work on a single story for weeks and sometimes months.

A watchdog press in this country didn’t become viable until the late 1800s, early 19th century during the Progressive Era, when citizens were educated enough to read the newspapers and magazines that published stories on government and business wrong doing. These tabloid publications were affordable to the masses, and there was plenty to report on. Americans had moved from rural areas to burgeoning cities where businesses operated under lax or no regulation.

Publishing stories that said the president was a crook appeared to some Americans as disrespectful of the office of the President of the United States. It did not make The Washington Post popular even among its peers. Other major media did not look deeply into Watergate until the evidence became so incontrovertible that even the country’s most trusted newsman, Walter Chronkite, began covering the scandal.
