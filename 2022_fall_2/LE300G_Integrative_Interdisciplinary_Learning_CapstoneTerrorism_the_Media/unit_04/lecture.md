# Unit 4: Lecture

Power of Images and Moving Pictures

“Photographs have the kind of authority over imagination to-day, which the printed word had yesterday, and the spoken word before that. They seem utterly real. They come, we imagine, directly to us without human meddling, and they are the most effortless food for the mind conceivable. Any description in words, or even any inert picture exists in the mind. But on the screen the whole process of observing, describing, reporting, and then imagining, has been accomplished for you.” --- From “The World Outside and the Pictures in Our Heads” a chapter from Walter Lippman’s classic communications text, Public Opinion, published in 1922.

Lippman, a newspaper columnist who won the Pulitzer Prize – twice -- presciently theorized that media images become internalized -- as if they were part of firsthand lived experience. Think for a moment about the implications of that idea and how it may apply to viewers of televised images of terrorist acts. It implies that seeing images of terrorism is akin to living through the experience. Look again at the photograph above. It is unlikely that you were perched from a distance that day in New York with a view of the World Trade Center towers as they were hit by airplanes; yet, it is likely that that photograph – or an image like it -- is an image indelibly etched into your brain.

So far in this course we have talked mostly about the press as a group of professional writers who use words to convey meaning. But photojournalists also are members of the press, expected to adhere to the guidelines we discussed in Unit 1 regarding professional ethics and responsibilities presumed to be worthy of First Amendment protection.

Research suggests our brains have a built-in visual bias. We are moved more by images than words. For that reason, ethical journalists, when editing photos or moving images, struggle to find a balance between informing the public and exposing readers and viewers to disturbing images. They argue among themselves and with their editors over what is fair and appropriate. Usually, when the images under consideration are not part of breaking news story, there is time to mull these choices over.

For example, should these images be shown:

Body and severed head of U.S. contractor killed in Iraq?

Photos of Osama bin Laden after his death?

American soldiers dragged through the streets of Somalia?

Flag-draped caskets coming home from war?

However, a terrorist act is always breaking news. Streaming images flooding in require immediate editing decision making. Consider that on 9/11, while working “live,” with unfolding images and footage, most mainstream news organizations did not show images of the estimated more than 200 people who jumped to their deaths from the burning twin towers. Yet, images of the burning towers were everywhere in print and rebroadcast over and over on 24/7 cable news channels.

Other images that media scholars have said were used to tell the story of 9/11 include:

Slow motion footage of a plane hitting a tower and the tower beginning to burn and collapse

Looped images of a tower collapsing, followed by a shot of clouds of debris rolling into the streets as New Yorkers frantically ran away

Shots of rescue workers emerging from billowing clouds of dark dust

Three firefighters with an intact flag pole rising from the rubble of the trade center – an image that harkened back to the iconic Iwo Jima photo

Think about the concept of framing from the last unit. Now apply that theory to how a photograph or moving image is edited so that some features are shown, and emphasized, and others are not. Think about how such choices could alter meaning. For example, observe the following photograph that has been cropped and then the full image.

Scholars who analyzed images from 9/11 say the coverage revealed vital information about how journalists unwittingly become accomplices to terrorists’ goals to get media coverage of their political ideologies and spread fear among civilians. One, visual framing is different from verbal framing because meanings are more fluid, less precise. Two, the way film is edited can put images in juxtaposition in a way that alters meaning – think of quick cuts to jump from one scene to another or the effect of showing an image or scene in slow motion; all of which make the images more horrific, memorable and in unwitting service of the terrorist goal of spreading psychological fear among civilians. Regardless, journalists will continue to believe it is part of their job to tell truth, and that requires showing awful images of terrorism and its destruction.

Another aspect of the unintended consequences of repeatedly showing such imagery has been called the CNN effect. A theory that 24/7 cable news acts as an agenda-setting agent and accelerant to foreign policy decision making. The playing of a breaking news story – such as a terrorist attack – over and over again during a news cycle tends to put leaders on the spot to do something, pressured to act, with less time to reflect on what is best in the long term.
