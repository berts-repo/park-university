Last week I brought up the point that I think awareness of terrorist and their activities is important. I don’t like how the reporter, in Sony Hackers Make New Threats Ahead of 'Interview' Release, compared the bomb threat to Sep. 11th; that is where there can be an issue of instilling fear in the public and fueling terror, when the rhetoric set behind the event (bombing of movie theaters) is exaggerated to such an extreme example like Sep. 11th. I find Reynolds and Barnetts study of “professional confidence” to be a good example of why the journalist would feel confident enough to exaggerate terrorism. (pp. 101-102)

I do believe the the threats from The Guardians of Peace to be an act of terrorism in the political sense, as it implied "the illegitimate use of force to achieve a political objective by targeting innocent people."  (p 15) We find out that the hacker group was North Korean insurgents, but at the time of reporting there was only information that the hackers "didn't like" the rhetoric and framing from the movie.

Hackers are becoming more prevalent and abundant, and this is talked about in the end of the second video, and this is back in 2014 imagine now. The USA has paid over a billion dollars in Ransomware (retrieving stolen data) in 2021 alone. What we must be careful about is what we are classifying as “terrorism” when it comes to hackers. A 13-year-old could perform some of these cyber attacks on a zero-day threat, which is just the day a known vulnerability is leaked to the public and everyone rushes to exploit it, and it’s that easy. The point is that there are many different interests of hackers and their motives matter in determining if it is terrorism. An activist hacker may threaten to bomb a processing plant, but is that terrorism? Most the time that is an well intended innocent threat.
