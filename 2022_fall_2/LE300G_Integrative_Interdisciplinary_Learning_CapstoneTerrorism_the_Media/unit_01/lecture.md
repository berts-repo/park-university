What Do We Mean When We Talk About ‘The Media’?

Media. It’s all around us.

On our way to work, billboard ads beckon us to buy whatever they are selling. A favorite song on the car radio is interrupted by a news bulletin. In the not so distant past, the “media” meant print newspapers and the newsmen – they were all men back then – whose trustworthy and straightforward demeanor delivered the TV newscast at precisely 6 o’clock.

Media. It’s part of our everyday lives.

These days, “media” is often what we spend more and more of our waking hours looking at – on large flat TV screens or handheld smartphones.

Since the dawn of the 21st century, digital media has begun replacing traditional media. These days, anyone with a cell phone can become a published writer by clicking send or uploading a tweet. No wonder the lines between legitimate media we can trust and “infotainment” have blurred.

So, what exactly are we talking about when we talk about media?

In this course, when we talk about the relationship between media and terrorism we will be referring to what is known as “the press,” professional journalists who cover the news for a living and adhere to the ethical code laid out by the Society of Professional Journalists. That is not to say that a lone blogger is disreputable; that blogger might be considered a pro if he or she adheres to the Society of Professional Journalists Code of Ethics. “Journalism is the best attainable version of the truth.” – Carl Bernstein, pictured on the right, with Bob Woodward when both were reporters for The Washington Post working on the Watergate break-in story that would help bring about the resignation of U.S. President Richard Nixon in 1974.

As Bernstein’s quote suggests, reputable journalists strive to uncover facts, and since we are talking about interviewing humans, the “truth” is often elusive – not exactly akin to conducting scientific research. Journalists are a breed of social science researchers. Given the limits of certainly, they seek the “best attainable version of the truth.” Going about their work, journalists are lot like detectives. The Watergate investigation involved interviewing sources, corroborating statements and tracking down hard-to find documents to uncover wrong doing.

The best journalists differentiate in their reporting between fact and inference, opinion and assumption and label each accordingly in their presentations. Newspapers, for years, indicated the shift from fact to opinion by labeling that all views on the “Editorial” page were labeled opinion. (More later on how that has changed since the advent of cable news and digital media.)

Obviously, not all journalists are so thoughtful or ethical. Just as there are good and bad cops and good and bad teachers, there are good and bad journalists. However, at this point in this course, we are talking about what the press is supposed to do under the U.S. Constitution.

You may remember for high school civics about freedom of the press and the First Amendment. The founders’ vision was of the press as a fourth pillar of democracy.

The idea was that representative democracy required an informed citizenry. Not everyone could travel to Washington, D.C. to sit and listen to politicians debate an issue. In the place of those busy citizens, off working or tending to their farms back then, would be the press, which would not only inform the public on issues of the day but also hold the government accountable to the voters. For the founders, freedom of the press was such an important factor in the balance of powers that it was included in the First Amendment to the Constitution.
