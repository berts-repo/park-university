I first want to note how well framed Orson Welles, The War of the Worlds, was compared to a new organization. The book defines framing as a centralized way to organize ideas for news content that gets a points across with methods including: emphasis, exclusion, and elaboration. (Barnett & Reynolds, 2009, p. 47) A good example of this framing is in the first 20 minutes; the broadcast talks about evidence of "aliens" and discovering explosions on the surface of mars. The broadcast then cuts out to a field reporter who talks about a UFO crash site where they saw the aliens, which is much less un-believable story, but after that very unbelievable story it goes back to the Mars explosions, and he leaves the listener with a logical evidence about the explosions on mars as "volcanic activity." This framing over false-evidence, then telling much more unbelievable story that provides the correct rhetoric, suddenly makes the first explanation seem perfectly logical even with a believable explanation like volcanic activity, it leaves the listener not believing the logical evidence.

Although Welles did cause terror with his broadcast debut, he was not a terrorist. I can't say I know much about Orson Welles or his political agendas that could be affected my opinion of the matter, but from the information provided the sorry does not fit the categories of terrorism, it does not use violence, it doe not have a political agenda (that I am aware of), and it does not have the intention of sowing fear into the public. (Barnett & Reynolds, 2009, p. 15) The intention of sowing fear into the public is where Orwell most likely faced his troubles. At the time of the War of the Worlds debut the "The Hypodermic Needle Theory" was recently developed and had the peoples attention, as it was brainwashing the public. People believed that the media and news had direct and linear influence over the public, they thought the that people were just sponges that would absorb whatever they were shown.

I believe the Hypodermic Needle Theory is valid to an extent. I do believe in narratives, agendas, and framing having an affect on how the news is perceived and absorbed, but we are also very influenced by the people we surround ourselves with, and the narrative we have in our own head about ourselves and the world (e.g. religion). There is a reason that algorithms are designing information to be targeted to the specific individual and profiled groups.
