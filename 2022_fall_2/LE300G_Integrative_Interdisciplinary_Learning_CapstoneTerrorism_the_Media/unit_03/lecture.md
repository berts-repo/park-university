# Unit 3: Lecture

The News Media and Terrorism

By now, after watching Page One: Inside the New York Times and reading the required chapters in your course textbook, you should be developing your own ideas about why the relationship between terrorism and media has been termed paradoxically symbiotic. To appreciate the complexity of this issue even more, let’s consider how professional journalists decide what IS news.

News is defined by a number of factors related to a media outlet’s audience. An editor or reporter will ask several questions to determine the newsworthiness of a topic such as:

Impact or who will be affected – will my readers/viewers be affected?

Timeliness or when it happened

Prominence of the person or persons or places or event

Proximity? Where did it happen – is it near our readers/viewers?

Human interest – is the story compelling on an emotional level?

Is there controversy or conflict

Is it unusual or bizarre?

News can also be defined as what is not normal, not routine.

An airplane flying into the World Trade Center is undeniably news. It fits every element in the criteria for determining newsworthiness, and terrorists know that.

Terrorists choreograph their actions to garner the most media attention to spread fear far beyond the physical plane and into the psychological terrain, the minds of the media’s readers and viewers.

The authors of the course textbook are media scholars who include in their overview on the relationship between terrorism and media relevant studies from researchers in a variety of fields including psychology, criminal justice and political science. They point out that until the 1960s – and then particularly after the 9/11 attack -- most scholars who study terrorism, its history, definitions and causes, did not factor in media as an influence. Bridgette Nacos, a leading political scientist, has explored the significance of the media in understanding how modern terrorism is practiced. She created the term “mass mediated terrorism,” meaning that terrorists gain entrance to the public discussion between government officials and the public through media. The argument here is not that the news media in general or journalists in particular sympathize with the perpetrators of political violence, but rather that they are unwitting accomplices of media-savvy terrorists. This is not a new development. However, as the move from news as information to news as entertainment continues, especially in television, media organizations seem increasingly inclined to exploit terrorism as infotainment for their own imperatives (i.e. ratings and circulation). More than ever before, terrorists and the media are in a quasi-symbiotic relationship.

No doubt, the need for higher ratings or circulation is always a factor, which you might appreciate more than ever now that you have watched the Page One documentary on quality journalism’s struggle to stay in business in the digital media era.

Remember the Hypodermic Theory from the Unit 1 Discussion? The idea that media could be used to inject fear into the populace. Lack of direct correlation sunk that old theory. Nevertheless, throughout the 21st century as media began to mean not just radio but television and then the internet, media scholars continued to research ways to quantify media influence.

On a most basic level, media theory suggest that media gets in our heads quite literally. Media scholars have found that although the media do not tell us what to think – such as who to vote for – it does tell us what is important to think about – an upcoming election, for example – which became known as the Agenda Setting Function Theory.

Terrorists know that their acts fit the definition of what is news. The media become “unwitting accomplice” to terrorists by spreading fear among the populace when they focus on the act 24/7 on cable news (Agenda setting) and limit interviews to newsmakers who stir negative emotions (framing).

One approach to the study of the relationship between terrorism and the news media is to look at how journalists go about doing their jobs when there is not a crisis or breaking news story such as a terrorist act. David Weaver and G. Cleveland Wilhoit, media scholars who defined three basic journalistic roles: 

Interpretive – offering an analysis of complex problems such as national policy and claims

Dissemination – an emphasis on getting information to the public quickly, the facts

Adversary – maintaining an adversarial stance in relationship to government officials, the watchdog after the “best available version of the truth” on behalf of the people

 These roles overlap. Are not mutually exclusive. For example, a broadcast journalist on a 24/7 cable TV show will open a segment by reporting the latest on an ongoing story (disseminate the facts), then pivot to interview and question a government official about the issue (adversary) and may conclude the broadcast by offering some parting words, a perspective that analyzes both sides on a controversial issue (interpretive).
