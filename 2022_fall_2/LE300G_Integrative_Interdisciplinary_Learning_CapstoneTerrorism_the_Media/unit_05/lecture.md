# Unit 5: Lecture

The Whole World Is Watching

This unit we focus on television, the medium Americans most often turn to when there’s a terrorist attack. As political scientist Brigette Nacos has written:

 “No other medium has provided more oxygen to terrorism than television because of its ability to report the news instantly, nonstop, and in visuals and in words from any place to all parts of the globe.”

Because of those features, television has been termed the gold standard for terrorists.

Critics say television news coverage of terrorist attacks too often borders on sensationalism. That wasn’t always so evident as you may see when you watch the following two clips. The murder of Israeli athletes in 1972 at the Munich Olympics is considered the first terrorist event to garner live TV coverage. Did you notice the relatively sedate tone and lack of graphic visuals in those clips from 1972, the era before the advent of Cable TV news. No split screens or juxtaposing of eye-catching footage with MTV style quick-jump cuts. Also, not evident: the number of “talking heads,” journalists acting more as commentators, speculating – as live news footage continued streaming in – about what was happening and why. Remember from earlier in this course we talked about the role of journalism in a democracy and the importance of objectivity and fact-finding in quality journalism?

Critics say standards have slipped since the advent of cable TV in the 1980s at least in part due to the sheer amount of air time to fill with content 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. In general, during a 24/7 cable news broadcasts, reporting facts takes the least amount of time. The rest of the broadcast typically includes interviews with government officials and other interested parties or stakeholders, who in the course of the interview segments will opine on the evidence at that time; after which -- again this is a generalization – a few “big names” in journalism and public affairs will hold a round table discussion speculating on what the known facts might mean or what government officials might do to alleviate the problem. In the best instances, these conversations offer an in-depth analysis from experts on a given topic. Yet, at any time, the “Breaking News” banner might explode across the screen, prompting the anchor to break for the latest update. And the news cycle begins again, repeating over and over, 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

 In the old days of newspaper journalism, readers could more easily tell the difference between opinion and news. All articles printed in a newspaper were news unless otherwise indicated by the words “Editorial" or "Opinion.” Open a new window on your computer and Google “sample editorial pages” and see what comes up. No doubt you will see articles labeled – as is the case with the page shown on the left -- so that readers know that what they are reading is opinion not news, not straight reporting of fact.

Similar labeling is rarely seen during TV news broadcasts when the anchor pivots from giving a fact-based update to offering commentary. As such, a frequent criticism of cable TV news is that commentary –from anchors as well as guests – make up most of the broadcast, factors that feed the impression that journalists are unwitting complicit in helping terrorists reach their goals.

Consider the CNN effect, which we in last unit’s lecture and you read about in your course textbook. The scenario typically unfolds this way. After a terrorist attack, reporters will question government officials about what action the U.S. should take. If he or she says anything that might be construed as consequential or likely, that comment becomes news and is broadcast globally. Other networks broadcast what the official said, and still other officials discuss the ramifications of that comment. An unintended consequence of this rush to get comments and fill airtime is that foreign policy officials feel pressured to react. Former Secretary of State James Baker in 1996  told the author of a Harvard University study on the CNN effect:

“All too often, television is what determines what is a crisis. Television concluded the break-up of the former Yugoslavia and the fighting in the Balkans was a crisis, and they began to cover it and cover it. And so the Clinton administration (was left) to find a way to do something. (Yet) they didn’t do that in Rwanda where the excesses were every bit as bad, if not worse. And so, you have to ask yourself, does that mean you should do foreign policy by television? Are we going to define crises according to what is covered, by what the editors decide to cover? I don’t think we should do that.”

Becoming more literate consumers of news is one way viewers can discriminate between news and opinion. Howard Schneider, a journalist for more than 35 years at Newsday before becoming dean of Stoney Brook State University’s journalism school and director of its center of News Literacy, suggests watching for the following markers to spot commentary or opinion on cable TV news broadcasts:

First person voice

One-sided argument

Personal attacks

Straw-man arguments

Exaggeration or superlatives

Overly dramatic
