## READ BEFORE SUBMITTING YOUR FIRST DISCUSSION POST
*Posted: 2022-10-11T22:58:33Z*

Ground rules for Discussions

 

Why this Announcement now?

We are living through politically divisive times. A coarse tone in dialogue over issues is evident in our media and especially in social media posts – particularly among strangers on opposite sides of complex issues who figure they will never see each other face to face. The tone is akin to drivers on the freeway speeding past each other, raging about perceived slights. I have noticed that tone creeping into discussions in my course and I have written this Announcement in an effort to preempt it from happening again this semester.

 

Discussions are not like Facebook posts

Unlike conversations like those, you might have between friends and acquaintances on social media such as Facebook, saying “I liked your post” will not suffice in a discussion in a college course. Discussions in a college course are meant to foster intellectual inquiry and an exchange of substantial ideas. One way to further the discussion is to arm yourself with facts such as what experts in the course textbook have had to say about that week’s Discussion topic.

 

Why do I care?

Those of us who teach online courses want you to get a first-rate education in which you grow intellectually through your college experiences. Intellectual discussion is essential to developing the ability to think systematically about complex issues, a tradition that goes back to ancient times, well before the establishment of colleges in the West. Using your mind this way is a lot like working out physically. It may sometimes feel awkward, but as you practice you get stronger. In the short term, consciously trying to respond intellectually will prepare you mentally to propose and write a fact-based, not opinion or feeling-based research paper in this course. In the long term, I hope that you will be better prepared to do analytical work in your career as well as handle discourse related to living well and prospering in all facets of your life.

 

Tip 1: Stay on topic

Narrowing the scope of a topic allows for depth. As indicated in Lecture 1 Module 1 the topic in this course is legitimate news media, not Facebook posts or TMZ broadcasts or something you heard or saw somewhere that you don’t remember or can’t find now. If you post something to the effect of “All media are in it for the money and promote X.” I will ask you to give me a source or provide an example of the incident.

In this course, we are looking at the relationship between mainstream professional media operations. Yes. They may have Facebook pages and tweet links to their headlines. But what they publish and why they publish it is different than the vast majority of what’s posted online, which is akin to a wild west, due to the lack of regulations that apply to more traditional media.

We can’t intelligently talk or productively analyze all the different types of media messaging out there these days in one course – that’s one reason why we narrow the scope by looking at what is considered news.

The best news media strive for objective reporting of facts. Try as they might, reporters are humans and humans are biased. Bias has in recent years become a way for news organizations to “brand” their product. Refer to the chart below and attached for your convenience, throughout the semester to “read between the lines” and decipher bias in reporting.

 

Tip 2: Try not to always react based on your personal experience

The personal anecdote is where you start not where you finish. To be educated means to learn what you don’t know and be open to using your intellect and not your emotions. That means refraining from always posting using phrases such as “I feel.” Try using “I think” and follow that phrase with why you think the way you do armed with facts, not opinions.

Do not be afraid of appearing uninformed or being made fun of – see the next tip.

 

Tip 3: Tone is crucial in online communications

First, read the rubric. Closely. Students who explain their reasoning methods – including their lack of understanding and interest in learning more – garner more credit for intellectual honesty and openness to new ideas

Maintain a respectful tone. Part of getting a college education is learning to fight with words and ideas, not your fists and feelings. Challenge but don’t confront. Perhaps it’s a sign of the times but just because we are online does not mean we should act less cordial than if we are face-to face in a classroom.
