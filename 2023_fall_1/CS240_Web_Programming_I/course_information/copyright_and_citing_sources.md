# Copyright and Citing Sources

Can I use any text or images in my web programming homework or discussion activities? 

No. You cannot use any photo or graphic that you do not have the legal rights to use or is not in the public domain. Being enrolled in a course doesn't mean you can copy or use anything you want. You are not required to create any graphics for your site or in this course. However, graphics can enhance the appearance of your web site. You may use third party graphics provided you have the legal rights to use them and you cite the resource in your web page code, and they are in the public domain! If you would like to create your own graphic artwork you may do so. However, you will need to cite yourself as the resource owner in your web page code.

Do I Need to Cite References to Images, Content or Code?

Yes for all of them!

Make sure that include a statement in the web site that the web site is for school purposes and the copyright of the images remains with the rightful owner. You can do this in the footer of the page or in the comments, with the image. In other words, don't try to pass the images off as yours. When you use a photo or graphic, you must include the citation reference for the image in the comments with each of the images, or have a web page that cites all the sources.

Please make sure you cite your name in all of your code files! Please know, this is only for code that has been pre-approved from the instructor, such as Bootstrap, JQuery/JQueryUI or other files. You may not copy other code from other web sites and use them in your homework programming assignments. Any snippets of code you share in the discussions, must be cited as well. 

Do not just copy from a web site in the discussion and cite the reference. Follow the recommendations in the APA Style Guide for referencing works online. You need to write your own discussion posting and respond to the discussion questions with your own answers. You can integrate the content you learn from another site, but you may not simply copy and paste the code. 

Acknowledging the source of the copyrighted material is not a legal substitute for obtaining permission. However, in academia, we identify the source, to give credit to the original author or creator. So you should know about Copyright Information. Please review the information below on Copyright. 

Copyright Information

Many companies are well known for protecting their copyright status. Companies such as Disney, and many sports teams do not want their images and content used on other web sites. Copyright and patents, balance the needs of society with the needs of business. So there are instances where you may use copyrighted works, without permission. For example, faculty may use some copyrighted works in class online, when the courses are locked with a login, and when the content used is minimal.  As a web developer, you need to understand basic copyright laws. It is illegal to violate federal copyright laws in the US. In other courses you might have talked about the Copyright Act and Fair Use. In school exercises, there are exceptions for the use of copyright materials. However, you MUST follow all copyright laws.

What is Fair Use?

The 4 requirements must be met to be able to use copyright items under Fair Use. 

Section 107 lists what reasons are considered fair or not fair to use under this clause. Fair uses explicitly included are: criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, and research. 

Note that in determining fair or not fair use, all four factors are considered together. Failing to meet one of the four standards would deem the use unfair. 

17 U.S. Code § 107 - Limitations on exclusive rights: Fair use

The purpose and character of the use, including whether such use is of commercial nature or is for nonprofit educational purposes

The nature of the copyrighted work

The amount and substantiality of the portion used in relation to the copyrighted work as a whole

The effect of the use upon the potential market for, or value of, the copyrighted work

Reference: Copyright.Gov. (n.d.). 17 U.S. Code § 107 - Limitations on exclusive rights: Fair use. 
Retrieved from  [link: https://www.copyright.gov/title17/92chap1.html#107] https://www.copyright.gov/title17/92chap1.html#107

Guidelines have been recommended by the Copyright Office and publishing lobby, to help determine if the activity is fair use or not fair use as evaluating these four standards consistently is difficult because there are no specific number of words, lines, graphics, or musical notes that may be taken under this regulation without permission.  

"How much of someone else's work can I use without getting permission? Under the fair use doctrine of the U.S. copyright statute, it is permissible to use limited portions of a work including quotes, for purposes such as commentary, criticism, news reporting, and scholarly reports. There are no legal rules permitting the use of a specific number of words, a certain number of musical notes, or percentage of a work. Whether a particular use qualifies as fair use depends on all the circumstances. See FL 102, Fair Use, and Circular 21, Reproductions of Copyrighted Works by Educators and Librarians."

In general:

If the users name is not available, leave the brackets off the screen name. 

Provide a URL that links directly to the content, not the home page of the website or book.

If an Internet document is more than one web page, provide a URL that links to the home page or entry page for the resource. 

If a date is not available use (n.d.) for no date.

In most cases, when you cite the reference in-text simply: (Gates, 2013).
