Unit 1 HOME PAGE

There is a great dichotomy in the Mathematics discipline; pure mathematics and applied mathematics. Pure mathematics is a general study of mathematics, with no discussion of how the concepts are to be used outside of the realm of the Mathematics discipline. Mathematicians and most mathematics instructors/students are interested groups in this type of mathematics.

Applied mathematics is a study of adapting the general mathematics concepts to enhance and generate other disciplines outside the realm of mathematics. The field of Mathematics is independent of other disciplines, yet is a fundamental component of nearly every other discipline, especially all the sciences. It is this reason that Mathematics is referred to as "the Queen of all Sciences."

Although Statistics is a part of the Mathematics discipline it also falls into the category of applied mathematics. It uses concepts from such mathematical topics as algebra, probabilities, and calculus. Statistics students do not need to know anything about those topics to understand statistical concepts and to perform the necessary calculations required for this basic course.

The best, and perhaps the only way, to become proficient at the skills needed to meet the objectives of this course is to learn the methods and then practice until you can repeat them without the aid of another person, your text, or even your notes. Mathematics is a comprehensive discipline. What you learn in Chapter One will be built upon in Chapter Two. Both chapter concepts will be used in Chapter Three, and so on. Recall how you learned to add columns of numbers years ago. You probably have continued to use those skills on a near daily basis since then, and we will continue to use those skills and other math you previously learned throughout this term. So plan on using paper, pen/pencil, and a scientific calculator (the one found on your computer will fit the bill) to practice the skills and then to complete the assignments.
