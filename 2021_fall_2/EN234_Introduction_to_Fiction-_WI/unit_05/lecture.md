# Unit 5: Lecture

Reading a Novel

In the last unit, we read a novella, which transitioned us from short stories to novels. When considering a novel, which is a much longer piece of fiction than anything we’ve yet read this semester, it is important to remember all the fictional elements we’ve discussed. Plot, characters, setting, theme, point of view, tone, style, and symbol are all features that can be found in a novel – what is perhaps both exciting and overwhelming is that these features might be much more complex. The character list might be more extensive, and the setting might change multiple times, and there may be a variety of different themes present.

Writing about a longer piece of fiction, such as a novella or novel, can seem equally overwhelming. There is just so much you analyze or consider. But if you focus your attention on a specific aspect, it will make developing your response a bit easier.

Organization/Structure of Literary Analysis Essay

This is probably review for most of you, because a literary analysis essay follows a similar organization to that of other academic essays. But before you complete your second short paper, we should talk about how to organize or structure an essay.

Title – Name your paper something that lets your reader know what to expect. Short Paper 2 is not descriptive, but “Selfish Families in Kafka’s Metamorphosis” is because it not only names the text that will be discussed but hints at the topic.

Introduction – Your introduction is where you introduce your topic and get your reader interested. You also want to forecast where your paper will go. You should start with an attention grabber or hook, something that will make your reader want to continue reading. You could use a surprising quote or statistic related to your topic or the story, you could tell a hypothetical story or personal anecdote related to your topic or the reading, or ask a question (just remember that you have to answer that question by the end – don’t leave your reader hanging!). After you have your reader’s attention, you might introduce the context or background of the topic or story before ending with your thesis statement. We’ve already talked about thesis statements, but remember that it should be specific, arguable, and unique. It represents your argument for how one can (and should) read the story. Your reader should know what you are trying to convince her of and the main points you will expand on throughout the rest of the paper.

Body Paragraphs – The main portion of your paper consists of the body paragraphs. Each paragraph should start with a topic sentence, which is a kind of mini thesis statement for the paragraph. It lets your reader know what the paragraph will be about, while also connecting back to your larger thesis statement. The topic sentence might also begin with a transitional word or phrase, such as “First,” “The next point,” “In addition,” “Moreover,” “On the other hand,” or “Finally.” The rest of your paragraph will use specific examples and details from your primary texts (the stories you read) as well as any secondary sources (information about the author or historical context or about the story). Remember that you must explain why those examples support your overall argument/thesis. Lead your reader by the hand through the maze of your thinking. They may not see the same things you do, so you have to point them out. Be sure to include citations for your examples – what page did the example come from? If it is from a secondary source, name the author.

Conclusion – The conclusion is where you wrap everything up. A good way to bring it full circle is to refer back to your opening attention grabber/hook. Then remind your reader of your thesis statement and main points, and end with a short discussion (1-3 sentences) for why your paper matters. So what? What is the significance of what you just argued?

Works Cited page – You must include a list of your sources at the end of your paper, even if it is just the one story you read in class. Refer to the Purdue OWL for how to create a Works Cited page using MLA formatting.

This Unit’s Author

Since the novel for this course (and consequently its author) changes depending on your instructor, you may be reading a different novel than someone who took this very same course last term. As such, when looking for information about the author and/or other contextual information, look to the announcements for the week. Your instructor will provide you with links to that information. But you should also do some digging on your own! What can you find about the author? About the historical time period in which it was written? About the particular genre (or genres) that the novel might fall into?

Now why does any of this matter? Because reading a text through a biographical or historical or cultural lens can give you a new or better understanding of the text.

Definition

Definition comes from An Introduction to Fiction by X.J. Kennedy and Dana Gioia, 11th Edition, Boston: Longman, 2010.

Novel: An extended work of fictional prose narrative. The term “novel” usually implies a book-length narrative (as compared to more compact forms of prose fiction such as the short story). Because of its extended length, a novel usually has more characters, more varied scenes, and a broader coverage of time than a short story.
