# Unit 1: Lecture

What is Fiction?

There are four main categories or genres of literature: fiction, nonfiction, drama, and poetry. In this course, we will focus on the first: fiction. But what distinguishes it from the other three? Well, poetry is usually written in verse (with lines and stanzas), and it emphasizes emotions and ideas through word choice and rhythm. Drama can be written in verse (think Shakespeare) or prose, and it can be based on facts or made up, but it is meant to be performed – usually through dialogue. Nonfiction is usually written in prose (sentences and paragraphs), and it usually focuses on facts or real events and people. Fiction does some of the same things as the previous genres. For example, fiction often pays close attention to language and how it sounds, like poetry. It can contain dialogue, like drama. And it is usually written in prose like nonfiction, but fictional stories are shaped by the imagination. That is not to say they can’t reveal true things, but the stories are not entirely factual. The setting might reflect reality and the experiences the characters face may mirror events that can happen in real life, but the exact details of the story come from the author’s imagination.

Within the larger category (or genre) of fiction, there are numerous subcategories (subgenres). We will spend significant time during the semester discussing some of these, but for this first unit we will focus on the short story. A short story is usually defined as a brief fictional prose narrative, often involving one unified episode. A short story cannot be as comprehensive as a novel and thus must concentrate as much as possible into a few pages. Some of the elements or features you might explore in the short story include plot, conflict, characters, setting, and theme.

Elements of Fiction

When reading a story, the most obvious element is the plot. It is the sequence of events in the story and their relationship to one another that keeps the reader engaged. The plot can follow a chronological order (first this happened, then this, finally this), but sometimes it skips around and the reader has to pay attention to whether an event is happening in the current time of the story or is a flashback or vision of the past. The plot often revolves a conflict or struggle between opposing forces. These opposing forces affect the main character, or protagonist. The opposing force can be another character, an antagonist, or it can be a force of nature, society, or external event. These are external conflicts. But an opposing force can also be internal, a struggle within the main character herself.

The plot often follows a pattern, which can be seen below in Gustav Freytag’s pyramid. It begins with exposition, which sets up the background or setting of the conflict. Then it dramatizes events that set the conflict in motion (the rising action). Next comes the climax or turning point, which is the emotional high point of the narrative. The events begin to wind down in the falling action and finally conclude with the denouement, which resolves the conflict (to varying degrees).

Another important part of a story are the characters who are participating in and guiding the events of the plot. Characters can be flat or round, static or dynamic. Flat characters are one-dimensional and don’t show much complexity or depth. Round characters are multi-dimensional, complex, and more like real human beings. A static character does not change, while a dynamic one does. The reader learns about the characters through paying attention to what they say and how they say it as well as their actions. These reveal the characters’ personalities and morals. The characters are sometimes defined by their setting. Setting is the place and time in which a story’s actions take place, but it can also be the broader culture or way of life of the characters. To set the scene and suggest a mood or atmosphere for the events, the writer attempts to create in the reader’s visual imagination the illusion of a solid world in which the story takes place. 

The last element for this unit is theme. Theme is a generalization about the meaning of the story. It is more than just the subject or topic of the story, which can often be described in one word. Instead, a theme requires several words or a phrase, because it is an abstract statement of the meaning of the story. It does not need to be a moral judgement. Theme is best discussed last, because it is a consequence of all the other elements in a story. A story does not have to have a single theme; it can have several. If you were to read Nathaniel Hawthorne’s “Young Goodman Brown,” you might describe it as being about religious fanaticism. But a theme would be “losing religious faith can destroy a person’s life.”

How to Read and Write About Fiction

Now that you know a little about what fiction is and how we might talk about it, what do we do when we are actually sitting down to read a text? You probably want to have a pen and paper nearby to jot down notes (studies show handwriting notes is more effective in learning and retaining information than typing), and you want to find a location or space that is conducive to concentration. If that means locking yourself in your bedroom so your kids will leave you alone for 30 minutes or going to a coffee shop with strong, caffeinated beverages, do what you need to do. When first approaching a text, you will want to think about the genre and expectations you have about that genre, and taking a look at the title and year of publication might give you a bit of context as well. As you start to read and think about how you might talk about (or write about) the text, consider the following tips, which are adapted from Janet E. Gardner's Writing about Literature: A Portable Guide. Bedford, 2009.

Tips for Good Reading

Slow down and reread complex passages several times 

Look up difficult words in the dictionary 

Annotate the text by underlining key phrases and write questions or concerns in the margins 

Consider how or why these passages are compelling to you 

Mark those places in the text that make you feel uncomfortable or present a worldview that feels strange to you 

During and after you read, take notes so you can keep track of your ideas 

Try to identify the genre of the text. Describe its style and tone. 

Try to learn more about the author and his/her cultural context 

Reflect on your perspective as a reader and how that might affect your interpretation

Tips for Writing about Literature

Don't assume that your readers will remember (or consider important) the same ideas or incidents in the literature that you do. 

Do not retell the plot or text at length. 

Do not assume that quotations or summaries are self-sufficient and prove your point automatically. You have to explain why the details you have identified are important.

It is customary to use the present tense when writing about literature. 

The first time you mention an author, use his or her full name.  Thereafter, use the author's last name. 

Titles of poems, stories, and essays should be put in quotation marks. Titles of books, plays, and periodicals should be underlined or italicized. 

Give your paper a title. 

Use common sense and be consistent.

Definitions

Definitions come from An Introduction to Fiction by X.J. Kennedy and Dana Gioia, 11th Edition, Boston: Longman, 2010.

Antagonist: The most significant character or force that opposes the protagonist in a narrative or drama. The antagonist may be another character, society itself, a force of nature, or even – in modern literature – conflicting impulses within the protagonist.

Character: An imagined figure inhabiting a narrative or drama. By convention, the reader or spectator endows the fictional character with moral, dispositional, and emotional qualities expressed in what the character says – the dialogue – and by what he or she does – the action. What a character says and does in any particular situation is motivated by his or her desires, temperament, and moral nature.

Conflict: In Greek, agon, or contest. The central struggle between two or more forces in a story. Conflict generally occurs when some person or thing prevents the protagonist from achieving his or her intended goal. Opposition can arise [externally] from another character, external events, preexisting situations, fate, or even [internally] through some aspect of the main character’s own personality. Conflict is the basic material out of which most plots are made.

Fiction: From the Latin ficio, “act of fashioning, a shaping, a making.” Fiction refers to any literary work that – although it may contain factual information – is not bound by factual accuracy, but creates a narrative shaped or made up by the author’s imagination.

Plot: The particular arrangement of actions, events, and situations that unfold in a narrative. A plot is not merely the general story of a narrative but the author’s artistic pattern made from the parts of the narrative, including exposition, complications, rising action, climax, falling action, and denouement. How an author chooses to construct the plot determines the way the reader experiences the story.

Protagonist: The central character in a literary work. The protagonist usually initiates the main action of the story, often in conflict with the antagonist.

Setting: The time and place of a literary work. The setting may also include the climate and even the social, psychological, or spiritual state of the participants.

Short Story: A prose narrative too brief to be published in a separate volume – as novellas and novels frequently are. The short story is usually a focused narrative that presents one or two main characters involved in a single compelling action.

Theme: A generally recurring subject (topic) or idea conspicuously evident in a literary work. A short didactic work like a fable may have a single obvious theme, but longer works can contain multiple themes. Not all subjects in a work can be considered themes, only the central subject or subjects.
