# Unit 7: Lecture

Genre and Graphic Fiction

We have covered several different genres so far this semester, from fables and fairy tales to short stories, novellas, and novels to gothic and magical realism. In this unit, we will read the first chapter from a novel, but what makes this text different from the novel we just read is that it is a graphic novel. Graphic Fiction is usually a substantial single volume of pictorial images arranged in a sequence to narrate a story with or without words. 

A panel from Fun Home

Some of you will be very familiar with comics (shorter, less complex examples of graphic fiction), but many of you will probably be unfamiliar with reading a graphic novel. I've included below some tips for reading, many of which would work for any literary text but several that are specific to graphic novels.

Take notes - Just as you would with any assigned reading, take notes. Mark (highlight/underline) places in the text that you think are important, that you don't understand, or that evoke some kind of emotional response from you. For example, I might put a smiley face next to a line or scene I find funny, or I might write a brief note to myself about a scene that relates to a theme I've seen elsewhere in the text or even in other texts we've read. With homework from several classes, it is often difficult to remember exactly what you read the night before, so taking notes is crucial.

Read and look at the images sequentially and simultaneously - Words require sequential reading. For example, you read left to right in order, one word after the other. In contrast, images are usually read simultaneously. For instance, you take in everything in an image at once. You will need to do both with a graphic novel. (Lynell Burmark "Visual Literacy" 8)

Pay attention to the appearance of the written text - Written text can sometimes indicate motion or sound or feeling (exclamation points, all capital letters, bolded print, etc.). The position of the text can also indicate whether we are to read it from the narrator’s perspective or as dialog among the characters. For example, a flat text box at the top of the page might indicate narration from our main character, whereas the bubbles of text are spoken by the characters.

Look at the panels like a film - When analyzing a film, you might consider where the director places the camera. In other words, how is the scene framed and what is the focal point? Are we looking up, down, from a distance, close-up, or from behind? For a graphic novel, we might ask similar questions.

Examine the overall layout of the two-page spread - Look at each frame, then the panel, then the entire page and two-page spread, noting the contrast or blending of panels. We will briefly practice this in class, but think about things such as the size of the frames, how many there are in each row, whether the frames show the same setting or jump to a different setting, the similarity or difference in color choices/shading, and whether they indicate movement.

Writing About Fiction

The writing skill we will discuss this week is how to integrate sources effectively (and ethically) into your paper. You have been practicing using evidence and examples from the stories themselves to support your claims all semester, but for this final Core Assessment essay you will also have to use several secondary sources.

The strategy that often works for students is to imagine a quote sandwich. The meat or veggie of the sandwich is your quote (or paraphrase or summary) with an appropriate citation in MLA style. The top piece of bread is your introductory phrase/signal phrase/attributive tag. This phrase leads into your quote, letting your reader know a little about it and setting it up. Who said it? Where’s it from? How does it transition from what you were just discussing? Then the bottom piece of bread in this quote sandwich is your explanation. Why is this quote, this information, important? How does it support my claims about the literature? This bottom piece of bread (your explanation) is the foundation of the whole sandwich, without it the whole thing falls apart. You have to explain to your readers why you are using that particular quote. It cannot just stand on its own; don’t expect it to.
