Class,

I chose to the article "A Study of Narrative Strategies in A Farewell to Arms." The article examined some literary analysis techniques that I was unfamiliar with; analepsis which is a literary device in narrative, in which a past event is narrated at a point later than its chronological place in a story, according to Google, and prolepsis which "is a foreshadowing or anticipation in telling a story." (Kaifu) I want to expand on the latter; while reading A Farewell to Arms I did not recognize some of the symbolism being used till I read about it in the academic journal by Chen Kaifu. What particularly struck me as obvious, once I read through the article, was the foreshowing and symbolism of 'rain.' Hemingway used the symbolic occurrence to forecast, ill events that happen to Henry and Catherine. There is an excellent quote in the book that strikes me when Catherine is talking to Henry after his knee injury; “That’s good. Because I am afraid of the rain. I like to walk in it. But it’s very hard on loving. I’m afraid of the rain because sometimes I see me dead in it”. The rain is constantly foreshowing Catherine's death. The book ends with Henry walking home from the hospital, in you guessed it, the rain.

The article wasn't terribly hard to understand. I had to work through a couple words and definitions, but after a couple quick google searches I was able to push through. It highlighted quite a few points that I agreed with but it also had an analysis that I am not convinced of. "In his essay Whittier (1992) argues that Catherine’s death in childbirth has a prophetic significance, which signifies women’s procreativity in the interests of male art." The article did not go deeper then a paragraph into this subject, but I would need further examples to prove that is more then just idioms used, in a era where the male and female role were culturally segregated in 1929.

 

Kaifu, Chen. "A Study of Narrative Strategies in A Farewell to Arms." English Language Teaching; Vol. 12, No. 9; 2019. English Language Teaching; Vol. 12, No. 9; 2019. Online Published: August 30, 2019. doi: 10.5539/elt.v12n9p122
