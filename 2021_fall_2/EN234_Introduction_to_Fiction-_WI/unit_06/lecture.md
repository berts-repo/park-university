Reading a Scholarly Article

A scholarly article is an essay written by an academic (likely someone who teaches at a university and has advanced degrees) and is probably published in a peer-reviewed academic journal (published by a university or scholarly press, peers in the field have decided it is worth publishing). For literature, scholarly articles are often complex literary analyses of a text or collection of texts, highly researched investigations of an author or historical literary movement, or theoretical explorations of a text.

When approaching a scholarly article, take your time and don’t worry about understanding every sentence or reference. If you have read scholarly articles in other classes, you will know that they can sometime be full of jargon (language specific to a discipline) and assume a higher level of knowledge than you possess. But that does not mean you can’t still get some good information.

Tips:

You might skim through the first page or two of several different articles before making your decision on the one you want to read fully. Pick one that seems to address a topic you are interested in and that does not seem too difficult (it should be a little challenging, but you don’t want to be totally lost).

Choose an article that is at least 5 pages long – anything shorter than that won’t go into much detail.

Read the abstract (if it has one) – it will give you an overview of the whole article – and pay attention to the title (it will often give you clues to the author’s thesis)

Print out the article. Read with a pen and/or highlighter.

Take your time reading. Look up words you don’t know.

Underline or highlight important sentences. Write in the margins (these can be questions or ideas that you have while reading).

Reread as necessary.

Again, don’t worry if you didn’t understand everything. As long as you can accurately summarize the main points of the article and give a few examples, then that is all you need to do. If you do understand everything, great job!
