# Unit 3: Lecture

Genre and the Gothic’s Influence

Two more elements of fiction that will be important this week are style and symbol. Style is a characteristic way an author uses language to create literature. This might include rhetorical patterns, such as sentence length and complexity, word choice (diction) and placement, and punctuation. It might mean the level of formality or use of slang. However, you must be careful to separate the narrative voice, which is a creation of the author, from the actual voice of the writer. The writer/author/storyteller evokes the imaginary landscape of fiction and conveys part of the story’s meaning, but keep in mind that the writer may not agree with what a character does or says.

Symbolism or the use of literary symbols can be an aspect of an author’s style. A symbol can be anything in a story’s setting, plot, or characterization that suggests an abstract meaning to the reader in addition to its literal significance. Think back to “The Yellow Wallpaper.” The wallpaper is an actual physical object in the story and it’s ugly color and unusual pattern catch the protagonist’s attention, but as a symbol it might represent the decay of her mental health or her feelings of entrapment.

In moving to the readings for this unit, we need to dig a little deeper into genre. It has been mentioned in previous units – remember that a short story is a genre of literature, but within fiction there are numerous genres. Genre is often used synonymously with category, and separate genres have a set of characteristics that define it. Readers and authors usually agree on what those characteristics are, but sometimes writers will mix things up and surprise a reader by going outside the normal boundaries of a genre. Over the years, many of the big genres have broken up into smaller subgenres. Consider the genre of comedy; there are romantic comedies, buddy comedies, satirical comedies, slapstick comedies, dark comedies, etc. You might even be able to break down the subgenres into even smaller categories. The rest of the semester, we will explore several different genres.

This unit, we will read several examples falling into the genre of Gothic literature. Gothic literature is the term used to describe work with a sinister or grotesque tone that seeks to evoke a sense of terror on the part of the reader or audience.  The gothic became popular in the 18th and 19th centuries and included such works as The Monk, The Mysteries of Udolfo, Frankenstein, Wuthering Heights, Dracula, and most of Edgar Allan Poe’s work. The gothic genre is characterized by a general mood of decay, suspense, and terror; action that is dramatic and generally violent or otherwise disturbing; loves that are destructively passionate, and landscapes that are grandiose, if gloomy or bleak. There is often a blurring of lines or hybridity (life/death, natural/supernatural, ancient/modern, realistic/artificial, unconscious/conscious); haunting / ghosts; an isolated castle/mansion/or monastery; and/or deviant Others (outsiders, people who are different/Other). According to some scholars, in the early eighteenth century there was a difference between gothic written by women and gothic written by men, and the difference fell to terror versus horror. Terror is a mental operation that lifts the mind to higher wisdom, while horror is a physical operation and can numb the mind; the difference between “the smell of death and stumbling against a corpse.” It is the terror of knowing something is there, is wrong, but not being able to see it; versus the horror of seeing with your own eyes that something terrible has happened.

The gothic is also often associated with the “uncanny,” which Freud defined as a dread when confronted with certain people or things or experiences, the repressed feelings and complexes that come back to haunt a person.

One genre that grew out of the gothic is crime fiction. Crime fiction is usually suspenseful and focuses on crimes committed, the criminals and their motivations, and the solving of the mysteries by detectives/law enforcement/private eyes/interested parties. Detective fiction is a type or subgenre of crime fiction (detectives are necessary in order to figure out who committed the crimes, after all), and Poe’s 1841 “Murders in the Rue Morgue” is often considered the earliest detective fiction. But Sir Arthur Conan Doyle’s Sherlock Holmes (written decades later) is perhaps the most famous detective in literature. The reason detective fiction started in the mid-nineteenth century is because that is when the modern detective was born. Police forces were being created, and a need arose for detectives (or inspectors) to investigate specific crimes. Detective (or whodunnit) fiction often focuses on unravelling the events that involved the crime and focus on the activity of detecting. Sometimes the activities by the person trying to solve the mystery will stray outside the law, and they may be confronted by dangerous characters, which pushes the genre towards thriller.

Engaging with Literature in Different Ways

Most literature courses in college will focus on literary analysis as the most effective way to approach a text. And we will do the same in this class (reading closely, figuring out how the different elements work together, discussing what it all means). However, anyone who has seen a film adaptation of a novel knows that there is more than one way to engage with the original text.

As you think about your Midterm Creative Project, consider how responding to a text in more creative ways might actually help you to understand the text better (or at least differently) than you would have done in the more traditional way. For example, in rewriting a short story from another character’s perspective, what do you learn about point of view or tone? In creating a visual or multimedia representation of a story, what symbols did you pick up on? In doing research about the historical setting of a story, how does it make you rethink the characters’ roles?

*Activity to try: Pick one of the stories we’ve read so far this semester and attempt to draw the setting. What colors will you use? Can you figure out the layout of a room or town? What specific details will you add about furniture or landmarks? (This activity combines the creative and visual with close reading.)

Definitions

Definitions come from An Introduction to Fiction by X.J. Kennedy and Dana Gioia, 11th Edition, Boston: Longman, 2010.

Genre: A conventional combination of literary form and subject matter, usually aimed at creating certain effects. A genre implies a preexisting understanding between the artist and the reader about the purpose and rules of the work. A horror story, for example, combines the form of the short story with certain conventional subjects, style, and theme with the expectation of frightening the reader. Major short story genres include science fiction, gothic, horror, and detective tales.

Style: All the distinctive ways in which an author, genre, movement, or historical period uses language to create a literary work. An author’s style depends on his or her characteristic use of diction (word choice), imagery, tone, syntax (sentence structure), figurative language, and punctuation. 

Symbol: A person, place, or thing in a narrative that suggests meanings beyond its literal sense. A symbol usually contains multiple meanings and associations. In Herman Melville’s Moby Dick, for example, the great white whale does not have just a single significance but accrues powerful associations as the narrative progresses.
