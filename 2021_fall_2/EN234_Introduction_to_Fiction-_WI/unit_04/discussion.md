I will be exploring the different elements of fictional literature in "To Build a Fire," if it was to be written as a magical realism story. The first thing to explore is the setting. If the tone of the book is set into a mythical world we can interpret things like an overcast day as something maleficent and dark; "There was no sun nor hint of sun, though there was not a cloud in the sky." As we look more into the setting, most readers will be unfamiliar with extremely cold weather, and the freezing of the mans spit mid air can be imagined as a unnatural rather then real weather conditions. The line, "There was a sharp, explosive crackle that startled him," takes on a different tone if are the reader is imagining it within a magical realm.

The point of view of the protagonist also takes on a different meaning, while imagining the story as magical realism. The readers view of the man comes off as, overly confident and "the tremendous cold, and the strangeness and weirdness of it all--made no impression on the man. If we were to take him as the hero or even a victim of the abnormal we would see the man differently. The reader could describe the man as brave in both versions of the story, but again the tone would be a different kind of brave.

Without rewriting the story, even the relationships in Freytag's Pyramid takes on different outlooks. The climax or falling action falls into a perception of being something unreal instead of the stupid actions of the main character.
