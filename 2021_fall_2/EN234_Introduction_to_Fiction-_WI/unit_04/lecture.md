# Unit 4: Lecture

Genre, the Novella, and Magical Realism

In this unit, we will read a longer piece of fiction, a novella. Novellas, as you might be able to deduce from its name, are short novels or extra long short stories. The difference between a short story and a novella is not just its length, but the characters are usually more complex and there is more development of the plot.

In terms of what other genre Kafka’s novella might fall into, it is usually agreed that it is a mix of fantastical elements and the everyday/the real. This mixture describes a genre called Magical Realism, where the story introduces something magical or fantastic as if it were just an everyday occurrence or at least not as extraordinary as we might expect.

Definitions

Definitions come from An Introduction to Fiction by X.J. Kennedy and Dana Gioia, 11th Edition, Boston: Longman, 2010.

Realism: An attempt to reproduce faithfully the surface appearance of life, especially that of ordinary people in everyday situations. As a literary term, realism has two meanings – one general, the other historical. In a general sense, realism refers to the representation of characters, events, and settings in ways that the spectator will consider plausible. In a historical sense, it can refer to a literary and theatrical movement in nineteenth-century Europe.

Magical Realism: A type of contemporary narrative in which the magical and the mundane are mixed in an overall context of realistic storytelling. The term was arguably first used by Cuban novelist Alejo Carpentier in 1949 to describe the matter-of-fact combination of the fantastic and everyday in Latin American fiction, but others have argued for earlier origins, including in discussions of Kafka’s Metamorphosis.

Novella: In modern terms, a prose narrative longer than a short story but shorter than a novel (approximately 30,000 to 50,000 words). Unlike a short story, a novella is long enough to be published independently as a brief book.
