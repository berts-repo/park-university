# Unit 2: Lecture

Early Fiction

Storytelling has existed for millennia, well before the invention of the alphabet or even the printing press. The earliest fictional tales told to entertain or instruct were originally transmitted orally, from person to person. Creation myths explaining how the world and people came to be as well as legends and epic narratives about national heroes are some of the first and most common stories across all cultures. An important stage in the evolution of the story was the period of beast fables, stories in which animals are shown acting like humans in order to teach a moral lesson. The Greek slave Aesop (620?-560? BCE) and his fables are the most well-known.

"The Lion and the Mouse," from The Æsop for Children

In the medieval period in Europe, most short narratives were written in verse and written either about heroic deeds or lowly comic tales (or in Don Quixote a mixture of both). Prose was only used for devotional or instructional pieces. Then in the 14th century, secular tales in both prose and verse became popular (think Chaucer’s The Canterbury Tales). But it wasn’t until the 18th century that fictional narratives as we might recognize them started to be produced. Periodicals (newspapers/magazines) offered a venue to share stories to a wider public, and a new market grew up for professional writers. The modern novel was invented and experimented with starting in the 18th century and extending through today, while the modern short story showed up in the nineteenth century. In Germany, Jakob and Wilhelm Grimm assembled a collection of fairy tales, which were originally shared orally. In America, Washington Irving, Nathaniel Hawthorne, and Edgar Allan Poe greatly influenced short fiction. Fiction was no longer simply about heroes or emphasizing moral truths; it could be humorous, it could deal with realistic experiences, it could evoke strong emotions, and it could reflect or even criticize current cultural/social issues. Of course, throughout the centuries, authors continued to experiment and try new things, but they were often (always?) constrained by the tastes of their readers and their own education and/or their access to publishers.

Much of this history comes from The Story and Its Writer by Ann Charters, 8th Edition, Bedford, 2011.

More Elements of Fiction

In addition to the features introduced in Unit 1 (plot, conflict, characters, setting, theme), there are several others that are important to consider when reading fiction. Point of view refers to the author’s choice of a narrator for the story. The narration could be in first-person (“I”), where the narrator is a major or minor character in the story. The first-person narrator can move freely within the fictional world and interact with other characters, but the narrator can’t know what any other character is thinking except through observation of their language and actions. The first-person narrator can be reliable or unreliable, meaning we the readers should or shouldn’t trust their point of view. Or the narration could be in third-person (“She” or “He” or “They”), where the narrator is not a participant in the story. This third-person narrator could be omniscient or all-seeing, or he or she could be limited omniscient, which means he or she can see into one or maybe two characters minds but not all. Finally, there is a second-person narrator (“you”), but that is much less common.

An author’s choice of tone in a narrative conveys his or her unstated attitudes towards the story. Tone is created by many different elements, but word choice and setting is often important in creating tone. Remember to be careful judging tone when reading a work in translation, it is very dependent on the translator and whether they accurately/effectively captured the original.

Creating a Thesis

When I ask you to make a claim or an argument, argument refers to a writer's or speaker's (that's you!) attempt to establish the validity of a given position. When you write a paper, you work to convince your reader that what you are saying is valid and persuasive.

Difference between a topic and thesis: Your topic is the issue or area upon which you will focus your attention, and your thesis is a statement about this topic.

Topic: I am interested in the exploration of sin in Jonson's "On My First Son." 

Thesis: In "On My First Son," the speaker seems to use the death of his young son as a way to explore his own sin. However, the poet is just as interested in demonstrating his poetic mastery by drawing from an ancient Roman epigram as his model.

Thesis should be: 

Clear and specific 

Relevant 

Debatable 

Original 

Complete sentence 

Appropriate to prompt/assignment

Tips for Drafting, Revising, and Editing

Can your reader immediately identify what the topic of the essay will be - that is, which text(s) you will analyze, and which angle of the text (for example, which prompt you’ve chosen)? 

Have you narrowed the scope of the thesis for your reader? How could it be further narrowed? Remember, it's not enough to say "Women are portrayed differently in X and Y." What do you mean by "differently"? Get as specific as possible.

Does your thesis clearly identify a claim that is contestable but valid? 

Has your thinking about the issues evolved as you have written? If so, how will you change your thesis statement? 

Have you answered the larger "So What?" question? Why is your argument important? 

Does the order of the ideas and paragraphs make immediate sense to you, or does some alternate structure suggest itself? 

What textual evidence have you used? Is it sufficiently provocative and persuasive? Or does it veer off into another direction? 

Have you successfully integrated quotations into your own writing, while at the same time acknowledging your source? A quotation that is unintroduced or uncontextualized, left disconnected from your sentences, is a good sign that you haven't thought enough about how this textual evidence supports your larger point - how it fits into your framework. 

Does every piece of evidence, every sentence, and every paragraph contribute to the validity of your argument? If not, eliminate extraneous discussions and save them for another project. 

Have you worked to convey why you are citing a particular passage? What particular details in it provide evidence that supports your interpretation?

Definitions

Definitions come from An Introduction to Fiction by X.J. Kennedy and Dana Gioia, 11th Edition, Boston: Longman, 2010.

Fable: A brief, often humorous narrative told to illustrate a moral. The characters in fables are traditionally animals whose personality traits symbolize human traits. Particular animals have conventionally come to represent specific human qualities or values. For example, the ant represents industry, the fox craftiness, and the lion nobility. A fable often concludes by summarizing its moral message in abstract terms.

Fairy Tale: A traditional form of short narrative folklore, originally transmitted orally, that features supernatural characters such as witches, giants, fairies, or animals with human personality traits. Fairy tales often feature a hero or heroine who seems destined to achieve some desirable fate – such as marrying a prince or princess, becoming wealthy, or destroying an enemy.

Point of view: The perspective from which a story is told. There are many types of point of view, including first-person narrator and third-person narrator. First-person narration occurs in a story in which the narrator is a participant in the action. Such a narrator refers to himself or herself as “I” and may be a major or minor character in the story. His or her attitude and understanding of characters and events shapes the reader’s perception of the story being told. Third-person narration is a type of narration in which the narrator is a nonparticipant. In a third-person narrative the characters are referred to as “he,” “she,” or “they.” Third person narrators are most commonly omniscient, but the level of their knowledge may vary from total omniscience (the narrator knows everything about the characters and their lives) to limited omniscience (the narrator is limited to the perceptions of a single character).

Tone: The attitude toward a subject conveyed in a literary work. No single stylistic device creates tone; it is the net result of the various elements an author brings to creating the work’s feeling and manner. Tone may be playful, sarcastic, ironic, sad, solemn, or any other possible attitude. A writer’s tone plays an important role in establishing the reader’s relationship to the characters or ideas presented in a literary work.

Unreliable Narrator: A narrator who – intentionally or unintentionally – relates events in a subjective or distorted manner. The author usually provides some indication early on in such stories that the narrator is not to be completely trusted.
