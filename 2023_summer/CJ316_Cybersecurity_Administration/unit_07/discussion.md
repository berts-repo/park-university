The main goal of contingency planning (CP) is being able to mitigate a threat and return the system back to functioning order after a threat event. According to our textbook and Hartford insurance company 40% of businesses that don't have a CP go out of business after a disaster. With threats continuously getting more sophisticated and the scope from which a disaster can develop (e.g. fire, theft, cyberthreat) it has become more important then ever to implement strategies, policies, and protocol to minimize the damages.

Utilities organizations (e.g. water, gas, electric) are some organizations that I find to be "falling short" of implementing cybersecurity contingencies. These systems are built on old systems like ICS, SCADA , and PLS micro-controllers, and are increasingly more susceptible to cyber threats. (SCADA systems, 2023) The reason that these systems falling short isn't necessarily the companies fault, but they were developed before cybersecurity had developed into the threat it is today, these system are old and threats are getting more sophisticated. As the need grows to monitor and connect to the internet, these systems are falling victim to a growing threat and a lack to be able to properly implement security practices.
