# Unit 7: Overview 

Proofreading and Editing

Our attention this unit turns to the cumulative project (and core assessment) for EN106, the Writing Portfolio. You should plan to dedicate a minimum of five hours this unit to preparing your portfolio. This unit’s discussion and peer review areas are intended to support you as you gather materials, proofread and line-edit the essays you will include in your portfolio, and create a new reflective essay to introduce your portfolio.

Your portfolio is a “best works” portfolio; its goal is to demonstrate your progress toward the core learning outcomes of the course. To that end, your portfolio must include:

Your two best essays from this EN106 course. These essays must be the product of multiple revisions, and should demonstrate your ability with the core learning outcomes of Focus, Development, Rhetorical Strategies, and Conventions. These essays should be representative of your absolute best work as an academic writer.

Artifacts that reveal your writing processes (brainstorming documents, discussion posts, outlines, rough drafts, peer feedback, instructor comments, etc.). These artifacts should fit together to provide a glimpse of your ability with the core learning outcome of Process.

A well-developed reflective essay that addresses your writing in the context of our writing-as-conversation metaphor and/or our core learning outcomes of Process, Focus, Development, Rhetorical Strategies, and Conventions. (More detail on this piece is found below and in the Portfolio content item.)

As you begin to organize your materials and writing the reflective essay, you might consider looking again at Greene and Lidinsky’s “habits of mind.” We discussed these habits in Unit One, and you have been practicing these habits throughout the term. Now is the time to reflect. Which of these habits came most naturally to you? Which habit did you struggle to enact in your coursework? What advice for future students do you have about these habits of mind? You should also consider reflecting on the overall aims of this course. To what extent have you sharpened your skills as a rhetorical reader and writer? What progress do you expect to make as an academic writer over the next term, semester, or year? Let your answers to these questions guide your work on the reflective essay. You may also use the Department of English philosophy statement about writing in EN106 (See Course Overview and Schedule) as inspiration for your reflective essay. A few more recommendations are provided in the Portfolio content item under Unit Seven.

For this unit's Peer Review, you will select one of the two essays you plan to include in your portfolio to share with peers, or you may choose to upload a draft of your reflective essay. The choice is yours: consider where you most need advice/feedback/help/collaboration at this stage in the revision process. In this peer review, we will concentrate on what compositionists term “lower-order concerns”— word choice (diction), sentence variety, sentence-level grammar, and correctness. Note that this assumes that your essays are already succeeding in "higher- order concerns"—focus/thesis, development, organization, research, rhetorical strategies. 

It is imperative to start the process of peer review as early in the unit as possible, so that you can get (and give) feedback to apply before the portfolio is due on Sunday. For this reason, please note that your initial posts to the “peer review” discussion area are due on Wednesday midnight CST, and you must respond to one peer’s draft no later than Friday midnight CST.

Task List

Monday - Wednesday, read all assignments and lecture material for  the unit, including the Portfolio assignment.

By Wednesday, post your selected essay in the Peer Review area.

By Thursday noon CST, make your post to the unit Discussion area. Note: There is no requirement for additional posts in this unit's Discussion area due to the expectation for dialogue in the Peer Review area.

By Friday, post a response to one peer’s essay in the Peer Review area. This will give you and your peers the weekend to incorporate each other’s readerly feedback.

By  Sunday  midnight  CST,  submit  your  final  draft  portfolio in the required file format (.doc, .docx, or .pdf). See the Portfolio content item for instructions on formatting and submitting your work.

Unit Learning Outcomes

Critically reflect on your written products and processes for writing.

Arrange your portfolio, considering the points of emphasis in the assessment rubric.

Apply writing processes, collaborative strategies, and effective academic research practices to participate in academic discourse (CLO #1).

Use common formats and conventions (e.g., research, structure, documentation, tone, mechanics) for various genres of academic discourse (CLO #5).
