# Unit 1: Lecture

The Rhetorical Situation

The term “rhetoric” often has a negative association. You may have watched a television pundit declaim, “Oh, that’s just rhetoric.” Or you may have read a political analyst decrying a senator's recent speech as "empty rhetoric." But rhetoric, as a form of knowledge, has a rich, complicated history. Scholars often associate rhetoric with classical Greek and Roman civilizations. In these societies, speaking and writing effectively were highly regarded as performances for influencing public awareness of civic issues. To be a “good” Greek or Roman citizen often meant developing the ability to employ skillful rhetoric in the public arena. (Of course, many pre-classical and non-Western societies have also developed rhetorical traditions. In fact, the more you study rhetoric this term, the more rhetoric you will see every day in the world around you.)

Rhetoric will be central to our study of academic writing in EN106. This is because rhetoric deals with the effective uses of language in a particular situation. First, let's define what this term rhetoric means. In fact, modern definitions of rhetoric vary considerably. I.A. Richards writes that rhetoric is “the study of misunderstandings and their remedies.” Kenneth Burke, on other hand, suggests that rhetoric deals with creating meaning and identifying with those who are different from you. Aristotle's definition is perhaps most famous: rhetoric is the consideration of the "available means of persuasion" in any given situation. So we can see that rhetoric deals with the how: how writers write, how speakers speak, how designers design, and so on. Rhetorical awareness compels us to consider our purpose for writing, our audience,  and  the  most  effective  means  for  persuading  readers  to  consider  our perspectives.

Let's use the "rhetorical triangle" above to interpret a simple example. Imagine that you have sent an email to your friends and family members, inviting them to a graduation party at your home. This email reads as follows:

Come one, come all – to a graduation open house!

Help  us  celebrate  Ilana's   graduation  from  Park  University   and commencement into her new career.

In lieu of gifts, please consider donating to Ilana's favorite charity:

see details below.

This Sunday, at the home of Jacob and Ilana Perez, 3-7 pm. Soft drinks and light appetizers provided.

Who or what is the subject, text, writer, reader, and context in this rhetorical situation?

The writer is, of course, you, and the readers are your family members. What is the subject of this email? Most basically, the subject is the party. The text you have produced is the email itself -- but what does that text consist of? Why an email, rather than a posted letter, a text message, a tweet, or a series of phone calls? Why use the word "open house" instead of "gathering," "party," "get-together," "fiesta," or "soiree"? And what about this idea of context? (For instance, how might the "cultural circumstances" of the writer's region, ethnicity, and family traditions influenced this email -- or even the type of party to be had?)

These kinds of questions are at the heart of rhetorical analysis. You may find that the number of guests you want to invite require the use of email, for the sake of efficiency. And you might find that "party" sets the best tone for the kind of event you want to host. And you might consider that if your party were to be more formal, a mailed invitation would be most appropriate.

As you move ahead with the work of the course, return to these models as a basis for rhetorical analysis. By identifying each of the factors involved in any rhetorical situation, you will come to understand it more fully and thereby be equipped to find the best means of persuasion. In fact, this type of rhetorical analysis can be used to more clearly understand any rhetorical event, from a small child appealing to a parent for ice cream to a U.S. President giving a State of the Union address.

As you engage in challenging reading and writing assignments this term, consider how you can “read rhetorically.” Greene and Lidinsky’s “Steps to Analyzing a Text Rhetorically” will help you complete this unit’s discussion assignment and, indeed, will be useful to you throughout the term:

Steps to Analyzing a Text Rhetorically

Identify the situation. What motivates the writer to write?

Identify the writer’s purpose. What does the writer want readers to do or think about?

Identify the writer’s claims. What is the writer’s main claim, or thesis? What minor claims does he or she make?

Identify the writer’s audience. What do you know about the writer’s audience? What does the writer’s language imply about the readers? What about the writer’s references? The structure of the essay?

This unit you will use these tools of rhetorical analysis to uncover a great deal about effective writing.
