# Unit 3: Overview 

Defining Academic Conversations

This unit, you will move from the sidelines of the academic conversation to the center stage, by crafting a thesis-driven essay that responds to at least one article you have read. Reading assignments, lecture material, and class discussions will focus on learning how to develop a persuasive, arguable thesis that makes a contribution to an academic conversation. As you move through this unit's coursework, keep in mind the lessons you learned about rhetoric and close reading in Units One and Two. Your earlier learning will serve as useful tools as your study this unit culminates in a persuasive essay, due Sunday.

Task List

Monday - Wednesday, read all assignments and lecture material for the unit, including the essay assignment.

By Thursday noon CST, make your initial posts in the unit Discussion area.

Thursday – Sunday, interact with peers by posting responses in the Discussion.

By Sunday midnight CST, submit your final draft essay in the required file format (.doc, .docx, or .pdf). See the Essay content item for instructions on formatting and submitting your assignment.

Unit Learning Outcomes

Articulate a position related to issues raised in the unit's reading and discussion.

Maintain a controlling idea/thesis for a variety of academic genres (CLO #2).

Demonstrate understanding of the principles of and practices for incorporating direct quotations, summaries, and paraphrases into your writing.

Use common formats and conventions (e.g., research, structure, documentation, tone, mechanics) for various genres of academic discourse (CLO #5).
