# Unit 3: Lecture

I. Thesis Statements: A Definition

This term, you are learning how to make an effective academic argument in writing. You have already considering using the tools of rhetorical analysis, close reading, summary, and paraphrase to write. This unit, your focus should shift to writing persuasively.

One very important element of effective academic writing is focus. We can define focus as the presence of a clear central message or controlling idea. For our purposes, a focus can be thought of as a “thesis.” A thesis is an arguable claim—your position on a subject. A thesis is not a statement of fact, a re-statement of an idea you’ve found in research, or a list of other writer's findings. Instead, a thesis can be thought of as a perspective. The best thesis statements are creative and compelling, helping the reader think about a topic in a new, novel way. Here are two sample thesis statements:

The US financial crisis of 2007-2008, while partly caused by both risky borrowing and reckless lending encouraged by the deregulation of financial markets, is best seen as the result of a global trend toward economic disparity.

Mark Edmundson has argued persuasively for a reshaping of college education. In this essay, I will examine how Edmundson's ideas about risk and collaboration in the humanities can be incorporated into online college courses. Such a shift in online education would increase student engagement and satisfaction, and would have a salutary effect on tuition costs.

Note that each of these thesis statements provide something of a road map for the rest of the essay. A careful writer will ensure that the organization of her essay matches the organization of her thesis. For instance, the writer of the first thesis statement would do well to first give a brief overview of the 2007-2008 financial crisis, followed by a summary description of how deregulation and lending contributed to it, before addressing at length her primary subject: the relationship between the local crisis and global economic trends.

According to the  [link: http://writingcenter.unc.edu/handouts/thesis-statements/] University of North Carolina Chapel Hill writing center, a thesis statement does the following:

tells the reader how you will interpret the significance of the subject matter under discussion.

In Easy Writer, Andrea Lunsford emphasizes the importance of the “working thesis.” The addition of the adjectival “working” underscores that a thesis should evolve and improve as you learn more and more about your topic. We will explore this principle in next unit's revision, but, for now, keep an open mind to letting your thesis evolve throughout your drafting process. Many writers report that their final step before turning a paper in is to check that the thesis presented at the beginning of the essay actually matches the content and the conclusion.

II. Limitations of the Five Paragraph Essay

Many students in EN105 and EN106 are most comfortable with a thesis statement that goes something like this: “This paper reviews the three root causes for the mortgage crisis in America.” Many students have been taught this thesis formula in conjunction with an essay format that includes five paragraphs: an introduction, a paragraph each for three supporting ideas, and a conclusion. Thesis statements like the one above identify what topics will be discussed in the body of the paper.

The five paragraph theme has value for certain rhetorical situations—for instance, a timed essay exam. However, college-level writers should recognize the limitations of the five paragraph essay:

A one-sentence thesis statement usually lacks specificity and sophisticated reasoning. Thesis statements can be more than one sentence.

Five paragraphs is rarely sufficient room to explore a comprehensive understanding of any topic.

Very often the three supporting ideas presented in the body of the five paragraph essay do not relate clearly to one another.

Five paragraph themes rarely allow for the writer to explore multiple viewpoints and address counter-arguments.

Finally, the five paragraph essay implies that the form of the essay is moreimportant than its argument. In other word, writers of five paragraphs theme attempt to fit their ideas into a pre-determined shape, rather than discovering and developing the appropriate shape for their ideas as an effective rhetor should.

III. Four Types of “Weak” Theses

As you draft and revise working theses this unit, learn how to recognize and correct what composition researchers David Rosenwasser and Jill Stephen call the “kinds of weak theses.” The following material is taken from their book Writing Analytically (197-217):

Weak Thesis #1: The thesis makes no claim. It does not contain an argument, a critical commentary, and instead only identifies a topic.

Original: This paper will address the characteristics of a good manager.

Revised: The very trait that makes for an effective corporate manager— the drive to succeed—can also make the leader domineering and therefore ineffective.

Weak Thesis #2: The thesis is obviously true or is a statement of fact.

Original: The jeans industry targets its advertisements to appeal to young adults.

Revised: By inventing new terms, such as loose fit and relaxed fit, the jeans industry has attempted to normalize, even glorify, its product for an older and fatter generation.

Weak Thesis #3: The thesis restates conventional wisdom.

Original: An important part of one’s college education is learning to better understand diverse points of view.

Revised: Although an important part of one’s college education is learning to better understand others’ points of view, a persistent danger is that students will simply be required to substitute their teachers’ answer for the ones they grew up believing uncritically.

Weak Thesis #4: The thesis offers personal conviction as the basis for the claim. Rossenwasser and Stephen explain this type of thesis: “Like conventional wisdom, personal likes and dislikes can lead inexperienced writers into knee-jerk reactions of approval or disapproval, often expressed in a moralistic tone” (201).

Original: Although I agree with Jeane Kirkpatrick’s argument that environmentalists and business should work together to ensure the ecological future of the world, and that this cooperation is beneficial for both sides, the indisputable fact is that environmental considerations should always be a part of any decision that is made. An individual, if he looks deeply enough into his soul, knows what is right and what is wrong. The environment should be protected because it is the right thing to do, not because someone is forcing you to do it.

Revised: Although I agree with Jeane Kirkpatrick’s argument that environmentalists and business should work together to ensure the ecological future of the world, her argument undervalues the necessity of pressuring businesses to attend to environmental concerns that may not benefit them in the short run.

Note the common thread in each of these weak thesis statements: none offer anything substantially new to the conversation. As such, each of these weak thesis statements are ineffective rhetoric: after all, why would any reader want to read a restatement of facts she already knows, or listen to a speech with no perspective or "take" on the material?

Equally important, however, is the fact that a weak thesis statement can usually be revised into a precise, arguable, and engaging thesis. Think of the "Burkean Parlor" once again: what can you offer to a conversation that began before you arrived and will continue after you leave?

IV. Entering the Conversation

As a final note: Remember that your thesis statement is your way “in” to an academic conversation. Your thesis provides focus for your essay; shapes and links your supporting arguments; and provides your readers with a preview of your perspective. Finally, you may find that a solid thesis statement will help you compose an essay draft. You can think of a thesis as driving directions: it provides a brief glimpse of how you will go from point A to point B and beyond.
