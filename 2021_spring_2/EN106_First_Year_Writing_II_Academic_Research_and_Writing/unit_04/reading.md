# Unit 4: Reading

Required

Greene & Lidinsky, “From Finding to Evaluating Sources” (Ch. 7).

Chapter 7 clarifies the purpose of academic research as inquiry. In short, academic research means discovering and  generating new  ideas to  offer to  an academic conversation—a concept you will apply in essay #3, due this unit. In this essay, you will re-discover, re-envision (revise) essay #2 in light of what you learn from scholarly inquiry via the Park library’s online databases.

Lunsford, “Conducting Research” (Ch. 11), “Evaluating Sources and Taking Notes” (Ch. 12), and "Arguing Ethically and Persuasively" (Ch. 8).

This unit’s readings from Easy Writer reinforce the purposes of academic research and offer additional tips for successfully creating and answering research questions.

Recommended

 [link: https://www.library.illinois.edu/ugl/howdoi/selectingsources/]  “Selecting the Best Information Source,” University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign

This site provides a clear, concise introduction to the various types of information sources you will encounter as an academic researcher, in EN 106 and beyond. You will learn how to distinguish from primary and secondary sources, books and periodicals, scholarly and non-scholarly (trade) publications, etc.

 [link: http://guides.library.jhu.edu/c.php?g=202581&p=1334997]  “Evaluating Internet Resources,” Johns Hopkins University Sheridan Libraries

In an information-saturated society, we must be active, critical consumers, especially in light of the fact that much content we find via the Web is highly unregulated (and un-reviewed). Johns Hopkins University offers a simple set of criteria to consider when evaluating the reliability of information found on the Internet.
