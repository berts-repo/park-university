# Unit 4: Overview

Conducting Research

Our subject this unit—finding and evaluating sources—will be applied to the writing project due on Sunday, which is an expansion of last unit’s essay. To undertake this expansion, you will extend the conversation you defined last week by finding additional sources to incorporate into your essay.

Further expanding the conversation, we will engage in peer review this unit to explore the value of reader feedback. It is imperative to start the process of peer review as early in the unit as possible, so that you can get (and give) feedback to apply before the revised essay is due on Sunday. For this reason, please note that your initial posts to the “peer review” discussion area are due on Wednesday midnight CST, and you must respond to one peer’s draft no later than Friday midnight CST.

Peer review in a writing class presents challenges to students and teachers alike. In general, we accomplish peer review by sharing drafts of an essay with others, in order to learn how our peers regard our written work. For some students, the process feels like an invasion of privacy. There is merit to this view; critique can be uncomfortable. However, respecting the work of others in this course, sincerely weighing another’s ideas, and responding to those ideas as “real” readers are at the heart of our work this unit.

This unit's peer review is not about proofreading each other’s work. (We will explore the value of that kind of feedback in Unit Seven.) Your primary role in this unit's peer review is to offer thoughtful, careful and deliberate feedback to your classmates. At the same time, you will learn what your classmates think of your work before you engage in deep revision, and you will be able to see how your peers have approached the assignment.

While your instructor will not respond to each post in the peer review thread, your instructor will observe the discussion—noting repeated themes and issues that emerge from your review of each other’s work and offering general input and guidance. Your instructor will also offer constructive suggestions for how you could begin your research process for this unit's assignment. Look for those suggestions in the Gradebook somewhere around the middle of this unit.

Unit Learning Outcomes

Read and respond meaningfully to the writing of another student in the class.

Pose provocative questions that will stir issues the writer might consider for deep revision.

Transform your own essay through a process of deep revision supported by peer and instructor comments.

Apply strategies for developing academic arguments across the disciplines, including conducting research and incorporating culturally diverse perspectives (CLO #3).

Consider the rhetorical situations faced by academic writers to respond appropriately in both writing and research (CLO #4).
