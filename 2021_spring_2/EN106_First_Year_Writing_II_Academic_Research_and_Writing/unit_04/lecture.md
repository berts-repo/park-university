# Unit 4: Lecture

What is research?

Academic writing is sometimes thought of as the result of research. But what is research, exactly? 

For the purposes of this unit, think of research as answering a question. Academic research starts with a question or inquiry. (Remember the habits of mind?) Research, then, is the process of answering that question, using the tools and methods you have at your disposal.

As you have read, discussed, and written about articles from our textbook, you have probably come up with many questions. (Most likely, these questions arose in discussions.) Your job in this unit will be to select a research question or two, and do what you can to try to answer that question, using the tools you have at your disposal.

For EN106 students, the research tools you have are probably straightforward: library databases, web search engines, perhaps relevant textbooks or other materials. As you move ahead as a student and scholar, you will learn specific research methods for your major discipline. But, for now, you should be thinking of precise questions that you can answer through finding credible sources. (You will start during this unit's discussion.)

 

What is peer review?

Also this unit, you will participate in peer review.

Peer review is not simply a classroom exercise—it is at the heart of all professional work of the academy, in all disciplines. Before moving on, please read  [link: http://guides.lib.jjay.cuny.edu/content.php?pid=209679&amp;sid=1746812] this web page about how peer review functions in the academy. Be sure to watch the two short videos!

It may bring you some satisfaction to know that when your professors submit scholarly writing for publication, their work is never automatically accepted (at least, not by a reputable scholarly journal). Instead, each manuscript is sent to peer reviewer, typically three. These reviewers determine if the manuscript will make a significant contribution to the field. The reviewers communicate their assessment to an editor, who then makes the determination about acceptance for publication. If a piece is accepted, a letter is sent to the writer, along with a list of required revisions. In this process, the focus is not on grammar and mechanics; instead, it’s on the real "meat" of what makes a piece of writing effective— the quality of the writer’s thesis, the strength of the supporting arguments; the extent to which the writer shows sensitivity to the audience; the organization and coherence of the writing; and the attention given to tone, documentation of sources, and proofreading.

Thus, revision involves a careful, concentrated effort toward re-thinking, re-arranging, literally re-envisioning your writing. Revision is distinctive from what we refer to as “editing” because it involves more than surface-level touch-ups. Revision asks you to re-make your essay with improved and amplified rhetorical effects, more coherent organization, and attention to the support and development in your various claims, evidence and examples.

The peer review process we will employ this unit emphasizes interacting with other readers to develop perspective. Rather than writing in isolation, peer review reminds us that writing and rhetoric take place in context. Through conversational exchanges with peers, we might see broader possibilities for our work. Sharing your writing and responses with real, interested readers will challenge you and reward you. You might consider taking notes on this unit's peer review activities, and including those notes in the reflective component of your portfolio, due in Unit Seven.

For you as a reviewer (one who will be reading and responding to the essays written by others in the class), this means that you should use tremendous care in formulating responses. Peer response at this stage in the development of an essay typically does not focus on the minutiae of grammar and syntax. Focusing on such issues in your response won't help the writer revise the essay. Also, recommending changes that are too big can be unhelpful. It's impractical, given our clear time constraints, that anyone will be able to completely rewrite the essay. So recommending changes that are too small (pointing out every typo, for instance) or too large (recommending a completely different topic) might be unhelpful at this stage. Instead, try to focus on asking questions and making suggestions that will help the writer have an improved sense of the text. This is usually accomplished by asking questions (“What did you mean by...”) or making suggestions (“Your strongest point is in paragraph six; you should consider restating that point nearer to the beginning of the essay.”). Because our attention this unit is focused primarily on revising your own work and responding to the writing done by others in the class, you should divide your time accordingly.

Finally: peer review is a difficult skill to learn, but it can be learned -- and you can learn it! The grade emphasis on peer review (15% of your grade in the course) should tell you that you should treat peer review as seriously as any other formal writing assignment.
