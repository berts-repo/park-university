Assignment Descriptions

Each assignment for this class will have detailed instructions, including a grading rubric that your instructor will use to evaluate your work. These descriptions will provide you a general overview of the assignments you will complete.

Discussions

Each unit, you will take part in class discussion. Why discussions? Because pedagogical research suggests that discussions are one of the best ways to learn. Discussions allow you to try out new concepts, test new ideas, invent research questions, and debate important questions. Your work in discussions will lead to some of your most important learning this term. All discussions will take place in an asynchronous, threaded discussion format. You will be asked to make an initial post that responds to a question or prompt, and then engage in follow-up discussion. Specific details for each discussion's requirements are contained within the discussion prompt. Initial posts to each discussion are due each Thursday by noon Central time. Follow-up discussion is due each Sunday by 11:59 pm Central time -- although most discussions should take place earlier in the unit.

Peer Review

In units 3, 4, and 6, you will engage in peer review of your writing. What that means is that you will trade drafts with one or two classmates and provide each other with constructive feedback. Peer review is an important academic activity because it allows writers and readers to engage in discussion that will lead ultimately to growth. Learning to be a good peer reviewer is a crucial academic skill – one that will serve you well as you move along to upper-division coursework. 

Specific instructions for each peer review will vary. Peer reviews will ask you to post a draft early in the unit (usually by Wednesday) and provide a written response to assigned classmates, using a question guide. (Responses are usually due by Friday.) Specific instructions are provided in each peer review assignment.

 

Essays

You will complete five Essay assignments in this course. Essay #1, due in Unit 2, will ask you to identify and evaluate the rhetorical strategies used by an author in our textbook. In Essay #2, you will define an academic "conversation," by connecting two articles in our textbook. Essay #3 is a research report, in which you will pose a research question and find credible sources that help you answer that question, summing up your findings in a brief report. In Essay #4, you will enter a new academic conversation -- a conversation begun by assigned readings from our textbook. Essay #5 is a revision/expansion project that will ask you to enhance and expand Essay #4.

Each Essay will be graded with a rubric derived from these criteria: focus, development, organization, and mechanics. Some of the criteria are more focused on form, and some on content. Writers must have both in mind as they write; as you will discover in this course, form and content are intrinsically linked.

Keep in mind that these criteria are general; close analysis of the particular writing task (as described in the assignment) and the specific audience will help you determine more precisely the form and content appropriate for each assignment.

Focus: A successful piece of writing will stay focused on the controlling idea, message, or thesis it is trying to convey. Without focus, writing seems disjointed, uncentered, and lacking clarity. Here I will assess how closely your writing focuses on a complex idea or thesis throughout. Do you wander from topic to topic? Is it clear what you are arguing? Do you attend to your central thesis/message throughout the assignment?

Development: Evidence is key to persuading your audience to adopt your position. Successful writing  presents enough evidence to support the claims the writer is making. Without evidence, your claims will seem ungrounded and unconvincing. Do you have evidence and support for each of your claims? Is the evidence related to the claims that are made? Are the details specific or abstract? Is the evidence from relevant and reliable sources?

A second evaluative criteria for academic writing is the complexity of the content. Complexity means that the writer moves beyond summary, and beyond a surface analysis of the material to offer a new perspective on the subject. A writer might raise significant questions about a topic or reading, or make connections between and among varied texts. Less complex writing will stay on the surface by remaining summary or by pointing out only the obvious.

Organization: Academic writing is organized. Each of your major writing assignments should have a clear beginning, middle, and end, with effective and creative transitions from idea to idea and from paragraph to paragraph. Without a clear structure, your writing will lack coherence, your ideas will seem to wander from point to point, and each section will  seem disconnected to the next. How is each point related to the next? Are the connections clear from paragraph to paragraph? Does the piece clearly develop an idea from beginning to end, persuasively ordering the main points?

Mechanics: “Mechanics” is used broadly here to encompass everything from word choice, sentence variety, and grammatical correctness to the accurate citation of sources using standard academic documentation guidelines, such as those compiled by  the  Modern  Language  Association (MLA) or the American Psychological Association (APA). Successful academic writing uses complex and effective sentences with sophisticated word choice, with few, if any, significant grammatical or punctuation problems. Academic writing with a low level of linguistic maturity might repeat simple sentence structures and/or pose challenges to the reader due to grammatical and mechanical problems. Does the writer use only one kind of sentence? Has the writing been proofread to make sure all typos, grammatical and mechanical errors are eliminated? Are the ideas conveyed in sophisticated and interesting language? Has the writer acknowledged—with both in-text and end-text citations—all words and ideas gained from research?

 

The weight of each category will change depending on the assignment. In general, your earlier assignments will give more weight to focus, while the later assignments give more weight to development and mechanics. This mirrors the ongoing work you will do in discussion and peer review. Your instructor will also provide you with personalized responses to each of your major writing assignments. Each assignment will contain a detailed grading rubric, and your instructor may provide additional details on their grading philosophy in an Announcement.

No sense of introduction, body, and conclusion. There might be a clear middle, beginning and end with the content, but paragraph breaks don’t make sense or are missing. Overall organization of points might be confusing or jumpy.

The writing is generally clear, but sentence structure may be simplistic and/or slightly repetitive. There are several grammar error patterns but nothing that seriously interferes with reading, perhaps a few comma splices and fragments. Word choice might be confusing in one or two places. Documentation is missing in some areas or incorrectly applied.

There are several grammar patterns that seriously inhibit understanding, perhaps a pattern of fragments or run-ons throughout. Wording and sentence structure are confused to the point where they interfere with the reader’s understanding. Documentation is incorrect or absent.

 

Writing Portfolio (Core Assessment)

Your major goal this term is to create a portfolio of your best writing from the class. This Writing Portfolio will be turned in at the end of Unit 7, and represents the largest percentage of your grade in the course.

To complete the Writing Portfolio, you will choose your two best major writing assignments from this class and revise them further, using feedback from your instructor and your classmates to guide your work. You will also compile artifacts of your writing process, to help illustrate how you created your writing assignments. Finally, you will compose a detailed reflection that describes your writing process and identifies specific lessons about writing you learned this term.

The reflective essay should be an 800-1200 word essay that examines your writing in light of our core learning outcomes: Process, Focus, Development, Rhetorical Strategies, and Conventions. You may choose any number of approaches to this assignment. For instance, you can introduce your reader to any of the following:

evidence of ways you understand your writing to have improved this term, either by identifying your habits and processes of writing or by examining specific examples from the essays included in your portfolio

accounts of struggles or challenges this term, specific to academic writing consideration of the rhetorical contexts of academic writing, using the terminology you explored in Units One and Two

descriptions of your next steps as an academic writer analysis of the similarities and differences between academic writing and the sort of popular discourse in magazines, newspapers, and popular web sites

examples of particular paragraphs that you find to be strong or representative of your best work (in the body of the portfolio, you might put these passages in bold if you refer to them in the introduction)

examples of particular paragraphs that you find to be weak or representative of frustrations you encountered related to writing or certain ideas

meditations on the most important writing skills you will bring forward to your upcoming academic coursework

descriptions of changed attitudes or levels of confidence related to your writing (for better or for worse)

analysis of one or more particular learning outcomes and how it is represented in the portfolio at large

 

Your Writing Portfolio serves as the Core Assessment of your learning for English 105. That means two things: first, that the Writing Portfolio should show off everything you've learned this semester, demonstrating how you have achieved the Core Learning Outcomes for EN105. Second, your performance on the Writing Portfolio may be analyzed by the faculty in Department of English to make decisions about curriculum, learning outcomes, student success, and adjustments to our teaching strategies.

Your writing portfolio will be graded using the rubric found at the end of your syllabus, also reproduced here. Note that this rubric is similar, but not identical to the rubric I will use to assess your essays.

Grading and Communication

Your instructor will be accessible throughout the course in the virtual Office and via email.

Your instructor will also communicate with you via the Gradebook and in the commentary provided on each graded item of the course. Consistent with Park University policies, your Discussion and Peer Review posts will be graded within 48 hours of the Sunday due date. Your Essays will be graded within six days of the due date.  

In our unit Discussion threads, your instructor will serve as an active colleague in conversation, posing questions, observing connections, and registering my own opinions. Your instructor will check the discussions daily, but may not post daily, because students need to be involved and initiate the dialogue. 

Your instructor's role in Peer Review threads is to note recurrent themes and issues and over broad advice applicable to all writers. If you have specific questions about your coursework, please do not hesitate to contact your instructor via the virtual Office or via email.
