# Unit 5: Lecture

Strategies for Organizing

One decision you will have to make for this unit's essay will be about organization. How can you organize your essay to most effectively support your thesis? 

This lecture focuses in on issues related to effectively organizing the body paragraphs of your essays—some compositionists call this “paragraphing.”

Consider the following strategies for critically analyzing the effectiveness of an essay’s organization:

Reverse outlining: Write a sentence that summarizes the point of each paragraph of your essay. Do points follow logically and necessarily? Can you explain why they are in the order they are? Should some parts be left out? Should some new parts be added?

Paragraph sorting: Cut your paper into separate paragraphs and jumble them up.

Number the jumbled paragraphs (be careful to number them in their jumbled order, not their original order). Can you put the paragraphs back into order? Could you change the order of the paragraphs? Are there better orders than the one you chose in your first draft? Do some paragraphs need to be divided into two? Do any need to be combined into one?

Now, enlist another reader. As your reader to put your jumbled paragraphs into order. Did that person put them into the same order you did? Ask the person to tell you which paragraphs were easy to order and which seemed more loosely connected to the controlling idea or to surrounding paragraphs.

Look at the paragraphs your reader put in a different place than your original. Would the paragraphs work better, follow more logically, in that new order?

Look also at the paragraphs your reader had trouble putting into order. 

Do those paragraphs belong somewhere other than their original order? Could they be connected more tightly to the controlling idea? to the surrounding paragraphs? Do those paragraphs belong in the paper at all; do they develop your controlling idea, or might they be tangents better left out?

Compare the easily placed paragraphs to the difficult ones. What did you do in the easy ones that more clearly signaled your organization? Could you do more of that in the difficult paragraphs?

Paragraph analysis: Examine the beginning of each paragraph. What bridge do you build to show the logical connection of this paragraph to the one before it? Can you underline transitional words or phrases or, better yet, a sentence that explains the logical move you are making, repeated key words or sentence structures, or pronouns? If not, see if you can improve the clarity of the transition you are asking the reader to make from the one paragraph to the next.

Put brackets around every quotation you use in your draft. Then consider these questions: Do you need the full quotation, or could you use a partial quotation or paraphrase it? If you need the full quotation, have you  introduced it with the author’s name and credentials (in a signal phrase) or with another preview-type statement? Have you stated the significance of that quotation for your own ideas and argument?

Select a paragraph that might be choppy or not cohere especially well. Examine it sentence by sentence, looking for how you connect each sentence to the topics and ideas of the preceding sentence. Underline repeated key words, pronouns, and transitional words and phrases. Where do you have sentences with no explicit connection to the preceding sentences? Does the sentence belong there? If so, how could you help your reader follow you from one sentence to the next? Would repeating key words, pronouns, or sentence structures help? Would adding a transitional word, phrase, or even whole idea make the logical connection clearer? Go on to examine all your other paragraphs similarly.
