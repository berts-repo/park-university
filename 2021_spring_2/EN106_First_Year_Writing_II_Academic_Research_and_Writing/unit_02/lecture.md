# Unit 2: Lecture

I. Academic Writing as a Conversation

As we learned last unit from Greene and Lidinsky, academic writers “see writing as a conversation”:

[A] habit of mind at the heart of academic writing is the understanding that ideas always build on and respond to other ideas, just as they do in the best kinds of conversations. . . . You are responding to something someone else has written (or said) and are writing back in anticipation of future responses. Academic writing [places] a high value on the belief that good, thoughtful ideas come from conversations with others. . . (8)

Here, Greene and Lidinsky echo the ideas of literary theorist Kenneth Burke. In analyzing what makes enduring literature—well…endure—Burke developed a metaphor of communication now referred to as the Burkean Parlor. The Burkean Parlor has remained a useful conceptual frame for writers, a frame that emphasizes the importance of research and writing as ongoing dialogue.

Burke’s “Parlor” metaphor, introduced in his The Philosophy of Literary Form, illustrates how any form of communication is a rejoinder in a larger dialogue:

Imagine that you enter a parlor. You come late. When you arrive, others have long preceded you, and they are engaged in a heated discussion, a discussion too heated for them to pause and tell you exactly what it is about. In fact, the discussion had already begun long before any of them got there, so that no one present is qualified to retrace for you all the steps that had gone before. You listen for a while, until you decide that you have caught the tenor of the argument; then you put in your oar. Someone answers; you answer him; another comes to your defense; another aligns himself against you, to either the embarrassment or gratification of your opponent, depending upon the quality of your ally's assistance. However, the discussion is interminable. The hour grows late, you must depart. And you do depart, with the discussion still vigorously in progress. (110-11)

The principles of rhetoric and the practices of rhetorical sensitivity we discussed last unit rely on audience awareness; in order to be a good rhetor, you must be able to “catch the tenor” of a conversation-in-progress, and you must be able to “put in your oar”—that is, you must  be  able to  make a  contribution that will  continue the  dialogue long  past  your departure.

II. Conversing with Texts: Understanding and Avoiding Plagiarism

This unit’s essay assignment asks you to take the first step in the process of entering an academic conversation, to “catch the tenor” of a conversation. In this case, it will be the conversation suggested by an article assigned from our textbook.

In order to do this, you must engage in a close reading of the article to determine what you consider the main argument, or thesis. As you analyze the argument, you will need to integrate direct quotations, summaries, and paraphrases. These should be active, not passive, endeavors. Selecting material from a text and determining how to fairly represent it in your writing is part of approaching reading as a conversation. But this work is difficult, especially when we consider standards for academic honesty, particularly plagiarism.

Plagiarism is the intentional or unintentional use of someone else’s ideas or words without explicit or accurate reference. When writers fail to enclose direct quotations in quotation marks (intentionally or unintentional), or when writers fail to make clear reference to the contributions of others, they have plagiarized. Three of the common reasons that student writers are accused of plagiarism are the following:

Failure to enclose direct quotations in quotation marks

Failure to indicate exactly which ideas come from which source

Taking words, phrases, sentences, or paragraphs from other writers without any form of attribution

In this course, we won't need to worry about the third bullet: that is stealing, plain and simple, and will likely result in an academic dishonesty report, as outlined in the syllabus. But the first two bullets sometimes involve making a judgment call about what needs a citation and what does not. Part of the way you can learn to judge this is to pay close attention to the published writing you read—how do published writers document their sources? I will also help you to make appropriate decisions this term. If you have a question about documentation, please ask in the Instructor's Office tab. No question is too small!

Different groups of writers cite things differently. In fact, disciplinary communities have established relatively standard codes and formats to guide writers in acknowledging their sources. One of your rhetorical challenges as you move forward in college will be to determine which disciplinary standards your readers expect you to follow. For instance, in the humanities, we usually use guidelines developed by the Modern Language Association (MLA). Your own major or degree program may tend to follow documentation guidelines developed the American Psychological Association (APA), the editors of the University of Chicago Press (Chicago), the Council of Science Editors (CSE), or some other organization. Your Easy Writer handbook can be your chief resource for accurately documenting sources via MLA, APA, and many of the other major style guides.

All documentation systems acknowledge sources in two ways—in the body of the text (in- text citations) and at the conclusion of the text (end-text citations, sometimes called "Works Cited," "References," or "Bibliography"). You no doubt noticed some in-text citations above, in reference to Greene and Lidinsky’s work and the ideas of Burke. Notice that the author and page number of the reference were provided. If there were a bibliography or “Works Cited” for this online course, it would include the full publication information for both texts.

For the purposes of this class, I ask that you think about documentation rhetorically. Consider the audience, purpose, and context that govern your rhetoric in this class. This means that I am very familiar with MLA guidelines for citation and formatting, and it means that MLA guidelines were developed with writers like you in mind—that is, writers who are engaged in the work of the humanities. So, you might decide that MLA guidelines are best for your work in this class.

However, you may have already begun coursework in a major that expects you to follow APA, Chicago, or CSE guidelines. You may wish to practice your use of your discipline's particular  conventions.  If  you  do  have  a  clear  reason  to  follow  formatting  and documentation guidelines other than MLA, please note in an email or your first essay which style guidelines you will be following this term, and why.

III. Conversing with Texts: Integrating Direct Quotations

Your reading this unit in Greene and Lidinsky will provide principles and strategies for incorporating direct quotations into your own writing, and paraphrasing and summarizing the writing of others.

Key to the use of direct quotations is discretion. An essay filled with direct quotations can often obscure or overwhelm the writer’s own ideas and interpretations. So, when you choose to integrate a direction quotation, ask yourself if the quotation really warrants being reproduced, or if the ideas could be represented in your own words (with, of course, an in- text citation). We call this paraphrase, and you will learn how to create a good paraphrase in your reading from Greene and Lidinsky this unit.

No matter if you choose to quote, paraphrase, or summarize, please ensure that you have identified the source of your reference. The simplest and best way to do this is to use what writing teachers call "signal phrases." Signal phrases “signal” to the reader that the upcoming information is taken from an outside source. At their most basic, signal phrases can take the following form:

Author's Name + Verb

A few examples:

Lunsford writes…

Greene and Lidinsky suggest…

Edmundson argues…

However, skillful signal phrases are used to not only introduce a quotation, but also to integrate it into the rest of the essay. To that end,  [link: http://www.sonoma.edu/writingcenter/resources/handouts/plagiarism.pdf] Sonoma University provides the following advice and examples:

Use signals—entire sentences or phrases—to introduce quotations. This signal sentence summarizes and previews the direct quotation:

Body modification appears to be universal among human species, perhaps due to the nature of the human body itself. Scholars consider humans “the only animals which can consciously and deliberately change their appearance according to their own whims” (Greer 161).

This signal phrase, which is paired with the transitional phrase “for instance,” lets the reader know how the quoted material fits in.

Many more mundane elements of our social life can be thought of as body modification rituals. For instance, as Germaine Greer argues, “Fashion, because it is beyond logic, is deeply revealing” (162).

Verbs that will be helpful to you in crafting signal phrases include:

acknowledges

agrees

asserts

claims

concludes

concurs

describes

disagrees

emphasizes

observes

offers

remarks

replies

responds

suggests

writes

 

As you noted in the above examples, and learned about in the Purdue University guides, MLA format calls for the in-text citation to include the author’s last name and page number (no comma in between). If the author is mentioned in the signal phrase, however, the writer need only put the page number in parentheses. For online sources, which do not have “page numbers” per se, use the paragraph number, if given.

APA guidelines require that the year of publication be included any time the name of the author or authors appears, as well as a page number. Again, if the author and date are mentioned in a signal phrase, a writer needs to provide only a page number in parentheses (preceded by a p.). For instance, APA versions of the examples above would look like this:

Body modification appears to be universal among human species, perhaps due to the nature of the human body itself. Scholars consider humans “the only animals which can consciously and deliberately change their appearance according to their own whims” (Greer, 1990, p. 161).

Many more mundane elements of our social life can be thought of as body modification rituals. For instance, as Germaine Greer (1990) argues, “Fashion, because it is beyond logic, is deeply revealing” (p. 162).

In addition to signaling, skillful academic writers tend to follow a direct quotation with a brief summary, example, illustration, or connection. Providing a clear indication of how the source material fits into your argument or analysis will help your essay from feeling "choppy" or disjointed.
