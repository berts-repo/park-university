# Unit 2: Overview

Reading Like An Academic Writer

As you begin work for class this unit, consider this quotation from Greene and Lidinsky: “Reading for class and then writing an essay might seem to be separate tasks, but reading is actually the first step in the writing process.”

This unit, you will practice your skills of close reading, direct quotation, and paraphrase to write your first essay, a summary and analysis of an article from our textbook. (Check with your instructor for which articles you may choose for this assignment.) This first essay assignment is intended to underscore the principle that successfully entering academic conversations starts with careful observation from the sidelines.

This unit’s lecture will explore Greene and Lidinsky’s use of the academic writing-as-conversation metaphor, with the help of literary and language theorist Kenneth Burke. Burke explains that in order to successfully enter an academic conversation, one must first “catch the tenor” of that conversation; in other words, one must know who is speaking, what central claim is being made, how it is being made, to what end, and so on. (These questions might remind you of the rhetorical analysis models from last unit – in fact, you will find those models useful as you begin work on your essay this unit.)

Unit Learning Outcomes

Apply the “habits of mind” of academic writers to perform critical, close reading of a text.

Demonstrate understanding of the principles of and practices for incorporating direct quotations, summaries, and paraphrases into your writing.

Apply writing processes, collaborative strategies, and effective academic research practices to participate in academic discourse (CLO #1).

Cite sources in-text using MLA format guidelines.

Use common formats and conventions (e.g., research, structure, documentation, tone, mechanics) for various genres of academic discourse (CLO #5).
