# Unit 3: Cell Structure

Cells 
Cells are the smallest units that exhibit the properties of life. Because they are so small, no one actually saw them until microscopes were invented in the mid-1600s. Robert Hooke first described cells in 1665 when he examined a thin slice of plant tissue called cork. He saw an array of tiny, empty (because the cells were dead) compartments. He called them cellulae which is Latin for small rooms. However people did not recognize the importance of cells. In 1838, Matthias Schleiden stated that all plants were made of cells and in 1839 Theodor Schwann reported that all animals are also made up of cells. These discoveries led to the cell theory.

1.       All organisms are made of one or more cells. 
2.       Cells are the smallest living things. 
3.       Cells come from preexisting cells.

Overview

All cells are surrounded by a membrane ( plasma membrane ) that controls the permeability of the cell. A semifluid matrix fills the interior of the cell. Scattered within the matrix ( cytoplasm ) are organelles which perform various functions.

Cell membrane

All cells are enclosed by a very thin semi-permeable membrane which has many small pores (holes). Through the membrane, many nutrients important for cell function can enter, while the waste products can be excreted. The cell membrane is not only permeable to nutrients necessary for cell function, but also to damaging toxic materials (such as mercury and lead). 

Fluid mosaic model :  Because of the chemical characteristics of phospholipids , the very center of the membrane is hydrophobic while the external surfaces are hydrophilic . This lipid bilayer is virtually impermeable to large molecules, relatively impermeable to molecules as small as charged ions, and very permeable to lipid soluble low weight molecules.    Molecules that can diffuse through the membrane do so at differing rates depending on whether they are soluble in the hydrophobic middle.  All cell membranes have protein receptors, transporters, and enzymes. Also within the cell membrane are proteins for adhesion, communication and recognition.

Some proteins are entirely outside of the membrane, but are bound to it by weak molecular forces. Other proteins are embedded within the membrane.  As is illustrated in the diagram below, many of them extend from one side of the membrane to the other.  These proteins can function to transport ions and molecules across the membrane that could ordinarily not cross.  Some of the proteins act as receptors to gain information from the extracellular fluid and relay this into the cell (act as antennae of the cell).

Cell Types 

There are two major kinds of cells: prokaryotes and eukaryotes . It is important to remember that all cells have cell membranes, even if they have a cell wall. Prokaryotes do not have membrane-bound organelles or a nucleus. You may commonly know these as bacteria. Prokaryotes are the simplest cellular organisms. Outside of almost all bacteria is a cell wall which provides the structure for the cell. Prokaryotes have an area where DNA is concentrated called the nucleoid region . They have one chromosome in the form of a circle. Prokaryotes do have small structures called ribosomes , where proteins are made but ribosomes do not have a membrane boundary. Eukaryotes also have ribosomes but they also have an internal skeleton of proteins (cytoskeleton) and contain organelles (specialized structures within which particular cell processes occur).

Nucleus  

The nucleus is surrounded by a double membrane (nuclear envelope) and contains chromosomes (made of DNA)  [link: http://www.rothamsted.ac.uk/] . It is the control center of the cell and directs protein synthesis and cell reproduction. The nuclear envelope is also a phospholipid bilayer, functioning in many ways like the cell membrane, and has pores which allows things to move in and out of the nucleus.

Endomembrane system (think of this system as the packaging and shipping division)

Endoplasmic reticulum (ER) is formed by a convoluted sheet of membrane arising from the nuclear envelope. It forms a passageway through the cell. There are two kinds of ER. Close to the nucleus, it is covered by ribosomes giving it a rough appearance; so it is called rough endoplasmic reticulum (rER).  At other points farther from the nucleus, it has no ribosomes and is called smooth endoplasmic reticulum (sER). Other membrane bound organelles

Lysosomes contains enzymes for digestion of various molecules -- "recycling center"

Mitochondria -- surrounded by two membranes; inner one has many folds "cristae"-- energy powerhouse of the cell

Chloroplasts -- also surrounded by two membranes; site of photosynthesis in plants and algae

Microtubule organelles

Cytoskeleton -- network of protein fibers which supports the shape of the cell and anchors organelles. Three different kinds of protein fibers, microfilaments, microtubules, and intermediate filaments (most stable) that function in many different one.

Centrioles -- associated with the nucleus of animal cells that aid in the organization and construction of the cytoskeleton as well as cell division.

Nonmembraneous organelles

Nucleolus --responsible for production of ribosomes and found inside the nucleus

Ribosomes --small, complex assemblies of protein and RNA, may be bound to endoplasmic reticulum, site of protein synthesis

There are some differences between plant and animal cells.  These are related to their functions.  Plant cells have a central vacuole and cell walls that help provide the structure of the plant.  They have chloroplasts in addition to the other membrane bound organelles, but do not have centrioles.

Multicellularity

In the process of building a multicellular organism, cells are attached to each other in specific ways in the process of building tissues and organs. Cells express both informational and junction molecules on their surface, so that cells recognize and adhere to similar cells.

Cell-cell junctions can be classified into three functional types

Occluding or tight junctions : function to seal cells together, these are especially important in the intestine, bladder, and glands; prevent fluid movement between the cells.

Adhesive junctions or desmosomes : mechanically attach cells to one another. These act to hold cells in fixed position and to provide stability. These are abundant in areas where tissues need to withstand mechanical stress.

Communicating or gap junctions : allow direct passage of chemical or electrical signals from cell to cell. These junctions act as direct connections between the cytoplasm of adjoining cells and can allow exchange of small molecules as signals.

Movement of materials across the cell membrane

For cells to survive, food, water and other materials must travel into the cell, and waste materials have to be eliminated.  Because the cell membrane is semipermeable , it allows some things to cross but not others.  All of this occurs in one of three ways: water & other substances diffuse through the membrane; food particles & sometimes liquids are engulfed by the membrane folding around them; or proteins in the membrane act as doors that admit certain molecules only.

Diffusion is the net movement of molecules down a concentration gradient toward areas of lower concentration as a result of random movement. (A prime illustration of this is when you open a bottle of ammonia in the corner of a room and soon the odor spreads to the entire room.) Eventually the molecules will reach a state of equilibrium (no net movement of molecules).

Osmosis is a very special case in that it is the diffusion of water across a semipermeable membrane.

Facilitated diffusion is the transport of molecules across a membrane by a carrier protein in the direction of lowest concentration.

Active transport is the movement of a particle across a membrane by a protein carrier to a region of higher concentration at an energy cost.   Passive transport is the diffusion of a particle across a cell membrane, through a transport protein without the expenditure of energy.

Endocytosis is the process in which the plasma membrane extends around and engulfs a particle bringing it into the cell. The reverse of endocytosis is exocytosis , the discharge of material from vesicles (sacs) at the cell membrane.

In biology, a hypotonic cell environment is one with a lower concentration of solutes (solids) than the cytoplasm. In a hypotonic environment, osmosis causes water to flow into the cell. Plants thrive in hypotonic environments because their cells have rigid cell walls that prevent bursting. In fact, the pressure of the cytoplasm against the cell wall keeps the plant from wilting and losing its shape.

On the other hand, cells without cell walls will swell and, if the environment is sufficiently hypotonic, burst and die or lyse. If the cell and its environment have equal concentrations of solutes, they are isotonic .  I am sure that you have heard, in a medical situation, an example of someone receiving an isotonic saline solution.  This is because if the solution being given is hypertonic or hypotonic, the cells would be stressed.

A hypertonic environment has a higher concentration of solutes than the cytoplasm.  A good example of this is the ocean.  In this case water would tend to move out of the cell into the environment and the cells would shrivel and collapse.
