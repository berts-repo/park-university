What is Science?

In today's world it is becoming essential to have "biological literacy".  What does this mean?  It is almost impossible to pick up a newspaper, magazine, or listen to radio and television, without hearing something about how science is affecting our world. Biological literacy is the ability to (1)use the scientific method to think creatively about issues that have a biological component, (2) communicate these thoughts and ideas to others, and (3) be able to use these ideas in our decision making.  

Biological issues--including global warming and stem cell research, among others--have become important in political campaigns.  We are also constantly bombarded by claims from various manufacturers whose products have too-good-to-be-true claims. 

Science is the concerted human effort to understand how the natural world works and is done through observation of natural phenomena, and/or through experimentation.  You can’t think "science" without thinking about the scientific method.  But what is the scientific method? The "Scientific Method" is a systematic approach to advancing knowledge. While there are differences among scientists and among disciplines concerning the details of the method basically the steps are

Observation & description

Formation of hypothesis to explain phenomena; Hypothesis = testable explanation of observations

Use hypothesis to create a testable prediction

Test prediction 

Analysis and repeat 3 & 4 as needed

This course is specifically about the basic biological concepts.  But what is biology?  Most everyone realizes that biology is the study of life, but it also includes the places and ways that things live and even die.   

The levels of organization that apply to living things start with the very smallest and simplest and get more complicated and larger as they progress.  We would normally start our study with the simplest components of matter (chemistry). These levels are illustrated by the list below:

Atom

Molecule including macromolecules like DNA

Organelles

Cells

Tissues

Organs

Organ systems

Organisms

Populations--a group of one species in a certain area (one specific kind of organism--such as all the Persian cats living in your town)

Communities--a group of populations in a certain area (this might be the population of Persian cats, of humans, of dogs, and trees in your town)

Ecosystem--a group of communities and the associated abiotic factors

Living organisms have several properties or characteristics in common. There are many different ways in which these are grouped; however, the characteristics that we will use for this course are listed below.

Characteristics of Life

Cellular organization – one or more cells

Metabolism – acquire and use energy

Homeostasis – makes controlled responses to keep the body in balance

Growth and reproduction

Heredity – DNA and genetics

Irritability – the ability to react to stimulus

Populations are important to ecologists. In fact, some of ecology's most interesting and challenging questions center on populations. For example: Deer are wonderful and exciting animals to have around. But, how can we keep them from becoming pests in suburban America? 

The story of ecology does not stop with populations. Populations are components of ecosystems. Within any ecosystem, populations interact with each other and their surroundings, both biological and physical. One or a few populations may dominate a particular ecosystem while other populations may be limited in number and remain inconspicuous.

Ecosystems are highly complex as well as very organized, made up of all of the organisms as well as all of the physical conditions and processes in a particular area. Plants trap energy from the sun and tap into nutrients in the soil, making both available to other organisms. Nutrients and energy are passed from organism to organism as plant eaters eat the plants and are in turn eaten by meat eaters. Eventually, nutrients are returned to the soil, to be recycled by other organisms

It is important that you understand how protons, electrons, and neutrons are arranged into atoms and ions.  It is the arrangement of these particles that gives an element it's particular characteristics as well as how it interacts with other elements to form molecules and compounds.  This interaction is the result of chemical bonds.  One of the most important molecules in both our bodies and our environment is water.  Because of the bonds formed, water has many important characteristics.

After gaining some basic background on chemistry, we will examine the four categories of organic macromolecules that are so very important to life.  These macromolecules or polymers are made up of smaller units called monomers in much the same manner as a chain has individual links.  We will also discuss the purposes of each of these different categories and why the particular structures are so important.

An essential atom for life is carbon, which can assemble into very large molecules.  Much of the chemistry that goes on in organisms depends on the actions of one category of these macromolecules called proteins.
