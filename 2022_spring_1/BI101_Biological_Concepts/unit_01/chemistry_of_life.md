Chemistry of Life

The collection of chemicals that make up our body has increased in value over the last 30 years from approximately $.89 to today’s $4.50 (Kennedy, 2009).  Obviously we are more than our chemical makeup, however, the chemicals in our body and their levels are important in the maintenance of life. Of course, this value is just for the chemicals and skin. 

If you were to set a price for your organs, such as heart or kidney, the price would probably be between 50 and 100 thousand dollars.  (Kennedy, P. 2009. Your Body's Worth:Asset or Liability. AC Associated Content: Health & Wellness.  Accessed July 12, 2009.)

To understand biology we need a little knowledge of chemistry. Taking this further we know that an understanding of chemistry requires a little knowledge of mathematics, but don’t worry, it is not important to delve deeply into mathematics and its applications to understand the basic principles of biology. Modern biology overlaps with chemistry in the many explanations of cellular processes at the molecular level.

For our purposes, the simplest form of a pure substance that retains all of the properties of that substance is an atom. The simplest form of an element is also an atom. An element is made up of only one type of atom. 

For instance, gold is an element. All gold atoms look the same and they are the only atoms that are gold atoms. A different atomic structure would be a different atom and therefore a different element. All atoms have three components: protons, neutrons, and electrons. The first element, hydrogen, has one proton, usually one neutron, and one electron.

Protons have a positive charge and along with neutrons, which carry no charge (neutral), are found in the nucleus of the atom. They are also responsible for 99.9% of the mass of the atom (See the figure above.) Electrons are negatively charged and orbit around the nucleus in a three-dimensional cloud of energy levels. Electrons have very little mass, yet they determine the chemical behavior of the atoms because they are close enough in nature to interact. In reality, most of the volume of an atom is empty space. The positive-negative attraction (magnetism) keeps the electrons from flying away from the nucleus.

Conversely, the kinetic energy (energy associated with motion) of the electron provides it with the energy of movement and keeps the electron from slamming into the nucleus. The balance between these two forces, magnetism and kinetic energy, defines the orbital pathways of all electrons. If a neutral atom loses or gains one electron (or more), such as might happen during a chemical reaction, the resulting atom has an imbalance between the existing charges. This imbalance creates either an excess positive (+) charge, if the atom lost an electron; or an excess negative (-) charge, if the atom gained an electron. Atoms that have a charge are called ions, and they are very reactive and fundamentally important in chemical reactions.

The atoms are arranged in the Periodic Table according to atomic number—the number of protons—and to some degree the atomic mass number—the number of protons plus neutrons. The elements are arranged so that their position tells us something about the characteristics of the material itself.  
The groups, or columns, have similar characteristics because of electronic arrangement.  The families are the rows.  The following resource has a good overview of the Periodic Table:

Metals are located on the left side (the blue and green columns) and nonmetals on the right side (the orange columns) of the Periodic Table.  Two special classes of elements with special properties are noble gasses (the group in yellow), which combine with almost no other elements, and rare-earth metals that are so similar to one another that they are extremely difficult to separate (the coral colored elements at the bottom).  Noble gasses are colorless gasses that are extremely unreactive and are also called inert. The family of noble gasses includes helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, and radon. All the noble gasses are found in small amounts in the earth's atmosphere. 

A chemical reaction occurs when reactants are joined together to create a product that has different chemical properties than the original reactants. This always involves an energy change and a change in the electron configuration around the original atoms.

In all cases, the driving force for any chemical reaction is a move toward greater stability of the atoms. To increase stability, atoms tend to react so that they lower their energy and increase their entropy (randomness or lack of organization). In chemical terms, this means that they seek to have a stable number of electrons in their outermost orbital. The stable number means that the outermost energy level is either completely full or completely empty. 

Atoms act the way they do because of their electron arrangement and their ability to acquire, share, and donate electrons.  A molecule is a group of atoms held together by energy; this energy is a chemical bond.  There are three main kinds of chemical bonds: ionic, covalent, and hydrogen.  In ionic bonds, electrons are transferred from one atom to another, producing ions (charged particle).  A covalent bond is formed when electrons are shared.  The third type of bond is a hydrogen bond plays a very important role in biology.  These bonds are weak and so are not effective over long distances and they are directional.  You might think of hydrogen bonds as the hook and loops in something like Velcro™.  Each individual bond is not very strong but combined with others can form a very strong bond. 

 

A molecule is two or more atoms of the same or different elements joined in a chemical bond.  Water (H2O) is an example.  Compounds are molecules that have two or more different elements in proportions that don’t vary.  Again water is an example.  In a mixture, two or more molecules intermingle without chemically bonding, like sugar and water.

Water is one of the most unique molecules known to man and also one of the most important to biological systems. Not only does water exist in nature in all three states of matter (solid, liquid, gas), it also covers 75 percent of the earth and composes roughly 78 percent of the human body. 

We can live for months without food but even under mild conditions a fairly healthy individual can go only survive about a week without water.  

The uniqueness of water comes from its molecular structure. Because it is a polar covalent molecule, it has a slight positive and slight negative charge on opposite ends.

Examine the illustration above of the water molecule and note two important characteristics. First, notice the location of the slight positive and negative ends. Second, observe that water is a bent molecule, not linear or straight.

Because water is a bent, partially polar molecule, it has the following biologically important characteristics—all of them are critical to the creation and support of life on Earth: 

Temperature stabilizing effects

Heat storage - a large amount of heat is required to disrupt the hydrogen bonds. (This is the reason why your body is able to maintain a relatively constant internal temperature.)

Ice formation - if the temperature is low enough, very few hydrogen bonds break in the water and it forms a crystal (ice).  (This is why ice floats.)

High heat of vaporization - if heat is high enough, lots of hydrogen bonds break and the liquid is changed into a vapor.  (This is why sweating cools you off.)

Cohesion - the sticking together of two molecules of the same substance (This is why water forms droplets.) and adhesion which is the molecular attraction that holds the surfaces of two dissimilar substances together. (This is why things get wet when they are dipped in water.)

Polarity - having a slightly positive portion and a slightly negative portion gives it a polarity.  Because of this, polar molecules are called hydrophilic (water-loving) and are very soluble (dissolve readily) in water.  Nonpolar molecules like oil do not form hydrogen bonds and are not water soluble.  Because of this, nonpolar compounds are called hydrophobic (water-fearing).

Solvent Properties - in part because of cohesion and polarity, water is often called the “universal solvent”.  (This is not really true but it does dissolve many, many things.) 

Surface Tension - Surface tension is what permits Water Striders and other insects to walk across the surface of water and what enables a needle to float.

 

The bonds within water sometimes break spontaneously.  When this happens, one of the protons (hydrogen atom nuclei) separates from the molecule and becomes a hydrogen ion (H+).  This hydrogen ion may combine with another water molecule and forms a hydronium ion (H3O+). The rest of the water molecule is negatively charged and forms a hydroxide ion (OH-).  These ions are the basis for the pH scale which is a way to measure the relative amount of hydrogen ions in a solution.  One unit of change on the pH scale is actually a tenfold change.  This means that a solution with a pH of 4 has 10 times the concentration of H+ as a solution with a pH of 5 and a solution with a pH of 8 has one-tenth of the concentration of H+  as one with a pH of 7.  The figure below illustrates the pH scale and the pH of common substances.

Any substance that dissociates in water to increase the concentration of H+ is called an acid.  Acids have pH values below 7; the stronger an acid, the lower its pH.  If the substance combines with the dissociated H+ ion in water it is called an alkali or a base.  Basic solutions have pH values above 7.

The pH inside almost all living cells and in the fluid surrounding cells is fairly close to 7.  For example the pH of human blood needs to remain between 7.35 and 7.45; you would survive only a few minutes if the pH fell to 7.0 or rose to 7.8.  Because of this need for consistency, chemicals called buffers work to either take up or release H+.  pH is also important for the maintenance of proper three-dimensional structure of proteins. 

Living things consist mainly of hydrogen, oxygen, and carbon.  Carbon is important in part due to it's bonding behavior.  Each carbon can bond with up to 4 other atoms.

Macromolecules – biomolecules - organic molecules that have a carbon-based core with special groups (functional groups) attached.  The macromolecules are also polymers, which simply means they are molecules, built up of long chains of similar subunits (or monomers).  The four types of macromolecules are carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids.

1.  Proteins –  these are a major group of biological macromolecules and include the very important category of enzymes.  Some proteins play a structural role and still others act as chemical messengers throughout the body.  All proteins are made up of amino acids (monomers) which are linked together by peptide bonds.  Proteins have 4 levels of organization. 

Primary—the sequence of amino acids of the protein

Secondary—a folding or pleating of the primary structure

Tertiary—the folding and twisting of the polymer

Quaternary—the protein is composed of more than one polymer

If the polar nature of the protein is changed, primarily by either increasing temperature or lowering pH, the protein may unfold.  When this happens, the protein is said to be denatured and it will no longer function as it should.  Think, for example, about the white of an egg. The albumin is just about entirely protein, when it is heated it changes color and texture.  If the protein acts as an enzyme, denaturing will result in lack of action.

Enzymes increase the rate at which chemical reactions in cells occur.  An enzyme works by binding to a specific molecule and stressing the bonds of that molecule in such a way as to make a particular reaction more likely.  The key to this is the shape of the enzyme.  An enzyme is specific for a particular reaction and works to lower the energy required to start the reaction.  Regardless of the type of reaction, the enzyme is not affected by the chemical reaction and is available to be used again.  However if the bonding structure of the particular protein (enzyme) is changed, the enzyme is denatured and cannot work.

 2.  Carbohydrates make up the structural framework of a cell and also play a critical role in energy storage.  A carbohydrate is any molecule made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen in the ratio of 1:2:1.  If the carbohydrate is made of one or two monomers they are called simple carbohydrates, others that are long monomers are complex carbohydrates.  The simplest carbohydrates are the simple sugars or monosaccharides (examples are glucose, fructose).  Other simple carbohydrates are made of two monosaccharides linked together and are called disaccharides (sucrose or table sugar).

 

Complex carbohydrates are formed when sugars are linked together in long chains called polysaccharides.  Plants and animals store energy as polysaccharides; plants store as starch and animals store as glycogen.

3.  Lipids - For long term storage, organisms usually convert glucose into fats.  Fats and all other biological molecules that are not soluble in water but soluble in oil are called lipids.  Fats are made of glycerol and fatty acids.  Although not technically a polymer, lipids are still spoken of as having the building block (monomer) of fatty acids.  If the fatty acid has all internal carbon atoms with 2 hydrogens, they are called saturated (animal fats); if the fatty acid has at least one internal carbon that does not have 2 hydrogens, they are called unsaturated (oils).  Other types of lipids include phospholipids (found in the cell membrane) and steroids.                         

4.  Nucleic acids - long polymers (monomer is nucleotide) that serve as the information storage device of cells.  A nucleotidecontains a sugar (either deoxyribose or ribose), a  phosphate group, and a nitrogen containing base.  There are 5 different kinds of bases: adenine, guanine, cytosine, thymine, and uracil.  The presence and sequence of the bases is what is responsible for our genetic information.  We will go more into nucleic acids later in the term.

Homeostasis
In order to ensure that the four biological molecules (and therefore living organisms) function properly, our body must maintain a balanced internal environment. This ability is called homeostasis, which refers to stability, balance or equilibrium.

Because both the internal and external environments of the body are constantly changing and adjustments must be made continuously, homeostasis might be considered a dynamic equilibrium.

In order to maintain this equilibrium, an organism has to send signals, known as feedback, to signal to regulatory molecules on the current state of the organism.

Negative feedback loop: A process that will result in a reversal of the direction of change and tries to maintain homeostasis or a constant state. Most of the mechanisms in our body are regulated by negative feedback inhibition. In this type of process the final product shuts off the original stimulus and the system moves the variable back to the setpoint.

Positive feedback loop: A positive feedback loop is one where the system's output feeds its input, adding energy to the system and again increasing output, and so on, repeatedly. The response leads to a greater response and further away from the setpoint of the variable.  

 

Notice that the negative feedback works to return the system to normal while the positive feedback system just keeps building, pushing the system farther and farther out of normal.  One does not necessarily follow the other.
