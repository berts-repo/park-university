# Unit 4: Overview

How Cells Divide

Cell division is the process by which a cell or parent cell divides into two cells called daughter cells. Although cell division is very important, it is usually a small part of a larger cell cycle. Cell division is the biological basis of life. For simple unicellular organisms, one cell division reproduces an entire organism.

On a grander scale, cell division can create offspring from multicellular organisms, such as plants that grow from cuttings. But most importantly, cell division enables sexually reproducing organisms to develop from the one-celled zygote, which was produced by cell division from gametes. After growth, cell division allows for continual renewal and repair of the organism.

The primary concern of cell division is the maintenance of the original cell's genome or genetic information. Before division can occur, the genomic information which is stored in chromosomes must be replicated, and the duplicated genome separated cleanly between cells. A great deal of cellular infrastructure is involved in keeping genomic information consistent between "generations." 

Remember that cell division is required for growth, repair & replacement of damaged parts, and the reproduction of the species.

Cell division is actually two separate processes. Mitosis is nuclear division and cytokinesis is the division of the cytoplasm of a cell following the division of the nucleus. Mitosis produces two identical daughter cells during prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase. Interphase is often included in discussions of mitosis, but interphase is technically not part of mitosis, but rather encompasses stages G1, S, and G2 of the cell cycle.
