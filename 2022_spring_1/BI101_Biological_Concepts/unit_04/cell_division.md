# Unit 4: Cell Division

How Cells Divide

In the image to the left cells are dividing and you can actually see several stages of the process.  You can see the chromosomes lining up in the center of the cell or being pulled to opposite ends of the cell.  Some human cells divide frequently; the epithelial cells of your skin divides so rapidly that your skin is replaced every two weeks.  

Your stomach lining is replaced every few days.  On the other hand, nerve cells do not divide after they are formed.  Most of the cells of the body divide by a process called mitosis, but a more complex division takes place in the gonads to produce the gametes or sex cells. This is called meiosis.

You already know that there are two types of cells: prokaryotes and eukaryotes.  Because prokaryotes do not have a nucleus, their cell division takes place in only two stages.  First the DNA is copied, and then the cell splits by a process called binary fission.  Remember that in prokaryotes the DNA is found in just one circular chromosome. Eukaryotic cells have a cell division that is much more complicated.  One of the first things to remember is that their DNA is contained in multiple linear chromosomes.  A chromosome is a single, long DNA molecule packaged with proteins in a compact shape.  The eukaryotic cells either undergo a process called mitosis (in nonreproductive or body cells) or meiosis (in reproductive or germ cells).  The eukaryotic cells go through a cell cycle (illustrated below) which is the series of events from one cell division to the next.

Cell Cycle

As you can see from the diagram on the left, the actual mitosis makes up only a small part of the cycle.  The majority of the cell life cycle is spent in interphase, which consists of G1, S, and G2. Both G phases are growth phases and while a large amount of growth takes place in G1, growth still continues in G2. Please remember that mitosis does not include interphase.  

The following discussions apply to mitosis, which takes place in somatic cells (non-reproductive).  

Chromosomes exist in pairs which are referred to as homologous chromosomes.  These chromosomes, homologues, typically carry information about the same traits at the same locations on each chromosome.  

Cells that have two of each chromosome are called diploid cells.  One chromosome of each pair is inherited from each parent.  Before cell division can occur, each homologous chromosome must replicate, resulting in two identical copies, called sister chromatids.  The sister chromatids are held together by centromeres. 

One Chromosome made of two chromatids

Chromosomes are composed of chromatin, a complex of DNA and protein.  The DNA of a eukaryotic chromosome is one very long, double-stranded fiber that is unbroken throughout the entire length of the chromosome.  During cell division the DNA is coiled into compact chromosomes.  During interphase the chromosomes are not visible because they are uncoiled and the nuclear envelope is intact and nucleoli are visible.  Interphase is an extremely busy time for the cell. 

It is during interphase that the cell is actually producing the products that it is specialized for and genes are being read.  For the DNA to be actually used, it must be in an uncoiled condition. During prophase the chromosomes begin to coil and become visible, the nucleolus disappears, and the nuclear envelope disintegrates.  Spindle fibers (microtubles) form (used in animal cell division).  At the end of prophase, the nuclear envelope has disappeared and chromosomes are distinct. 

The chromosomes become aligned along a line (often referred to as the metaphase plate) figuratively dividing the cell into half during metaphase.  During anaphase the chromatids separate; this results in the number of chromosomes doubling.  Anaphase is a rapid process and is the shortest phase of mitosis.  During telophase the cell begins to divide into two; the chromosomes reach the poles and begin to uncoil; the nucleus begins to reform. The spindle apparatus disassembles (this is what has been responsible for the actual movement of the chromosomes).  In cytokinesis, the two cells are separated. 

Mitosis — A closer look

Remember that in interphase the cell has a visible nucleus but no visible chromosomes.  The DNA is still in an uncoiled state that is called chromatin. 

 

Notice that the chromosomes are visable and that you do not see a nuclear membrane in prophase.  The nucleolus is also not visible.

 

     

The next phase is metaphase.  Metaphase begins when the chromosomes─consisting of 2 chromatids each—align in the center of the cell.

 

In anaphase, the chromatids separate and move rapidly toward opposite poles of the cell.  When they finally arrive, each pole has one complete set of chromosomes.  This is the shortest stage of mitosis. 

Telophase involves the reformation of the nucleus and may or may not lead into cytokinesis (division of the cytoplasm).  The chromosomes become indistinct again and the nucleolus reappears.  You may notice the difference in the telophase examples provided.  The one on the left is typical of animals and illustrates how the cells have a constriction that ultimately separates the cells.  The cells on the right are plant cells and the cell undergoing telophase has a cell plate which forms between the two nuclei.

 

 

We now have two individual cells that are just like the parent cell.

Cancer

Cancer is a growth disorder of cells and starts when apparently normal cells begin to grow in an uncontrollable way spreading out to other parts of the body.  This cluster of cells is a tumor.  Benign tumors are completely enclosed by tissue and are therefore encapsulated.  These do not spread to other parts of the body.  Malignant tumors are invasive and are not encapsulated; because of this cells are able to break away from the tumor and spread.  The cells form new tumors at these new sites called metastases.  

Cancer, no matter what type, always has four characteristics: uncontrolled cell growth, loss of cell specialization, invasion of normal tissues, and metastasis.  Cancer eventually causes death because cancer cells continually increase in number while spreading to vital areas of the body, occupying the space in which normal cells would otherwise be found carrying out normal body functions.  Cancer cells put pressure on normal tissues and replace them, causing pain and bleeding.  In short, cancer disrupts normal cellular activities that are essential for life.

Skin cancer is the most common form of cancer in the US and its primary cause is ultraviolet radiation.  While everyone is now much more aware of skin cancers, not everyone knows what to look for.  There are different types of skin cancer: carcinoma, sarcoma, and melanoma.  By far the most common type of skin cancer is basal cell carcinoma.  Fortunately it is the least dangerous form because it grows slowly and rarely spreads.  The next most common form is squamous cell carcinoma and can become life threatening if not treated.  Sarcomas develop from soft tissues like fat, muscle, nerve, fibrous tissue around joints, etc.  Fewer than 0.5% of the carcinomas and sarcomas result in the death of the individual.  However, a small minority of skin cancers (4%) are called melanoma and they have a 20% chance of killing you.

Learn the ABCD's of melanoma to diagnose them before they spread too far.  The ABCD's of melanoma are as follows: Asymmetry, Border, Color, and Diameter.  Since the inception of the ABCDs, evidence has accumulated that adding the "E" for "Evolving" would substantially enhance the ability of physicians and laypersons to recognize melanomas in early growth phases. You absolutely need to know these ABCDs of melanoma.

Asymmetry: Most early melanomas are asymmetrical: a line through the middle would not create matching halves. Common moles are round and symmetrical.

Border: The borders of early melanomas are often uneven and may have scalloped or notched edges. Common moles have smoother, more even borders.

Color: Common moles usually are a single shade of brown. Varied shades of brown, tan, or black are often the first sign of melanoma. As melanomas progress, the colors red, white and blue may appear.

Diameter: Early melanomas tend to grow larger than common moles - generally to at least the size of a pencil eraser (about 6mm, or 1/4 inch, in diameter). However, they may also be smaller.

But regardless of these signs, if you have a suspicious mole, contact your physician.  

Meiosis 

What is the significance of sexual reproduction?  If we have one organism that produces an offspring and each of the new organisms is exactly the same as the original this is called asexual reproduction (binary fission or mitosis).  Sexual reproduction involvesmeiosis, formation of gametes, and fertilization (union of two gametes).  Because of the characteristics of meiosis, there can be mixing of traits leading to an increased variety.      

In animals, meiosis takes place in germ cells.  The germ cells are the egg and sperm. In plants meiosis produces spores. 

Just as in mitosis, the chromosomes have replicated before meiosis begins, during interphase.  There are two divisions in meiosis: meiosis I serves to separate the homologous chromosomes; in meiosis II the sister chromatids separate.  When meiosis is over, what started out as one diploid cell ends up as four haploid cells.  These cells have half of the chromosomes as the adult animal so that when gametes (sex cells) unite, the animal will again have the correct number of chromosomes.
 
Overview of meiosis 
Purpose of Meiosis: 
    Keeps chromosome number constant across the generations 
    Makes sure that each gamete contains only one member of each  homologous pair

Meiosis has two divisions:Since meiosis involves two nuclear divisions it producesfour haploid daughter cells, each containing half the total number of chromosomes as the diploid parent nucleus.

Meiosis I - the first nuclear division;  Prior to meiosis I, DNAreplication occurs and each chromosome has two sister chromatids; during meiosis I, homologous chromosomes pair; while paired up the chromosomes may have equal exchanges of genetic material; this is called crossing over; after crossing-over occurs, sister chromatids of a chromosome are no longer identical

Meiosis II - second nuclear division, nearly identical to mitosis.  No replication of DNA is needed between meiosis I and II because chromosomes were already doubled; centromeres divide; daughter chromosomes result as sister chromatids separate; chromosomes in the four daughter cells have only one chromatid

Crossing-over produces genetic variation.  Crossing-over results in the exchange of genetic material between nonsister chromatids so now the sister chromatids have a different mix of genes.  This may mean that an allele that was on the maternal chromosome is moved to the paternal one.  Therefore the chromosomes are no longer just like the parents.
