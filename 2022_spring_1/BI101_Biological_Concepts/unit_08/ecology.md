Ecology and Biodiversity 

Distinct ecological communities of plants and animals living together in a particular climate are called "biomes." All of the world's biomes together, in addition to marine and freshwater ecosystems are referred to as the biosphere, which includes all life on Earth.  

The distinction between population and community is an important one.  For example, I live in the community of Kansas City which in turn contains many different populations.  For example there is a population of redbud trees, crab grass, rabbits, etc.  All of these populations form the community and the community interacts with the physical and chemical factors (abiotic factors) like moisture and temperature to form an ecosystem.

An organism’s ability to survive and reproduce in a particular environment is a result of natural selection. By eliminating the least fit individuals within a population, environmental forces increase the likelihood that only the most successful species will survive in that particular environment. It is this ability of an organism to survive and grow in a particular environment that we will study this unit.

As mentioned in Unit 1's intro, there are various levels of organization—particularly cellular and organismic—and even some levels above the individual organism—species & population. Individual organisms are grouped into populations, which, in turn, form communities, which then form ecosystems. There are two basic categories of communities: terrestrial (land) and aquatic (water). These two basic types of community contain eight smaller units known as biomes. A biome is a large-scale category containing many communities of a similar nature, whose distribution is primarily determined by climate

Terrestrial Biomes: tundra, grassland, desert, taiga, temperate forest, tropical forest.

Aquatic Biomes: marine, freshwater.

Climate controls biome distribution by means of an altitudinal gradient and a latitudinal gradient. With increases of either altitude or latitude, cooler and drier conditions occur.

 A population is a group of individuals of the same species living in the same geographic area. The study of factors that affect growth, stability, and decline of populations is population dynamics. All populations undergo three distinct phases of their life cycle:

growth—occurs when available resources exceed the number of individuals able to exploit them. Reproduction is rapid, and death rates are low, resulting in a net increase in the population size.  Populations can grow quickly for a while, but not forever.

stability—often proceeded by a "crash" since the growing population eventually exceeds its available resources. Stability is usually the longest phase of a population's life cycle.

decline—decrease in the number of individuals in a population, and eventually leads to population extinction.

Biological communities, like the organisms in them, can and do change over time. Ecological time focuses on community events that occur over decades or centuries. Geological time focuses on events lasting thousands of years or more.

Community succession is the sequential replacement of species by immigration of new species and local extinction of older ones following a disturbance that creates unoccupied habitats for colonization. The initial rapid colonizer species are the pioneer community. Eventually a climax community of more or less stable but slower growing species eventually develops.

During succession, productivity declines and diversity increases. These trends tend to increase the total weight of living tissue (biomass) in a community. Succession occurs because each community stage prepares the environment for the stage following it.

Primary succession begins with bare rock and takes a very long time to occur. Weathering by wind and rain plus the actions of pioneer species such as lichens and mosses begin the buildup of soil. Herbaceous plants, including the grasses, grow on deeper soil and shade out shorter pioneer species. Pine trees or deciduous trees eventually take root and in most biomes will form a climax community of plants that are stabile in the environment. The young produced by climax species can live in that environment, unlike the young produced by successional species.

Secondary succession occurs when an environment has been disturbed, such as by fire, geological activity, or human intervention (farming or deforestation in most cases). This form of succession often begins in an abandoned field with soil layers already in place. Compared to primary succession, which must take long periods of time to build or accumulate soil, secondary succession occurs rapidly. The herbaceous pioneering plants give way to pines, which in turn may give way to a hardwood deciduous forest.

Early researchers assumed climax communities were determined for each environment. Today we recognize the outcome of competition among whatever species are present as establishing the climax community.

Climax communities tend to be more stable than successional communities. Early stages of succession show the most growth and are most productive. Pioneer communities lack diversity, make poor use of inputs, and lose heat and nutrients. As succession proceeds, species variety increases and nutrients are recycled more. Climax communities make fuller use of inputs and maintain themselves, thus, they are more stable. Human activity (such as clearing a climax forest community to establish a farm field consisting of a cultivated pioneering species, say corn or wheat) replaces climax communities with simpler communities.

Communities are composed of species that evolve, so the community must also evolve. Comparing marine communities of 500 million years ago with modern communities shows modern communities composed of quite different organisms.

Population growth
 
 Two of the most basic factors that influence the rate of population growth are the birth rate and the death rate (mortality). The intrinsic (inherent) rate of increase is the birth rate minus the death rate.  The age at which an organism reproduces affects the rate of population increase. Some organisms grow fast, reproduce quickly, and have abundant offspring each reproductive cycle while others grow slowly, reproduce at a later age, and have few offspring per cycle. However, most organisms are intermediate to these two extremes. 

Generally organisms that live in habitats that have plenty of resources will have a rapid reproduction rate while habitats that have very limited resources favor organisms with a slow reproductive rate.  The organisms that have a high rate of increase are said to have r-selected adaptations.  K-selected adaptations are held by organisms living in the limited ecosystem.  K-selected species are generally long-lived and have only a few, often fairly large offspring.  Species referred to as r-selected typically have a short life span; early reproduction; small size; and the ability to produce large numbers of usually small offspring in a relatively short period of time.

 Humans can remove or alter the constraints on population sizes, with both good and bad consequences. Human action is causing the extinction of species at thousands of times the natural rate. Extinction is caused by alteration of a population's environment in a harmful way. Habitat disruption is the disturbance of the physical environment of a species, for example cutting a forest or draining wetlands. Habitat disruption is currently the leading cause of extinction.

Changes in the biological environment occur in three ways.

1. Species introduction: An exotic species is introduced into an area where it may have no predators to control its population size, or where it can greatly out compete native organisms. Examples include zebra mussels introduced into Lake Erie, and lake trout released into Yellowstone Lake where they are threatening the native cutthroat trout populations.

2. Overhunting: When a predator population increases or becomes more efficient at killing the prey, the prey population may decline or go extinct. Examples today include big game hunting, which has, in many places reduced the predator (or in this case prey) population. In human history we may have caused the extinction of the mammoths and mastodons due to increased human hunting skill.

3. Secondary extinction: Loss of food species can cause migration or extinction of any species that depends largely or solely on that species as a food source. Destruction of bamboo forests in China, the food for the giant panda, may cause the extinction of the panda.

Population Controls

Natural populations are not governed by a single control, but rather have the combined effects of many controls simultaneously playing roles in determining population size. Which factors, if either, is more important in controlling population growth: physical or biological? Physical factors may play a dominant role, and are called density independent regulation, since population density is not a factor. The other extreme has biological factors dominant, and is referred to as density dependent regulation, since population density is a factor. It seems likely that one or the other extreme may dominate in some environments, with most environments having a combination control.

Density-dependent factors

Life will get harder for individuals when it gets crowded.  No matter what type of organism you are, difficulties will arise with increasing competition for limited resources  In particular, as population size increases, organisms experience:

reduced food supplies, due to competition

diminished accessibility to places to live and breed, also due to competition

increased incidence of parasites and disease, which can spread more easily when their hosts live at a higher density

increased predation risk, as predator populations grow in response to the increased availability of their prey and as the more densely packed prey become more visible

The limitations on a population's growth that are a consequence of population density are called density-dependent factors. Limiting factor examples include food, refuge from predators, living space, and water.  These factors have an affect on the sustainable size of the population. With increased density, a population's growth is reduced as the limited resources slow it dow.  This ceiling on growth is called the carrying capacity.  As the population density increases there are greater stressors because of these factors.  

Competition among organisms is a significant factor in the regulation of population size.  Competition is the interaction among organisms that compete for the same resources, such as food and living space.  Competition may occur between individuals of the same species (intraspecific competition) or between individuals of different species (interspecific competition).  Because the competition between individuals results in natural selection and survival of the fittest, it is one of the driving forces of evolutionary change.  
 
 

As the population size approaches the carrying capacity, the amount of competition for living space, food, and mates becomes more fierce. Changes in the nature and amount of competition that occur as the population increases are density-dependent effect. 

Density-independent factors

Density-independent factors can also knock a population down.  These are forces that strike populations without regard for population size, increasing the death rate or decreasing the number and rate of offspring produced.  They are mostly weather- or geology-based, including such things as floods, earthquakes, fires, and lightning. They also include habitat destruction by other species, such as humans.  

Carrying Capacity 
 
 The carrying capacity is defined as the number of individuals that the environment can support for an extended period of time without detrimental effects. The numbers of individuals in an area may increase tremendously until at some point the pressure (e.g. crowding due to competition for food) causes the numbers to drop.  As the population reaches the carrying capacity of its habitat, the growth rate slows to a near standstill. Population Size Controls

The environment is the ultimate cause of population stabilization. Two categories of factors are commonly used: physical environment and biological environment. Three subdivisions of the biological environment are competition, predation, and symbiosis.

Physical environmental factors include food, shelter, water supply, space availability, and (for plants) soil and light. One of these factors may severely limit population size, even if the others are not as constrained. The Law of the Minimum states that population growth is limited by the resource in the shortest supply.

Competition among organisms is a significant factor in the regulation of population size. Competition is the interaction among organisms that compete for the same resources, such as food and living space. Competition may occur between individuals of the same species (intraspecific competition) or between individuals of different species (interspecific competition). Because the competition between individuals results in natural selection and survival of the fittest, it is one of the driving forces of evolutionary change. As the population size approaches the carrying capacity, the amount of competition for living space, food, and mates becomes more fierce. Changes in the nature and amount of competition that occur as the population increases are density-dependent effect.

The biological role played by a species in the environment is called a niche. Competitive exclusion occurs between two species when competition is so intense that one species completely eliminates the second species from an area. In nature this is rather rare. While owls and foxes may compete for a common food source, there are alternate sources of food available. Therefore, niche overlap is said to be minimal.

Ecosystem 
 

An ecosystem is a complex of living organisms (biotic components), their physical environment (abiotic components) and all their interrelationships in a particular unit of space.  Ecosystems have definable boundaries, but are open and dynamic.  An example of an ecosystem illustrating definable boundaries might be a pond.  The state of Missouri would not be an ecosystem because its boundaries are defined politically, but not biologically.  Continuing with the pond as an open ecosystem: energy from the sun enters the pond as does water from runoff and incoming streams, water evaporates from the pond and may even flow out in streams, also flowing water carries nutrients in and out of the pond.  The dynamic part of an ecosystem is that it is continuously changing.  For example, temperature changes seasonally, daily, and possibly hourly.  Materials also tend to cycle within ecosystems.  Energy does not cycle, because a usable form of energy cannot be created from an unusable form nor can energy be created.  People say that plants create energy from the sun.  This is not true, the plants simply capture the sun's energy and change it into a more usable form.

Species interrelationships

Everything in biology is a part of something larger, and populations are no exception.  Invariably, populations share space with other populations.  They interact or are interdependent and form communities.  How they interact in these communities varies from population to population.  Take, for example, the American robin.  In the summer, robins nest in our bushes and feed in our yards.  To earthworms and insects, they are predators.  To cardinals and chickadees, robins are competitors.  To tapeworms living in their intestines, robins are hosts or habitats.  Therefore the niche of the robin becomes important.  A niche is the function of a particular species in an ecological community and includes all aspects of the physical and biological environment that are important to a species.  The niche is not only where the organism lives but its space utilization, food consumption, temperature range, appropriate conditions for mating, moisture requirements and many other factors. 

Because of interspecific and intraspecific competition there is always some competition between species in a particular area or environment.  The competitive exclusion principle states that two species that compete for the exact same resources cannot stably coexist. One of the two competitors will always have an ever so slight advantage over the other that leads to extinction of the second competitor in the long run.

One of the most obvious interactions between populations is the matter of who eats who.  Predators are organisms of one species that kill and eat organisms of another species—their prey. Because populations regularly exploit other populations as sources of nutrients and energy, the fates of the predator and prey populations are intimately intertwined.  When one removes predators from an area, the numbers of the prey organism increases.  When more predators are added, the numbers of prey decrease.  One example of this type of interaction is illustrated by the monarch butterfly.  The larvae feed on milkweed, which is very distasteful and even toxic to other animals.  The monarch has the ability to not only tolerate the plant fluids, but to accumulate it, making the monarch distasteful (or toxic) to their predators.

Interactions between species can become so intimate that one or both become dependent on the other; these are called symbioses.  An example of a symbiotic pair is cattle and the bacteria that live in their digestive tracts, enabling them to digest the cellulose in grass.   There are three types of relationships within communities that are types of symbiosis: commensalism,  mutualism, and parasitism.

In commensalism, one species benefits while the other is apparently unaffected.  One example is the remora and the shark. The dorsal fin of the remora (a  [link: http://users.rcn.com/jkimball.ma.ultranet/BiologyPages/V/Vertebrates.html#Bony] bony fish) is modified into a sucker with which it forms a temporary attachment to the shark. When the shark feeds, the remora picks up scraps. The shark makes no attempt to prey on the remora.  Intimate relationships in which both species benefit are mutualisms.  Some examples might be flowers and their pollinators, and cattle and their gut bacteria.  In parasitism, as in predation, one species benefits at the expense of another.  We can separate parasitism from predation by examining some differences.  Generally parasites are much smaller then their host and may even be species specific.  Usually, parasites do not kill their hosts quickly (as predators do) but rather they slowly kill the host organism and therefore regulate the population size as well.  As a population increases in density, parasites can more easily find new hosts by moving or reproducing.

It is almost impossible to discuss these interrelationships without thinking about alien species.  Alien species are those moved by humans (either directly or indirectly) to areas outside of their native ranges. Once transported in this manner, they become removed from the predators, parasites, and diseases that kept them in balance in their native environments. As a result of losing these controls, they often become pests in the areas into which they are introduced and may cause tremendous damage to their new habitat.  Two such examples are Kudzu (in Mississippi) and the nutria (in Louisiana).  Because of the lack of these natural controls the population will grow at a rapid rate. 

Energy Flow

It is important to notice that nutrients cycle through the ecosystem, while energy flows (does not cycle) through the ecosystem. This is an important distinction because energy will proceed from more organized to less organized states and therefore cannot cycle.  Of course the ultimate source of energy is the sun with plants being the organisms that can use the sun's energy (primary producers) and are therefore the key to life on earth.  The primary consumers are herbivores or plant-eaters while secondary consumers are carnivores or meat-eaters.  The cycle continues with one organism feeding on another, passing along nutrients as well as energy that was trapped from the sun.   Living things produce waste and eventually they die.  Without decomposers breaking down this waste material and dead organisms, collectively called detritus, the earth would be piled high with waste material.

Trophic levels are literally the levels of nourishment within an ecological system, and therefore describe how energy is transferred within food webs and chains.  A food chain shows how each living thing gets its food. Some animals eat plants and some animals eat other animals. For example, a simple food chain links the trees, shrubs, giraffes (that eat trees & shrubs), and lions (that eat the giraffes). Each link in this chain is food for the next link. A food chain always starts with plant life and ends with an animal.  
 
 Because a large amount of energy is lost at each level (80 to 90%-as you will see in the energy pyramids) the further along the food chain you go, the less food (and hence energy) remains available; most food chains have no more than four or five links; and a change in the size of one population in a food chain will affect other populations.  
 
 This large decrease in energy is due to the fact that the organism at any particular level expends some energy in everyday life.  This will include movement, reproduction, digestion, even growth.  Also each time the energy is transferred, some energy is lost as heat.  Although a food chain sounds very simple, there are not many simple independent food chains found in an ecosystem, but many interdependent and complex food chains look more like a web and are therefore called food webs. Energy Pyramids

Pyramid of Energy: As previously indicated, at each link in the food chain, energy that was originally stored by the autotrophic plants is dissipated along the food chain. The more links in the food chain, the more dissipated or unusable energy there is. There is generally an 80–90 percent loss at each link of the food chain, creating a pyramid-shaped diagram that is wider at the bottom and narrow at the top as in the illustration below.  
 
 In theory, when food energy is transferred from one trophic level to another in a food chain (from grass to bunny, bunny to hawk, etc.), only about ten percent of the energy is passed on. (The ten percent rule) So where does it all go? The vast majority of the energy is lost as heat. 
 
 The remaining energy goes into maintaining the organism's internal body temperature and metabolism, defending against predators, reproducing, moving and generally living. Because so much energy is lost from level to level, from an efficiency point of view it is much better for the state of the world to get our energy straight from plants (or other autotrophs) which produce their own food from solar energy. 

 

Pyramid of Mass & Numbers: The mass (weight) and numbers of organisms decreases along a food chain (e.g. grass-grasshoppers-mice-snakes-hawk). It takes many pounds of grass (or numerous grass plants) to support one hawk at the top of a food chain.

One really interesting situation that explains how things are all interrelated is that of DDT.  It was the first synthetic organic insecticide to be used and was originally thought to be the perfect insecticide.  It was inexpensive, long-lasting, relatively harmless to humans, and very deadly to insects.  However, DDT has several drawbacks.  
 
 Although many of these drawbacks are not unique to DDT, they were first recognized with DDT because of its widespread use.  One problem was that some insects developed a resistance to it and therefore were not affected.  Another problem was the fact that it affected many nontarget organisms and can accumulate in animal tissue.  It also degrades very slowly.  
 
 All of this makes it a good candidate for biomagnification, becoming more concentrated as it moves through the food chain.  When these effects were noticed, the US and many other developed countries banned the use of DDT.  However, DDT is still used in parts of the world where malaria and other serious insect-transmitted diseases are common.

Biomes

A biome is a major regional or global biotic community, such as a grassland or desert, characterized chiefly by the predominant forms of plant life and by adaptations of organisms to that particular environment.  Important to the definition of biomes is the climate of the area.  Biomes may be classified in different ways and thus broken down into many different types.  We will use five different types: aquatic, deserts, grasslands, tundra, and forests.  We could further break these down into specific types, but for our purposes, we will keep it simple.  For example, forests may be temperate rainforest, tropical rainforest, coniferous, or broadleaf.

While terrestrial biomes are determined by the amount and type of precipitation and by temperature, aquatic ecosystems are shaped by other factors.  Examples of these factors are the ability of the sun's rays to penetrate the water, the depth of the water, the nature of the bottom substrate, the water temperature, and the amount of dissolved salts.  Because of these factors we may think of marine ecosystems and freshwater ecosystems, with the estuaries somewhere in between.

A lack of water is the primary factor that determines if an area will be a desert; these are areas that average less than 25 centimeters (10 inches) of precipitation per year.  When we typically think about deserts we envision a hot dry environment sparsely populated.  Not all deserts are hot, many are quite cool during a major part of the year;  they are not devoid of life, many species of organisms are present but they are specially adapted to survive in their particular environment.

Grasslands, also known as prairies or steppes, are widely distributed over the temperate parts of the world.  They typically receive between 25 and 75 centimeters (10-30 inches) of precipitation per year.  These areas are windy with hot summers and cold-to-mild winters.  Grasses make up 60-90% of the vegetation and trees are rare in these areas except along waterways.  

The tundra is a biome that lacks trees and has a permanently frozen subsurface soil (known as the permafrost).  Because this biome is extremely cold and windy (up to 10 months of winter), no trees can live in the area.  Although the precipitation is similar to that of deserts, the short summer is generally wet because the winter snow melts in the spring and summer when temperatures are usually less than 10 C (50 F) and the evaporation rate is reduced.

A forest is a dense growth of trees, plants, and underbrush covering a large area.  Because of this definition and the varying conditions in which they can be found, there are many types of trees.  For example, temperate and tropical rainforests, temperate deciduous forests, and coniferous forests.
