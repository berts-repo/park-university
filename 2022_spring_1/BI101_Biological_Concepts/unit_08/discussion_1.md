Topic #1 Invasive or Alien Species

Invasive or alien species have decimated natural ecosystems and cost us untold millions of dollars in environmental damage.

Define what constitutes an invasive or alien species.

Invasive species is a non-native species that has spread its population to an area beyond its native location, that causes environmental or economic damage in the new area. (Simon, 413) An alien species is a species that has been moved by humans to an area outside of there native range.

Why, in general, do invasive species pose such a serious problem for natural ecosystems?

The invasive species may not have natural predators in the area, causing a boom in their population that can grow out of control. The invasive species can also consume all the nutrients in an area, causing other species to go drop in population, disrupting the homeostasis of the ecosystem. 

Select one invasive species from the list below (be sure it is different from your classmates) address the following:
 
Asian Swamp Eel (Monopterus albus)

Where did this species come from and where and how was it introduced?

Asian swamp eel is a fresh water eel native to Asia. It is also found in Central and South America, Africa, and Australia. (Asian swamp eel Monopterus albus (Zuiew, 1793)) The eel is considered a delicacy and introduced by many. The eel is known to emigrate through the water and can even travel short distances on land, laying up to 1000 eggs a year and drying up shallow water when their population is abundant. (Asian swamp eel Monopterus albus (Zuiew, 1793))

What effect has this species had on the ecosystem?

"Swamp-eel competition may displace native aquatic species. They may accelerate the drying of shallow water bodies (when the species is abundant) during periods of drought via their extensive borrow system, thereby causing additional risk to other aquatic organisms." (Asian swamp eel - FWS)
