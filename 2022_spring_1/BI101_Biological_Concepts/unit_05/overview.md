# Unit 5: Overview

DNA: What is it? How does it work? 

Old ideas die slowly, even when they are wrong. This was the case with the chemical nature of DNA. While early studies had established that DNA was the genetic material, there were others who argued that genes were made of proteins. It is two young scientists, James D. Watson and Francis H. C. Crick, that are credited with possibly the greatest discovery of the 20th century; the three-dimensional structure of DNA.

DNA structure is compatible with the four roles of genetic material in cells. These are:

DNA makes copies of itself. Before a cell can reproduce, it must make copies of its genetic material.

DNA encodes information. Different DNAs, regardless of the source, must have some features in common and some differences.

DNA controls cells. It's not enough just to carry information. The information must be put to use controlling what the cell does.

DNA changes by mutation. DNA's molecular structure must be flexible enough so that minor changes can occur without rendering the molecule nonfunctional.

Core Learning Outcome

Describe the structure of DNA and illustrate its significance in determining the composition, characteristics, reproduction, and behavior of an organism.

Unit Objectives:

Identify the similarities and differences of DNA and RNA in terms of structure, function and location in the cell,

Describe the relationship between the sequence of nucleotides in DNA and the production of proteins,

Compare the different types of DNA mutations in terms of mechanism and outcome,

Explain the importance of RNA splicing,

Describe the various mechanisms that regulate gene expression.

Analyze and interpret results from experimental data.
