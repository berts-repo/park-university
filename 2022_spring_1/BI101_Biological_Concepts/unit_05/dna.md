A Timeline of DNA's Discovery

1865 Gregor Mendel, in experiments with peas, theorizes about how traits are passed on from one generation to the next. 

1869 Swiss scientist Friedrich Miescher isolates a material in cells from dirty bandages that will eventually become known as "deoxyribonucleic acid" or DNA. He called it "nuclein."

1909 German geneticist Wilhelm Johannsen coins the term "gene" to describe the unit of heredity.

1911 American scientist Thomas Hunt Morgan shows that these units of heredity are located on chromosomes. He receives the Nobel Prize in 1933.

1929 American biochemist Phoebus Levene determines the chemical makeup of DNA, identifying its four bases - - adenine, cytosine, guanine and thymine.

1943 English biochemist William Astbury makes the first X-ray diffraction images of DNA's structure.

1944  In what was arguably the defining moment for nucleic acid research, Oswald Avery, Maclyn McCarty and Colin MacLeod, at Rockefeller Institute (now University) Hospital, New York, proved that DNA was the material of inheritance. They showed that the heritable property of virulence from one infectious strain of  pneumococcus (the bacterial agent of pneumonia) could be transferred to a noninfectious bacterium with pure DNA.   1952 Work with viruses and bacteria by American scientists Alfred Hershey and Martha Chase supports the theory that genes are made of DNA.

1953 At Cavendish Laboratory at Cambridge, Francis Crick and James Watson describe the double helix structure of DNA and suggest how genetic material is copied. Their idea of a double helix formation was based on X-ray diffraction images made by Kings College's Rosalind Franklin, who was part of a laboratory group led by Maurice Wilkins. Watson, Crick and Wilkins receive the Nobel Prize in 1962.  In April 1953 a one-page report published in the science journal Nature revolutionized science by describing the three-dimensional structure of DNA.

The discovery that genes are made of DNA did not answer the question of how the information of DNA was used.  How does a string of nucleotides in a spiral molecule in the nucleus of the cell determine if you have red hair?  We now know that the information in DNA is arranged in blocks, each block is a gene specifying a protein.  These proteins determine what a particular cell will be like.  The gene location on the chromosome is called the locus.

DNA structure and replication 

DNA is a polymer of nucleotides with each nucleotide composed of phosphate, sugar (deoxyribose), and an organic base.  There are four kinds of bases- adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G) and cytosine (C).  DNA consists of 2 strands held together by base pairing:  adenine pairs with thymine and guanine pairs with cytosine.  Each strand is the complement of the other.

When we talk about the genetic code, it is important to remember that we are merely discussing a sequence of nitrogenous bases.  These bases are adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G) and cytosine (C) in DNA.  In RNA (all types) the thymine is replaced with uracil (U).  Also of significance is the fact that DNA has a double helix structure while all RNAs have only 1 strand of the bases.  In both DNA and RNA there is a definite relationship between these bases.  In DNA, A always binds to T and G always binds to C, therefore the amounts of A=T and G=C.  Each sequence of three bases on DNA is called a codon and codes for a particular amino acid.  The sequence of amino acids then makes up the primary structure of a protein.  Using the table below to look up AUG we get methionine (met) which at the beginning of a gene is also the start signal. DNA replication or DNA synthesis is the process of copying a double-stranded DNA strand, prior to cell division.  The two resulting double strands are identical (if the replication went well), and each of them consists of one original and one newly synthesized strand. This is called semiconservative replication.  As you will see in some of the animations in this unit, there are also enzymes involved in the replication.  First, the two strands of DNA unwind (enzymes unwind them) and expose their bases, then unattached nucleotides pair with exposed bases (another enzyme attaches these) making the new half of the DNA molecule.  Another enzyme actually hooks together these new short stretches of nucleotides into one continuous strand.

Sometimes a molecule of DNA breaks.  Sometimes it is replicated incorrectly so that it doesn't match the parent molecule--it can also acquire mismatched bases or missing or extra chunks.  The DNA repair processes help minimize the damage, again utilizing specialized enzymes.

Because it is important that the DNA is protected from damage, DNA never leaves the nucleus.  What happens is that copies of the genes, which are made of ribonucleic acid (RNA), are produced and leave the nucleus.  RNA is the same as DNA except it has ribose instead of deoxyribose for the sugar and has one different base.  A cell uses three kinds of RNA in the production of proteins: messenger RNA (mRNA), ribosomal RNA (rRNA), and transfer RNA (tRNA).  The process by which RNA is made from DNA is called transcription. 

Transcription of most genes produce mRNA, which is the only class of RNA that carries protein-building instructions.  Transcription of other genes produces rRNA, a component of ribosomes, and tRNA, which delivers amino acids one at a time to a ribosome.  Transcription differs from DNA replication in 3 ways.  A selected stretch of one DNA strand, not the whole molecule, is the template.  An enzyme brings nucleotides one at a time to the growing RNA strand.  The result is one free strand of RNA nucleotides, not a hydrogen-bonded double helix.  Each new molecule of eukaryotic mRNA is not in final form just after creation.  Just as a tailor may remove some threads before the garment leaves the shop, so do the eukaryotic cells tailor their "pre-mRNA."   Part of this preparation includes enzymes placing a 'cap' or a nucleotide on one end and a 'tail', which is a group of 100-300 nucleotides, on the other end.  Besides these alterations, noncoding portions (introns) are snipped out, and actual coding regions (exons) are spliced together to produce the mature transcript. We are now ready for translation, which is the conversion of the nucleotide order into the order of amino acids in a protein.  Recall that ribosomes are the organelles where proteins are assembled from their amino acids.  Ribosomes are made of proteins and rRNA.  The ribosomes link together the amino acids that are encoded within the mRNA.  The mRNA is “read” by a ribosome in three-nucleotide units.  Each three-nucleotide sequence codes for a particular amino acid, and is called a codon.  These codons represent the genetic code.

The tRNA molecule has an attachment site for an amino acid and a anticodon that binds to the mRNA codon.  Therefore the function of tRNA is to pick up the appropriate amino acids from the cytoplasm and bring them to the mRNA and the ribosome where they will be linked to form a protein.  There are actually three stages of translation: initiation, elongation, and termination.  Initiation is, of course, the beginning of the synthesis, elongation is the adding of new amino acids to the growing chain, and termination is the separation of this amino acid chain from the mRNA.

Mutations are changes in the nucleotide sequence of genes in DNA.  How do mutations arise?  Mutations are rare, chance events, but fortunately special enzymes correct most of the mistakes.  They can be caused by mutagens such as ionizing radiation (gamma and X-rays), ultraviolet radiation, and chemicals, which may act as carcinogens.  If a mutation arises in a somatic cell, it will affect only the owner of that cell and will not be passed on to offspring.  If, however, the mutation arises in a gamete, it may be passed on and thus enter the evolutionary arena. Either kind of mutation may prove to be harmful, beneficial, or neutral in its effects.

Small-scale changes in the nucleotide sequence of the DNA molecule results in gene mutations.  Mutations can be of several different general categories.

Point mutations are small (but significant) changes.often in a single nucleotide base.  This might also be known as a base-pair substitution. Sometimes this does not result in any change.  For example, if in the codon AUU, the second U was changed to C, because both sequences code for the same amino acid there would be no difference in the product.  However sometimes the results can be disastrous.  For example, in sickle cell anemia, there is a change in only one base.

Deletions remove information from the gene. A deletion could be as small as a single base or as large as the gene itself.

Insertions occur when extra DNA is added into an existing gene.

Because DNA is read in blocks of three, called frames, any change that would cause an alteration of the frame sequence can result in varying levels of mutations. Frame shift mutations result from either the addition or deletion of one or two nucleotide bases. When this occurs the "reading frame" is changed so that all the codons read after the mutation are incorrect, even though the bases themselves may be still present.  

Inversion—piece of DNA is cut out of a chromosome, turned around, and re-inserted into the gap

Translocation—chunk of DNA (often large) is removed from one chromosome and attached to another

Other types of mutations exist that cause changes in the chromosome and so are called chromosomal mutations.  These may include duplications, deletions, and inversions of segments of the chromosomes, among others. In these cases pieces of the chromosome may be cut out and then maybe reinserted flipped, not inserted at all, or added to a chromosome that is already complete.
