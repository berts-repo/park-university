Mechanisms of Evolutionary Change

Let us begin with the examination of descent with modification. Each of the following is a basic mechanism of evolutionary change.  Evolution only occurs when there is a change in gene frequency within a population over time. These genetic differences are heritable and can be passed on to the next generation—that is what really matters in evolution: long-term change.

1. Mutation
A mutation could cause parents with genes for bright green coloration to have offspring with a gene for brown coloration. That would make the genes for brown beetles more frequent in the population.

  2. Migration (also called Gene Flow)
Some individuals from a population of brown beetles might have joined a population of green beetles. That would make the genes for brown beetles more frequent in the green beetle population.

3. Genetic Drift
Imagine that in one generation, two brown beetles happened to have four offspring survive to reproduce. Several green beetles were killed when someone stepped on them and had no offspring. The next generation would have a few more brown beetles than the previous generation—but just by chance. These chance changes from generation to generation are known as genetic drift.

  4. Natural Selection
Imagine that green beetles are easier for birds to spot (and hence, eat). Brown beetles are a little more likely to survive to produce offspring. They pass their genes for brown coloration on to their offspring. So in the next generation, brown beetles are more common than in the previous generation.

All of these mechanisms can cause changes in the frequencies of genes in populations, and so all of them are mechanisms of evolutionary change. However, natural selection and genetic drift cannot operate unless there is genetic variation—that is, unless some individuals are genetically different from others. If the population of beetles were 100% green, selection and drift would not have any effect because their genetic make-up could not change.

Sources of Genetic Variation

There are four primary sources of genetic variation:

1. Mutations
2. Migration
3. Genetic Drift
4. Sex

Notice that the first three are also parts of the four basic mechanisms of evolutionary change.

A.  Mutations:
Mutations are changes in the DNA. A single mutation can have a large effect, but in many cases, evolutionary change is based on the accumulation of many mutations.  You should remember that a mutation is a change in DNA, the hereditary material of life. An organism’s DNA affects how it looks, how it behaves, and its physiology—all aspects of its life. So a change in an organism’s DNA can cause changes in all aspects of its life.

Mutations are random.
Mutations can be beneficial, neutral, or harmful for the organism, but mutations do not “try” to supply what the organism “needs.” In this respect, mutations are random—whether a particular mutation happens or not is unrelated to how useful that mutation would be.  Remember that a mutation is a change in DNA. An organism’s DNA affects how it looks, how it behaves, and its physiology—all aspects of its life. Therefore, a change in an organism’s DNA can cause changes in all aspects of its life.

Not all mutations matter to evolution.
Since all cells in our body contain DNA, there are many places for mutations to occur; however, not all mutations matter for evolution. Somatic mutations occur in non-reproductive cells and will not be passed onto offspring.  The only mutations that matter to large-scale evolution are those that can be passed on to offspring. These occur in reproductive cells like eggs and sperm and are called germ line mutations.

B. Migration (Gene Flow):
 Migration is any movement of genes from one population to another and is an important source of genetic variation.  Gene flow includes lots of different kinds of events, such as pollen being blown to a new destination or people moving to new cities or countries. If genes are carried to a population where those genes previously did not exist, gene flow can be a very important source of genetic variation.  Migration can be into a population (immigration) or out of a population (emigration).  

C. Genetic Drift:
Gene drift along with natural selection, mutation, and migration—is one of the basic mechanisms of evolution.

In each generation, some individuals may, just by chance, leave behind a few more descendents (and genes, of course!) than other individuals. The genes of the next generation will be the genes of the “lucky” individuals, not necessarily the healthier or “better” individuals. That, in a nutshell, is genetic drift. It happens to ALL populations—there’s no avoiding the vagaries of chance.

Genetic drift affects the genetic makeup of the population but, unlike natural selection, through an entirely random process. So although genetic drift is a mechanism of evolution, it doesn’t work to produce adaptations.

Genetic drift can cause populations to lose genetic variation.  Selection can only act on what variation is already in a population; it cannot create variation. 

When one or a few individuals migrate and become the founders of a new, isolated population that over time is significantly different than the original is called thefounder effect.  Even if the individuals do not migrate their population may be drastically reduced by something like flooding, drought, etc.  This restriction of genetic variability is called the bottleneck effect. Population bottlenecks occur when a population’s size is reduced for at least one generation. Undergoing a bottleneck can reduce a population’s genetic variation because of the loss of alleles. Because genetic drift acts more quickly to reduce genetic variation in small populations, undergoing a bottleneck can reduce a population’s genetic variation by a lot, even if the bottleneck doesn’t last for very many generations.

Reduced genetic variation means that the population may not be able to adapt to new selection pressures, such as climatic change or a shift in available resources, because the genetic variation that selection would act on may have already drifted out of the population.

D. Sex:
Sex can introduce new gene combinations into a population and is an important source of genetic variation.

You know that siblings are not genetically identical to their parents or to each other (except, of course, for identical twins). That’s because when organisms reproduce sexually, some genetic “shuffling” occurs, (as you saw in meiosis) bringing together new combinations of genes. 

For example, you might have bushy eyebrows, a big nose since your mom had genes associated with  bushy eyebrows, and your dad had genes associated with a big nose. These combinations can be good, bad, or neutral. If your spouse is wild about the bushy eyebrows/big nose combination, you were lucky and hit on a winning combination!

This shuffling is important for evolution because it can introduce new combinations of genes every generation. However, it can also break up “good” combinations of genes.Genetic shuffling is a source of variation. 
 

 

Natural Selection

Natural selection is the process of interaction between an organism and its environment that results in the differential rate of reproduction of different phenotypes in the population.

Natural selection is the most important agent of evolution.

Selection acts on phenotypes.

The result of natural selection is a change in the allele frequencies in a population.

Natural Selection is one of the basic mechanisms of evolution, along with mutation, migration, and genetic drift. For natural selection to occur these things must occur.

There is variation in traits. 

There is differential reproduction. Since the environment can’t support unlimited population growth, not all individuals get to reproduce to their full potential.

There is heredity. 

The offspring must be like their parents. If all of these occur, then you will have evolution by natural selection as an outcome. It is as simple as that.   

Individuals that for any reason do not mate randomly, fall into several categories including inbreeding, assortative mating (individuals mate with those that have the same phenotype), and sexual selection (males compete for the right to reproduce and the female selects).

When selection acts to eliminate both extremes from the variety of phenotypes, the result is an increase in the common intermediate phenotype and is called stabilizing selection.

If selection acts to eliminate the intermediate and results in an increase in the two more extreme phenotypes it is called disruptive selection.

Directional selection acts to eliminate one extreme and ultimately pushes the phenotype toward that extreme.

Natural selection is not all-powerful; it does not produce perfection. If your genes are “good enough,” you’ll get some offspring into the next generation—you don’t have to be perfect. No population or organism is perfectly adapted.

It is more accurate to think of natural selection as a process rather than as a guiding hand. Remember that natural selection is the simple result of variation, differential reproduction, and heredity—it is mindless and mechanistic. It has no goals; it’s not striving to produce “progress” or a balanced ecosystem.   

Variation + Differential Reproduction + Heredity = Natural Selection

This is why “need,” “try,” and “want” are not very accurate words when it comes to explaining evolution. The population or individual does not “want” or “try” to evolve, and natural selection cannot try to supply what an organism “needs.” Natural selection just selects among whatever variations exist in the population. The result is evolution.

At the opposite end scale, natural selection is sometimes interpreted as a random process. This is also a misconception. The genetic variation that occurs in a population because of mutation is random-but selection acts on that variation in a very non-random way: genetic variants that aid survival and reproduction are much more likely to become common than variants that don't. 

Natural selection is NOT random!

The Process of Evolution

The current best explanation for how evolution works is by natural selection.  Natural selection is often referred to as survival of the fittest, but it is probably more appropriate to call it reproductive fitness.  Natural selection is not one process but two: chance and selection.  Chance creates random changes in the gene pool of a population, while selection keeps much of what “works” or contributes to survival and reproduction and also eliminates much of what doesn’t.   Because of the diversity of traits of organisms and the fact that environments change with time, the organisms that have the traits that enable them to, will survive and reproduce. Of all life forms that ever existed, over 99 percent of species have died out.  Over time, new features and combinations of features or adaptations may increase in the population until the basic "type," or species has changed enough that it needs to be relabeled. 

  One example which has often been used of natural selection was of the "peppered" moths living near English industrial cities done by Kettlewell.   Kettlewell concluded that when soot darkened the tree trunks in the area, natural selection caused the dark-colored moths to become more common. Kettlewell's work has been considered a classic demonstration of natural selection in action.  Recent work now indicates that possibly Kettlewell did not fully understand what was taking place.  The predation of the moths was not the sole factor for the differential survival of light or dark moths but was ONE factor. 

One phenomenon that we observe often is that of artificial selection.  Artificial selection is the intentional reproduction of individuals in a population that have desirable traits. In organisms that reproduce sexually, two adults that possess a desired trait — such as two parent plants that are tall — are bred together. In this example, the mechanisms of heredity dictate that the next generation will consist of more tall plants than previous generations. If artificial selection is continued, all of the population will ultimately be tall. Also called selective breeding, artificial selection is perhaps best understood as a contrast to natural selection, where the random forces of nature determine which individuals survive and reproduce. In both cases, the outcome is the same: a population changes over time, so that certain traits become more common.

Evolution is the change in a population over time or, more specifically, the change is the modification of characteristics.  Because of advances in science and the science of genetics, we now describe evolution as the change in gene frequencies of a population over time resulting in a modification of characteristics.

 

There are actually two different types of evolution.

Macroevolution refers to the changes that are over a very long time and are large enough that the population is no longer able to breed with other populations, it is considered a different species. \

Microevolution is defined as the change of gene frequencies within a population.  These changes may result in an adaptation that improves the chances of surviving and reproducing in a given environment.

Biogeography & Shifting Continents

One of the areas that have provided background for the study of population change is that of biogeography.  Biogeography is the science that attempts to document and understand spatial patterns of biodiversity. It is the study of distributions of organisms, both past and present, and of related patterns of variation over the earth in the numbers and kinds of living things.

There are three fundamental processes in biogeography: evolution, extinction, and dispersal.

evolution: any irreversible change in the genetic composition of a population

extinction: the process of a species becoming permanently eliminated with no more living individuals on earth 

dispersal: the movement of organisms away from their point of origin

Of course we have already discussed the change in gene frequency over time (evolution).  Extinction is surely not a new concept to any of you.  It is important that we talk about dispersal of organisms.  Dispersal is the capacity of organisms to move from their birthplace to a new site.  It is an ecological process that is part of the life history of every species.  Without dispersal, genetic flow is limited, and species do not have a chance to adapt to a new environment. Therefore, dispersal is an important part of the process of evolution.

There are many different barriers to dispersal.  We may interpret barriers as negatives, however, without these barriers, every species would be everywhere.

Physiological barriers are those in which environments are so different from that of the original environment that a species can't survive in them for very long. Example: most freshwater fish can't survive very long in salt water.

Ecological barriers: if there's a big guy waiting to eat you next door, you will probably stay at home. Predation and competition are barriers to dispersal.

Physical barriers: it may be too far away or too difficult to get to a new site.

Recall that a species is a group of closely related and interbreeding living organisms and a population is a group of organisms of the same species populating a given area.  So if by some means a population became isolated from others of the same species and gene flow could not occur and yet the environment changed, you can see that the population would eventually be a different group of individuals.  This results in a new species being formed and is called speciation.  Eventually reproductive isolating mechanisms would develop.  These may be any aspect of body form, function or behavior that prevents interbreeding and is heritable.  There are different ways in which these are classified.

Prezygotic isolating mechanisms are anything that prevents the formation of the zygote as a result of egg and sperm union.

Mechanical isolation—incompatibility in structure of the male and female sex organs 

Behavioral isolation—this operates through courtship behavioral patterns. If one species displays a certain courtship pattern, it won’t be recognized by those of the other species. This is the mechanism which separates wolves from dogs, their courtship patterns are different in the wild 

Temporal isolation—prevents fertilization because the two different species reproduce at different times. The time periods could differ simply by hours, or by seasons. If one species reproduces in the spring, while the other reproduces in the fall, the two species aren’t able to breed

Ecological isolation—two closely related species are separated by what may be a slight difference in the niches they occupy; also called habitat isolation

 

Gamete mortality—occurs when the sperm from a male of one species dies in the reproductive tract of the female of a different species before fertilization can occur.  Sperm death is caused by hostile chemical secretions in the female reproductive tract

 

Geographical isolation—species that are found in different areas, so they are not likely to interbreed

Postzygotic isolating mechanisms are those that develop after mating occurs, a zygote is formed. 

Hybrid inviability—hybrids produced do not survive long enough to reproduce

 

Hybrid sterility—hybrids are viable (they live), but they have reduced fertility. A classic example is the mule, which is the result of a cross between a donkey and a horse.  Mules are viable, healthy animals, but they are always sterile

 

Hybrid breakdown—the hybrids produced are viable and fertile, but the offspring of the hybrids are inviable or sterile

Speciation

Think of speciation as the production of a separate species. A species is often defined as a group of individuals that actually or potentially interbreed in nature. In this sense, a species is the biggest gene pool possible under natural conditions. We will be discussing two types of speciation.

1. Geographic Isolation (allopatric speciation)
Scientists think that geographic isolation is a common way for the process of speciation to begin: rivers change course, mountains rise, continents drift, organisms migrate, and what was once a continuous population is divided into two or more smaller populations. 

This type of speciation is called allopatric speciation. 

2. Reduction of Gene Flow (sympatric speciation)
However, speciation might also happen in a population with no specific external barrier to gene flow. Imagine a situation in which a population extends over a broad geographic range, and mating throughout the population is not random. Individuals in the far west would have zero chance of mating with individuals in the far eastern end of the range. 

So we have reduced gene flow, but not total isolation. This may or may not be sufficient to cause speciation. Speciation would probably also require different selective pressures at opposite ends of the range, which would alter gene frequencies in groups at different ends of the range so much that they would not be able to mate if they were reunited.

Sympatric speciation does not require large-scale geographic distance to reduce gene flow between parts of a population. How could a randomly mating population reduce gene flow and speciate? 

Merely exploiting a new niche may automatically reduce gene flow with individuals exploiting the other niche. 

This may occasionally happen when, for example, herbivorous insects try out a new host plant. However, scientists generally question whether this type of speciation happens very often.

However there are two mechanisms for sympatric speciation:

Disruptive Selection - a single population can adapt to two or more different environmental regimes and progressively pull apart, resulting in speciation. This may be common among some insects and animal parasites. We have already discussed this type of selection.

Chromosomal Changes - this includes polyploidy (have more than one set of chromosomes). Polyploid individuals in a population are often unable to interbreed with normal individuals, and so even though there is no geographic barrier to genetic interchange, there is a biological one. This is most common in plants.

An example of sympatric speciation might be the European mosquito Anopheles group which consists of six morphologically indistinguishable species. They breed in different habitats making them reproductively isolated. Some breed in brackish water, some in running fresh water and some in stagnant fresh water; therefore, they never meet to breed. If this happens for subpopulations of a species, speciation may follow.
