# Unit 7: Overview

Evolution

Overview

Biological evolution, simply put, is descent with modification. This definition encompasses small-scale evolution (changes in gene frequency in a population from one generation to the next) and large-scale evolution (the descent of different species from a common ancestor over many generations). Our awareness of evolution, or changes in lines of descent, emerged long ago from biogeography, comparative morphology, and geology. Although they based their theory on the work of many others, Charles Darwin and Alfred Wallace proposed a theory that natural selection results in evolution. The key points of this theory are:

Populations grow in size until resources decrease and individuals compete for them. 

When individuals have forms of traits that make them more competitive, they tend to have more offspring. 

Over generations, those forms increase in frequency. 

Nature "selects" variations in traits that are more effective at helping individuals survive and reproduce.

In many pages of human history there are references to rats--Rattus--the most notorious and destructive mammalian pests of our time.  One kind or another has spread pathogens and parasites that cause bubonic plague, typhus, and other deadly infectious diseases.  In fact, the death toll from fleas that bit infected rats, then people, has exceeded the number of deaths in all wars combined

By one estimate, there is one rat for every person in urban and suburban centers of the US.  Besides spreading diseases, they chew their way through nineteen billion dollars of economic losses.  We do, however, fight back with traps, ratproof storage facilities and toxic poisons. 

During the 1950s, scientists developed baits laced with warfarin.  Warfarin interferes with blood clotting mechanisms; rats that ate the baits died within days from internal bleeding or minor scratches that would not heal.  However not all of the rats were killed.  The rats that survived were not susceptible to warfarin.

Studies have found that the difference between these two populations was, in fact, one gene.  The individuals of the resistant rats could therefore live and reproduce to yield a population of rats resistant to warfarin.  Over time, the frequency of the allele for resistance increased.  This is clearly a case of evolution; remember that evolution is simply heritable change in lines of descent.
