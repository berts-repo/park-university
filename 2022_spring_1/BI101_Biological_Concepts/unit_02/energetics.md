# Unit 2: Energetics

Energy Transformations  

Energy is the capacity to do work (you have all heard this definition before).  You also know that you can't create something from nothing.  According to the first law of thermodynamics , the total amount of energy in the universe is constant.  That means that it cannot be created or destroyed, only changed in form.  You should also know that all energy ultimately comes from the sun.  The second law of thermodynamics states that energy flows in only one direction, from high- to low-quality forms.  Each time energy is converted, some energy, usually in the form of heat, becomes unavailable for work.  As the systems lose energy they become disorganized, the measure of this disorder is called entropy. 

In any of the metabolic reactions, the starting materials are called reactants and those left at the end of the reactions are products . In general, there are two types of metabolic pathways. Biosynthetic pathways involve the assembling of small molecules into large molecules and usually involve the input of energy - endergonic (thus storage of energy). For example, simple sugars are combined to form complex carbohydrates. In degradative pathways, large molecules are broken down to form products with lower amounts of energy; the released energy can be used for cellular work (these are exergonic).

We cannot begin to discuss cell energetics without discussing a group of compounds called enzymes.Enzymes are proteins that speed up the rates of specific reactions without being changed themselves or used up. Even if the reaction is spontaneous, it won't start unless the reactants have enough internal energy to overcome the repulsive forces that keep the molecules apart. All molecules have an internal energy that is influenced by temperature and pressure. Activation energy is the minimum amount of energy that molecules must have before a reaction begins. Think of the activation energy as a barrier or a wall. See Figure 5.7 on page 80 of the text. An enzyme works to lower this barrier so that we can speed up the rate of reaction.

Enzymes are responsible for lowering the activation energy of a reaction. They do this by:

1. bringing substrates together in a position that favors a reaction and;

2. breaking existing bonds within the substrate and distorting substrate shape.

The efficiency of an enzyme depends upon the type of environment in which the enzyme is placed. Enzymes work most efficiently at specific temperatures and pH values. Most enzymes work best at body temperatures. Increasing body temperature will increase the rate of enzyme-catalyzed reactions by causing enzymes and substrates to collide more frequently. However, once body temperature reaches a certain point (about 40°C), the three-dimensional shape of the enzyme is altered. At this point, the enzyme is said to be denatured , and it loses its activity. Once an enzyme is denatured, it never regains its activity and is no longer capable of catalyzing reactions.

However temperature, pH, and salt concentration can affect the rate at which the enzyme works; either speeding it up or slowing it down. (You are examining this concept in the lab activity.) In fact these factors can denature the enzyme. To denature an enzyme means to cause the tertiary structure of (a protein) to unfold so that some of its original properties are diminished or eliminated. The substrate binds to the enzyme at the active site . This site is formed by the bonding forces within the proteins.  Recall that enzymes are proteins and there are different levels of structure for the proteins. This is why living organisms have very strict conditions in which they can live. 

Increases in temperature will speed up the rate of nonenzyme mediated reactions, and so a temperature increase speeds up enzyme mediated reactions, but only to a point. When heated too much, enzymes (since they are proteins dependent on their shape) become denatured. When the temperature drops, the enzyme regains its shape. The concentration of substrate and product also control the rate of reaction, providing a biofeedback mechanism.  Changes in pH will also denature the enzyme by changing the shape of the enzyme. Enzymes are therefore adapted to operate at a specific pH or pH range. 

 All organisms must take in energy to stay alive whether they are autotrophs (plants) or heterotrophs(animals).  Autotrophs use the energy from the sun as a source and make their own food while heterotrophs must consume either autotrophs or other heterotrophs.  The energy must then be converted into a usable form: adenosine triphosphate (ATP) the energy currency of the cell. 

 

Most of the cells and organisms require a constant energy input.  This energy drives endergonic reactions  which are paired to a few exergonic reactions which supply that energy.   The key fuel source in cell reactions is adenosine triphosphate, or ATP.   With ATP, the breaking off of the last phosphate, can release a fair amount of usable energy.

ATP is produced from ADP (adenosine diphosphate) and phosphate by several exergonic reactions in cells, but the main sources of ATP are the major energy reactions:  photosynthesis and respiration.   During photosynthesis, light energy is transformed into bonding energy between ADP and phosphate, and the glucose (the ultimate product) is made using mostly energy from ATP.  In respiration, the bond energy holding glucose together is transferred to ATP.  Light and sugars are the ultimate sources of energy in cells. 

In eukaryotes, the conversion of unusable sunlight energy into usable chemical energy is photosynthesis and is associated with the pigment chlorophyll which is located in the chloroplast. The basic process of photosynthesis may be written as:  

  6CO 2   +  6H 2 O  + light →  C 6 H 12 O 6   +  6O 2  

There are two steps of photosynthesis: light dependent and light independent reactions.  During the light dependent reactions the energy from sunlight becomes ‘fixed’ but the energy compounds are unstable (ATP & NADPH).  It is the light independent reactions that uses the products of the light dependent reactions (ATP & NADPH) to produce the carbohydrates that will serve as the primary stable fuel (and main structural constituent) of the plant. This connection between the light dependent and light independent portions of photosynthesis is very important.  Please remember that the plant DOES NOT CREATE energy but merely changes it from light energy to chemical energy.

Once the energy of the sun has been trapped into energy containing compounds, the organisms must be able to utilize these compounds to release energy.  The following is a comparison of the main types of energy-releasing pathways.

Fermentation pathways and anaerobic electron transfer can release small amounts of energy without the use of oxygen.

Aerobic respiration is the main energy-releasing pathway leading to ATP formation, oxygen dependent, and occurs in the mitochondria.

All energy-releasing pathways begin with the glycolysis reactions, which occur in the cytoplasm and produce two moleculs of pyruvate (3-carbon compounds) from glucose (a 6-carbon compound).

 

After the glucose is produced, the heterotrophs must be able to convert it into ATP.  All organisms use respiration to release energy in the form of ATP; this is called cellular respiration.  There are two main pathways of ATP formation: aerobic (oxygen dependent) and anerobic respiration.  In aerobic respiration glucose is broken down into carbon dioxide (CO 2 ) and water (H 2 0) and ultimately yields 36 ATPs.  (C 6 H 12 O 6 (glucose) + 60 2 → 6H 2 0 + 6CO 2 )  This is a very efficient process and mostly takes place within the mitochondrion. 

 

Anaerobic pathways operate when oxygen is absent (or limited) and are described as fermentation(either alcoholic or lactic acid).  Fermentation yields enough energy for many single-celled anaerobic organisms, and is sufficient for some aerobic cells when oxygen levels drop, but is insufficient for large, multicelled organisms.  Alcoholic fermentation begins with glucose and produces alcohol and carbon dioxide.  The ATP production only results in very small amounts of energy.  Certain bacteria and muscle cells have the enzymes capable of converting pyruvate to lactate, which is called lactic acid fermentation. In this process glucose is converted into lactic acid

C 6 H 12 O 6 (glucose) + 2ATP → C 3 H 6 O 3 (lactic acid) + 4ATP  

with a net gain of only 2 ATP's.  When a muscle is fatigued it produces lactic acid. Excess lactic acid in the bloodstream causes muscles to fatigue quickly, but it is not the source of muscle soreness.

By examining the diagram above, notice that all of the main energy-releasing pathways start with the same series of reactions called glycolysis in which glucose (a 6-carbon compound) is broken down into 2 molecules of pyruvate (a 3-carbon compound).  The second stage of aerobic respiration is a cyclic pathway called the Krebs cycle during which pyruvate is converted to carbon dioxide and water releasing electrons and hydrogen atoms.  It is the third stage ( electron transfer phosphorylation ) where most of the energy is released and water is produced.  Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm while the other two steps occur in the mitochondria, which is why the mitochondrion is called the powerhouse of the cell . It is not necessary to remember each of these steps and the compounds, like pyruvate, but rather understand the basic principles.  The compounds that we consume are recombined and ultimately produce ATP, carbon dioxide, water, and release waste energy.  

Energy is stored in the bonds that hold glucose together. As each of those bonds is broken, energy is released, but our bodies can never harvest all of the energy stored. Much of it is lost in the form of heat (= metabolic heat). This is why we warm up after a meal! Aerobic respiration is only 32% efficient (your car is about 25% efficient). This means that of the 686 kcals of energy in one molecule of glucose, our body is only able to harvest about 158 Kcal.

So what happens when you eat a meal?  First, glucose is absorbed into the blood.  The rising level of glucose triggers the pancreas to secrete insulin.  Insulin makes cells take up the glucose faster.  When more glucose is taken into the body than it can use, it is stored in the liver and muscle cells as glycogen.  Between meals, the blood level of glucose declines.  To continue the supply of glucose to the cells, the pancreas produces another hormone, glucagon.  Glucagon causes liver cells to convert stored glycogen into glucose and release it into the blood.  Glycogen is not the only energy reserve in the body; others are fats and even proteins.

If the system is working properly the homeostatic mechanisms will maintain the proper balance. 

However, if the blood sugar levels drop too low, hypoglycemia can happen suddenly. It is usually mild and can be treated quickly and easily by eating or drinking a small amount of glucose-rich food. If not treated, hypoglycemia can get worse and cause confusion, clumsiness, shaking, sweating, or fainting. 

Severe hypoglycemia can lead to seizures, coma, and even death. Hyperglycemia, or high blood sugar, is a very serious problem for those with diabetes. 

If this condition occurs frequently or for long periods of time damage to nerves, blood vessels and even organs can take place. Early signs may include increased thirst, difficulty concentrating, fatigue, blurred vision, and frequent urination. 

Fat conversions release ATP between meals, or during steady, prolonged exercise.  On the other hand, when too much glucose is consumed, part of it is stored in fat cells as triglycerides.  Another source of ATP comes from the breakdown of proteins into amino acids which are broken down further into compounds that can enter into the Krebs cycle.  

One of the big reasons that we need to maintain a level of glucose is the fact that our brain cells prefer this energy-rich molecule and cannot do without it for any period of time.
