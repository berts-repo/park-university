# Unit 7: Thinking About Rhetorical Concepts

Structure & Organization

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on rhetorical concepts. This unit, you will dig deeper into the rhetoric of structure and organization.

What's Wrong With The Five-Paragraph Essay?

If you are like many EN 105 students, then at some point in school, you have been assigned to compose a "five-paragraph essay." This assignment probably asked you to argue a point or establish a subject using a simple organizational pattern: an opening introductory paragraph; three body paragraphs, each arranged around a supporting reason or sub-topic; and a concluding paragraph of re-statement and summary. While this approach offers a potential starting place for organizing your writing, it is limiting -- particularly for the complex subjects you are asked to explore in college writing.

Even if you have found the five-paragraph essay structure useful in the past, it is a smart idea to leave it behind, for one simple reason: as your subject matter for writing becomes more complex, the five-paragraph essay will quickly become a hindrance, not a help. Why? Because the five-paragraph puts form ahead of meaning. The format of the five-paragraph essay, with its required supporting details, transitions, and summative conclusion, becomes more important than the writer's message. For that reason, the five-paragraph essay often makes it more difficult for you to achieve your aims as a college writer.

Two Useful Structures to Consider

When revising for structure and organization, you have many choices to consider: how you will open, how much factual background information you will include, whether or not you will use section headings, how you will organize your paragraphs, what kinds of transitions you will use, and much more. For now, spend time thinking about two possible overarching structures that can be useful for many sorts of college writing.

First is the problem-cause-solution structure. In this organizational strategy, the writer first outlines a particular problem, question, controversy, or issue. This may take one paragraph or it may take five pages -- length depends on the complexity of the problem. This opening section should not only establish that a problem exists, but also establish that the problem is in fact a problem, and therefore answering the "so what?" question.

Next, the writer examines the roots and causes of the problem. Again, doing so might take one paragraph, or it might take multiple pages.

Finally, the writer argues for a particular solution to the problem. Ideally, the writer will explain exactly how the solution will treat the cause of the problem, and how this solution trumps other proposed solutions. Often, this sort of structure concludes with a call to action, or "zooms out" to show the larger context and importance of the subject.

The problem-cause-solution pattern works especially well for writing about clearly defined problems or issues, especially social and political issues. This pattern also works well for making proposals or suggestions for action.

The second organizational structure you might find useful for college writing might be called the addressing misconceptions structure. This organizational pattern discusses a subject by explaining the flaws of common views of the subject. This pattern begins with the writer explaining the conventional wisdom or status quo around a particular issue. Then, point by point, the writer explains why and how conventional wisdom is wrong, correcting the record carefully, using evidence, reasoning, details, and examples to fully convince skeptical readers to revise their understanding of the subject matter. 

Writers using this structure might choose to include headings and sub-headings, in order to "divide" the subject into various misconceptions. (You might recall seeing this structure used in web-based "listicles" like often found on Buzzfeed and Upworthy.) The addressing misconceptions structure works best for informational texts or persuasive pieces on highly controversial subject matter. 

Revising for Structure

This unit, you will create final revisions of two major writing assignments for inclusion in your Writing Portfolio. To do so, spend time thinking about structure and organization. Consider using one of the two structural patterns described above, and use advice from the Purdue OWL and other online resources your instructor provides to help think about structure.
