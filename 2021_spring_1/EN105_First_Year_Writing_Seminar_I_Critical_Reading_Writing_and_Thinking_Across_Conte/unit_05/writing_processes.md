# Unit 5: Writing Processes

Framing Ideas

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on writing processes. This week, you will learn about common ways to frame ideas, claims, and evidence that originate in outside sources you have read.

Writing Is Conversation 

In unit 3, you learned about the "they say / I say" approach to academic writing. This mini-lecture is an extension of that idea.

By now, you know that readers expect academic writers to carefully acknowledge their sources. But documentation only goes so far. Experienced writers (both scholarly and popular) connect and frame their sources in a way that adds to their arguments. In fact, Ruszkiewicz's chapter on framing ideas and quotations provides plenty of examples of how real-life writers frame their ideas and the ideas of others.

In fact, you can use frames to create your own "They say / I say" templates, like this:

Original: Supporters of the status quo argue that passing new, tissue-specific legislation isn't necessary and the current oversight practices are enough....

Template: Supporters of _________ argue that _______ isn't necessary and ________ are/is enough.

Original: The debate over the commercialization of human biological material always comes back to one fundamental point: like it or not, we live in a market-driven society.

Template: The debate over __________ always comes back to one fundamental point: ________.

Original: More worrisome, Nicholas Carr points to a growing body of research suggesting that the cognitive abilities of those who use media frequently may actually be degraded.

Template: __________ points to a growing body of research suggesting that ________.

Original: Serious or not, he seems to value reading largely as a means to display cultural sophistication at parties.

Template: Serious or not, he seems to value _______ largely as _________.

These templates not only signal and document the writer's sources, but do something much more sophisticated: they frame the conversation. Phrases like "supporters of the status quo" and "the debate over" set the terms of the debate by loosely categorizing other perspectives into various camps or positions. Phrases like "More worrisome" and "serious or not" suggest where the writer's perspective fits into the conversation. And these frames also synthesize, or connect, various ideas and arguments in a manner that allows the writer to enter the conversation.

Tips For Framing

Think about the metaphor of the "frame" for a moment. What does a frame around a photograph or a painting do? For one thing, a frame sets boundaries: it defines what is "in" and "out." A frame sets limits. 

Why is this metaphor useful? Because when you deal with complex issues, frames help you define your terms. Rather than trying to summarize a writer's entire argument, using frames helps you make your sources work for you. Rather than simply quoting a source, framing it with phrases like "The debate over ______" or "Supporters of _______" allows to define and describe the issue.

Like many of the writing strategies you have learned this term, using frames takes practice. You will begin this unit, in your Writer's Journal, using "They Say / I Say" templates to begin framing the issue for this unit's major writing assignment.
