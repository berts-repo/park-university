# Unit 5: Critical Reading Strategies

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on critical reading strategies. This mini-lecture is designed to introduce you to strategies that will help you to understand how writers use evidence in what you read, both in college and in the everyday world. 

Am I Supposed To Be Arguing?

First-year college students often worry about making arguments. Some students worry that when they make an argument, their writing will come off as opinionated, biased, or overly personal. Others want their writing to be based on objective, verifiable patterns of evidence -- not on opinions or personal perceptions. Still others recognize that the word "argument" connotes disagreement and discord. One student even told the developer of this online course that, "Writing in college is all about proof and facts. You're not supposed to have anything except facts in your writing."

Let's be clear: Making and understanding arguments is at the heart of academic study. Every academic discipline, every scholarly field of inquiry, depends on its practitioners to make reasoned, sound arguments. When scholars in the natural sciences share their research findings with fellow scientists, they are making arguments. When business researchers present case studies on marketing strategies, they are making arguments. When social psychologists write interpretations of survey data, they are making arguments. Even this mini-lecture in this writing course is an argument, because it conveys a particular perspective on a subject and tries to persuade you to agree.

A big part of your job as a college student is to learn how to make your own arguments and how to understand, analyze, and evaluate the arguments of others. In fact, college is often distinguished from other types of education by its close attention to critical thinking, analysis, and close attention to logic and communication. The best way to start is by making sure that you can understand the arguments of others.

What Is An Argument?

But what is an argument? What does an argument consist of?

Your readings from Reading Critically, Writing Well will provide more detail on how you can read critically for arguments, but here are a few key principles to keep in mind.

First, arguments are not opinions. While an argument may be based in an individual point of view, an argument is not merely one person's take on a subject. Instead, an argument can be thought of as a claim supported by reasons why. In fact, a simple, useful model for making arguments is this:

   CLAIM
+ REASONS WHY
= ARGUMENT

While this formula is fairly straightforward, using it as a critical reading strategy can be a little tricky. Let's look at an example.

At some point in your experience as a student, you have probably heard a teacher state something like this: "Wikipedia is not a good source of information, because it is open for anyone to edit, and because it does not provide in-depth information on most subjects."

Breaking this argument into claim and reasons why is relatively simple. The first part of the sentence is the claim ("Wikipedia is not a good source of information") and everything that follows the "because" is a reason. Here is a visual representation of this argument:

CLAIM: Wikipedia is a bad source of information

REASON: Anyone can edit Wikipedia

REASON: Wikipedia does not treat subjects in detail

Now, let's look at a slightly more complicated example. Educators Darren Crovitz and W. Scott Smoot have argued that Wikipedia, in fact, is a valuable tool for learning, because, as they state, "...Wikipedia can demonstrate to students the process, importance, and excitement of real scholarship" (96).

This is a relatively straightforward claim about the value of Wikipedia for students. But do the writers provide a real reason? In fact they do -- in the very next sentences:

"Here is an authentic demonstration that knowledge isn't settled, that there are always more questions to ask and always differing perspectives on the answers. Students can see that opinions and facts aren't always easily differentiated and that uncontested facts can be used to support opposing conclusions. And they can learn that no piece of knowledge can be understood separate from its connections to other topics..." (96).

Here, we have one claim supported by three reasons why:

CLAIM: Wikipedia can teach students about what it means to be a scholar

REASON: Wikipedia shows that knowledge isn't settled

REASON: Wikipedia shows that facts can be interpreted differently

REASON: Wikipedia shows connections about topics

Knowing the reasons behind Crovitz and Smoot's claim probably helps you to understand and evaluate their point. For instance, you might object to Crovitz and Smoot's assumption about the malleability of knowledge, especially because Wikipedia strives to present its information in an objective, neutral tone -- a tone that suggests to readers that knowledge is settled. Or, you might want to understand whether or not there is any evidence that students actually do learn about interpreting facts from working with Wikipedia.

In other words, you probably can become a more critical reader of arguments by breaking them down into claims and reasons why.

One last example. In the first unit, you read Plato's famous "Allegory of the Cave." As you probably discovered as you read and discussed that text, Plato's argument is anything but simple. In fact, no single quotation easily sums up everything that Plato suggests about the nature of education. 

We can, however, paraphrase Plato's ultimate claim about education like this: education consists of moving from ignorance to understanding, and this movement is liberating.

But what reasons does Plato provide to support his claim? Does he include relevant statistics, patterns of factual evidence, or careful consideration of other writers' points of view? Of course not. Plato's support for his argument is an extended analogy that involves a cave, prisoners, a fire, suffering, and a struggle toward the light. Plato's argument depends not on listing facts or explanations, but on exploring a provocative comparison between the abstract concept of education and a tangible narrative. This argument could be represented like this:

CLAIM: Becoming educated means moving from ignorance to understanding, and this movement is liberating

REASON: Being uneducated is similar to being imprisoned in a dark cave

REASON: People naturally assume that their perceptions constitute reality, just like prisoners in a cave might

REASON: Learning to leave behind your ignorance can be painful or difficult

And so on. You can see that Plato's argument works differently that Crovitz and Smoot's. For one thing, Plato's argument is more complex, depending on a series of reasons that are not necessarily able to be proven. Does this mean that Plato's argument is faulty?

Far from it. Plato asks his readers to use their own reasoning and experience to judge the merit of his argument. He assumes that once his readers accept the initial comparison (learning is like being freed from prison), then they will compare his claim to their own experiences, and judge his logic based on their own sense of what is true.

As you will learn in your readings this unit, most arguments in college and everyday writing are complicated and not easily broken down into parts. But if you can identify claims and reasons why in everything that you read, your reading will be more powerful, critical, and even efficient. As you read during this unit -- and as you move forward in college and in life -- use this formula as a critical reading strategy to pick apart the arguments made by each of the authors you will read.
