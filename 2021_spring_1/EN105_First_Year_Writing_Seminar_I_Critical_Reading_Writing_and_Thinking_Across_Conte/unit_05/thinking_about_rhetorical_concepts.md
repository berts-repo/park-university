# Unit 5: Thinking About Rhetorical Concepts

What Counts As Evidence?

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on rhetorical concepts. This unit, you will think about what counts as evidence in various genres of academic writing.

Arguments & Evidence

As you learned in another mini-lecture, most arguments can be broken down into a simple equation:

   CLAIM
+ REASONS WHY
= ARGUMENT

But what makes a reason a good reason? When you are trying to include evidence to support your claims, how do you know what counts as evidence?

The answer depends, to a large degree, on audience, context, purpose, and genre. In the scholarly genres you will be working in during upper-level coursework in your major, good evidence usually means ideas and information discovered in scholarly sources (like journal articles or edited collections), analysis of primary sources (like historical artifacts, images, maps, newspaper articles, and other documents), and your own original research.

But each academic field has its own guidelines and traditions about what might be considered "good evidence," and those guidelines vary widely. For instance, if a scholar of literature attempts to argue for a particular interpretation of a Shakespearian sonnet, her evidence will be the text itself, along with a careful description of how she arrived at her interpretation or "reading." But an experienced psychology researcher will rarely make any kind of claim without carefully citing either previous psychology findings or analyzing his own original research. Because the contexts and purposes are different, what counts as "evidence" changes from field to field. One of your jobs as a college student majoring in a particular subject will be to determine what "counts" as evidence in your particular major.

What Counts As Evidence in This Class?

Great question -- and good news! This is a 100-level class that asks students to engage with the genre of the personal essay, which means that your own individual experiences "count" as evidence in your writing. EN 105 is designed to help you learn about writing by starting with what you know -- your own thoughts, ideas, and experiences.

Your probably relied on your own past experience in your first two major writing assignments, and perhaps even in your third. For this unit's major writing assignment, personal experience might serve you well again. You might be able to to add to the critical conversation by examining your own experience with the internet.

But, in order to build a solid argument and establish a sense of ethos, you will also want to seek at least some outside evidence to support your claims. Your evidence might be as simple as finding reliable statistical data about how many people worldwide use the Internet. Or, you can "test" your own experiences against researchers' findings, by looking to see your perceptions match what patterns of data. You might also try to check to see if any popular news sources have reported on recent neuroscience findings that connect to Nicholas Carr's article and the question of whether Google is "making us stupid."

In this unit's discussion, pay close attention to the claims and points that your classmates make, and try to imagine how such claims could be "tested" against outside sources. Good luck!
