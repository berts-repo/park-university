# Unit 3: Writing Processes

They Say / I Say

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on writing processes. This unit, you will learn about using the "They Say / I Say" technique to find a way to enter a critical conversation.

Writing Is Conversation?

Twentieth-century rhetorical scholar Kenneth Burke once compared academic discourse to a conversation at a party:

Imagine that you enter a parlor. You come late. When you arrive, others have long preceded you, and they are engaged in a heated discussion, a discussion too heated for them to pause and tell you exactly what it is about. In fact, the discussion had already begun long before any of them got there, so that no one present is qualified to retrace for you all the steps that had gone before. You listen for a while, until you decide that you have caught the tenor of the argument; then you put in your oar. Someone answers; you answer him; another comes to your defense; another aligns himself against you.... The hour grows late, you must depart. And you do depart, with the discussion still vigorously in progress.

Kenneth Burke's analogy seems to suggest that academic writing is not so much a debate as a discussion. Academic writing, then, can be thought of as entering into a conversation about a particular subject -- a conversation that precedes your contribution, and that will carry on after you are gone.

It's a striking analogy, and a useful one. For one thing, you might find that the conversation analogy helps you to make arguments. Rather that stating a position and supporting it with reasons, you might think of beginning your argument by summarizing other perspectives and then offering your own.

They Say / I Say

In fact, writing scholars Gerald Graff and Cathy Birkenstein published an entire textbook that uses the metaphor of conversation to teach college students to write. Graff and Birkenstein call their approach the "They Say / I Say" method. This method of writing essentially consists of responding to other writers' perspectives with your position, using common rhetorical "moves" or templates: first by stating what "they say," and then stating what "I say."

Graff and Birkenstein's templates look sort of like Mad Libs -- common phrases that many different subjects can be "plugged into." Here are a few examples:

Common sense seems to dictate that __________. However, a deeper consideration suggests that _________ is true.

The standard way of thinking about Topic X has it that __________.

In discussions of X, one controversial issue has been __________. On the one hand, __________ argues __________. On the other hand, __________ contends __________. Others even maintain __________. My own view is __________.

When it comes to the topic of __________, most of us will readily agree that __________. Where this agreement usually ends, however, is on the question of __________. Whereas some are convinced that __________, others maintain that __________.

You can probably imagine using any or all of these templates in many different writing projects. Many college students find them to be valuable tools. 

While writing teachers debate the value of these templates for student writing, there is no denying that using "They Say / I Say" templates can jump-start your writing process, especially when you are engaged in a writing project that argues a position or enters a critical conversation. In this unit's Writer's Journal, you will practice doing just that: using templates to define the critical conversation and to develop your own perspective, in preparation for this unit's major writing assignment.
