# Unit 3: Thinking About Rhetorical Concepts

Working With Sources

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on rhetorical concepts. This mini-lecture is designed to help you understand the theories and concepts behind the reading and writing strategies you are practicing. These rhetorical concepts are broadly applicable ideas that can help you succeed as a reader, writer, speaker, and communicator in many different contexts. This unit, you will explore various ways to work with sources.

Working With Sources: The Good, the Bad, & the Ugly

It's no secret that college students struggle to incorporate sources into their writing. Researchers from  [link: http://www.citationproject.net/] The Citation Project are in the midst of conducting an extensive study of how real-life students use sources in their writing. Their preliminary findings are striking. Among their discoveries from analyzing nearly 2,000 citations from 174 students at over a dozen colleges and universities:

Students most often use sources through direct quotation, by copying sentences from sources and pasting them into their writing, either with or without quotation marks and with or without valid citations

Nearly half of the sources cited by students are considered to be the equivalent of five pages or fewer

The vast majority of student citations (almost 70%) come from the first two pages of the source -- no matter if the source is a three-page news article or a thick textbook  

This is not a rosy picture: students are quoting instead of paraphrasing and summarizing; they are using brief sources; and they are probably not reading much of their sources. The researchers conclude that college student writing is characterized by a lack of depth. The researchers go so far as to state that students "lack the critical reading and thinking skills necessary to engage with the ideas of others" (Jamieson).

Ouch! 

But things are not quite as bad as they seem. For one thing, learning to write with sources is a process, and incorporating quotations is a good place to start. In fact, using sources well in your writing is something that will probably take you years to learn -- do not expect to master this difficult academic writing skill in a single class!

Do you recognize your own writing process in the results of the study? You make a claim, and decide that your claim needs support. So, you use a search engine or database to locate an outside source on your subject that you can quote. You skim your source for possible quotations, find one, and copy-paste it into your writing. Then, you compose a sentence or two of explanation, stick a citation on top of it, and move along with your argument.

Most college writing teachers have observed this pattern in student writing. In fact, the developer of this online course did the same thing when he was a college student! 

This is not the best way of working with sources in writing, although it is a start. In fact, instead of thinking about working with sources, you might think of getting your sources to work for you, by using them as entry points into the critical conversation on a subject.

Sources & the Critical Conversation

In this unit's mini-lecture on writing processes, you read a quotation from scholar Kenneth Burke. Burke argued that academic writing is best thought of as a conversation. In fact, that metaphor -- writing is conversation -- is a useful way to think about working with sources in your writing, particularly in writing that makes an argument.

Subjects don't arise out of thin air. Instead, the subject of almost any writing you do has already been debated and discussed by many writers before you. That debate and discussion gives the subject shape, and provides a structure and context for your perspective on the issue. Experienced writers are not content to simply state a point and move ahead. Instead, experienced writers indicate that they understand the "tenor of the argument," to use Kenneth Burke's phrase, by providing an overview of other perspectives on the subject.

Consider the difference between the following statements:

Wikipedia might be a decent tool for learning basic information, but when students rely on it as a research tool, they fail to learn more than surface-level facts about a subject.

Crovitz and Smoot fail to understand one important aspect of Wikipedia: while it might a decent tool for learning basic information, when students rely on it as a research tool, they fail to learn more than surface-level facts about a subject.

The difference is simple: the phrase "Crovitz and Smoot fail to understand...." But what does this phrase add to the writing? This phrase shows readers that the writer has really engaged with the argument of Crovitz and Smoot, and that the writer sees how her position on the subject fits into the larger critical conversation.

You can use this model in your own writing. Do not simply include quotations from sources in order to "prove" your point; rather, use sources to show how your position on the subject compares with that of others.

As you read for this class, look for how writers integrate sources into their writing, and emulate their techniques.
