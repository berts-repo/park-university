The Rhetoric of Style

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on rhetorical concepts. This mini-lecture is designed to help you consider the rhetorical implications of style and economy. 

The Rhetoric of Style

Let's return to our unit 1 definition of rhetoric: "the art of using language to persuade." As you have learned in this course, nearly every aspect of your writing has a rhetorical effect, in the sense that every choice you make as a writer leads your readers to draw particular conclusions. For instance, your choice of title sends a message to a reader, and usually allows your reader to interpret your writing before even beginning to read your work!

What, then, are the rhetorical implications of high, middle, and low style? 

As you might guess from the terms "high," "middle," and "low," style can be a matter of value judgment. So-called "low" style -- with its characteristic idiosyncrasies, humor, and colloquialisms -- can come across as unprofessional or out-of-place. And yet "low" style is often used by contemporary novelists, creative writers, and even screenwriters to create a personal, unique mood. Ruszkiewicz refers to the novelist Sherman Alexie as an example, and you can probably think of many other writers who use "low" style to artistic ends. (Shakespeare comes to mind as a writer who often mixes high and low styles to great comedic effect.)

The point here is that while all writing styles are valid for various purposes, in college writing you should be careful to use a writing style that matches your goals and purposes -- and the learning goals of any particular assignment. Many readers notice your writing style before they notice the content of what you have to say. Many student writers have seen their work dismissed not on the basis of its logic, evidence, or argument, but on the simple basis of its prose style. This might sound unfair -- in fact, it is unfair -- but it is the truth.

Yes, but so what? Isn't writing all about individual choices?

Yes and no. In many college writing courses, you will be asked to use writing for two broad purposes: as a way to learn something new, and as a way to demonstrate your knowledge and understanding. These two purposes are quite different, and so require different styles.

In writing-to-learn exercises, students are usually given some amount of leeway in terms of style and grammar. Writing-to-learn exercises are usually informal or impromptu small-stakes writing exercises designed to help students explore a new concept. (Your Writer's Journal entries in this class are examples of writing-to-learn assignments.)

You might think of writing-to-learn as "thinking on the page." For instance, most take-home quizzes, reading guides, learning logs, reading journals, and brief response papers are used as writing-to-learn activities. In these assignments, your goal is not necessarily to communicate to a reader, but to explore an idea or a concept. Your teachers hope that you will learn from the process of completing these activities. This doesn't mean you should ignore style. Rather, you should focus on completing the exercise in whatever style seems appropriate to you. What's important in a writing-to-learn exercise is that you learn from doing -- not that you fully support an argument, avoid grammatical errors, or write with a cohesive style.

Demonstrating-your-knowledge writing exercises are generally longer, more formal, more in-depth, and more focused on audience and genre. A research project, a formal argument, a business proposal, or a term paper -- all of these are examples of college writing assignments that ask you to show off what you know. For these assignments, you will want to pay very close attention to style, using a high or middle style, as appropriate for the course and assignment.

In this unit's reading quiz and Writer's Journal, you will practice editing prose for style and clarity. Good luck!
