# Unit 8: Writing Processes

Editing Your Prose

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on writing processes. This unit, you will consider a few strategies for editing your prose.

Revising Vs. Editing

So far, this class has focused on inventing ideas, composing drafts, and revising. You have not spent much time editing your writing on the sentence level. Why not? Because writing research suggests that students make the biggest improvements when they concentrate on learning about rhetorical concepts, writing in particular contexts on particular subjects, and making arguments. In fact, much writing research shows that student writing improves at the sentence level when students focus on these more global concerns.

Revising, in this course, means looking again at your writing from a broad perspective, and then adjusting your writing to better meet your aims. Editing, on the other hands, zooms in close to the level of the paragraph, the sentence, the phrase, even the word choice. In other words, revising means looking at the big picture of your writing, and editing means looking at the details.

Learning to edit your own writing for style, clarity, and economy is an important skill -- although a difficult one to master. Each writer has his or her own method to editing, but your readings this unit give you some things to think about. Above all, good editing means specificity and clarity.

Editing for Subject

A good trick is to make sure that the subject of each sentence (in other words, the main noun) is the most important noun in the sentence. Most sentences work best when the subject is something capable of action. Consider the following examples:

Original: It is an essential skill for college to be curious.
Subject of Original: It

Edited: Curiosity is an essential skill for college students.
Subject of Edited: Curiosity

Original: Without the ability to write and communicate clearly, the process of finding a job can be difficult.
Subject of Original: the process

Edited: Without the ability to write and communicate clearly, many graduates have difficulty finding a job.
Subject of Edited: many graduates

In each of the edited examples, the most important noun occupies the subject position in the sentence. Editing for subject is a simple, straightforward editing technique that can lend power and clarity to your writing. 

Editing for Pronouns

Another way to approach editing your prose is to focus on pronouns. Pronouns stand in place for a noun; that original noun is called the antecedent. For instance, in the following sentence, "her" and "she" are both pronouns; "The doctor" is the antecedent.

After the doctor finished her rounds, she finishing charting and placed orders.

Pronouns include words like "he," "she," "it," "they," "them," "their," "our," "it," and so on and so forth.

There are about a dozen types of pronouns, but the most important aspect of pronouns is agreement. Agreement means that pronouns should match their antecedents in number (singular or plural) and person (first-person, second-person, third-person). But pronouns are often ambiguous, as in the following examples:

After I plugged the printer into the computer, it sputtered and died.

As a nurse, we are obligated to care for the entire patient.

On the weather station, it said that storms would hit Kansas City this afternoon.

Each pronoun either disagrees with its antecedent, or is unclear. These sentences could be edited into the following:

After I plugged it into the computer, the printer sputtered and died.

Nurses are obligated to care for the entire patient.

The weather station said that storms would hit Kansas City this afternoon.

In this unit's quiz and Writer's Journal, you will practice editing own prose for subject, pronouns, and other suggestions for improving the clarity of your writing.
