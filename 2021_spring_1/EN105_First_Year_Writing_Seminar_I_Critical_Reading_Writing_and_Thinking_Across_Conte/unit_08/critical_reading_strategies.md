# Unit 8: Critical Reading Strategies

Identifying Style in What You Read

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on critical reading strategies. In this lecture, you will learn how to draw conclusions from the prose style of what you read.

Writing with Style in College

You will learn a little more about editing your prose for style later in this unit. For now, it is worth considering the concept of style in your college reading and writing.

"Style" can be a contentious term for college students and professors. Many professors require that students follow a particular "style" guide, such as those created by the Modern Language Association, the American Psychological Association, the Chicago Manual of Style, or the Council of Science Editors. While these style guidebooks do provide suggestions for sentence-level composition and phrasing, usually these academic "styles" consist more of documentation guidelines and rules for formatting and document design. Luckily, your Easy Writer textbook contains highly detailed instructions for crafting documents that follow MLA, APA, Chicago, and CSE citation and formatting styles.

But prose style is much more complicated than simply following a list of rules for documenting sources. Even though a psychology class and a business marketing class might ask you to "use APA style," you will still have to make choices on the sentence and paragraph levels about prose style.

In general, college writing falls into the categories of high style -- but not always. College students are sometimes asked to play the role of an expert, informing an audience about a technical subject. In this purpose, a high style, with an impersonal perspective, direct statements, and technical language is appropriate. Other times, college students are asked to make arguments on controversial subjects. (Does this sound familiar from your EN 105 class?) In these cases, a middle style is often appropriate, although some classes and disciplines have particular guidelines for writers. Other times, college students may be asked to reflect on particular subjects in whatever way they wish, in which case a low style may be useful.

In general, if you have questions about what style to use in a college writing assignment, you can usually ask your instructor for examples or models of the sorts of writing you are asked to do. Read these models critically, analyzing them for genre, style, and even rhetorical strategies. Use your analysis of model texts to make decisions about how to meet the goals of your writing assignments.
