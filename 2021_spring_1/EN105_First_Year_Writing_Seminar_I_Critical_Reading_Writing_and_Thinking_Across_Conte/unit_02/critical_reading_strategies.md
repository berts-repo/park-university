# Unit 2: Critical Reading Strategies

Identifying Genres

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on critical reading strategies. This mini-lecture is designed to introduce you reading strategies that will help you understand, interpret, and evaluate what you read, both in college and in the everyday world. This week, you will learn to identify common genres of writing you may encounter in college.

What's a Genre?

In the first week of almost every college class in the United States, students are provided a document called a syllabus. College teachers often place enormous significance on the syllabus, often telling students to "check the syllabus" before asking questions about assignments or class policies.

Okay, but what exactly is a syllabus? What makes a syllabus a syllabus?

We can use the rhetorical categories of writer, audience, and subject to begin our syllabus analysis. Think about a syllabus you have received for a class, and answer the following questions:

Who created this syllabus? 

Who is the primary intended readers of this syllabus?

What is the subject matter or topic of this syllabus?

Most likely, you decided that your teacher, instructor, or professor wrote the syllabus for you and your fellow students. You probably decided that the subject of your syllabus is your course: the syllabus serves to document what will happen in this course.

But that analysis does not quite get to the bottom of what a syllabus is. Let's think about the purpose of a syllabus. When, where, and why is a syllabus created? What purpose does a syllabus try to accomplish?

Once again, the answer is fairly clear: a syllabus documents a set of mutual expectations for students and teachers in a college course. For instance, a syllabus does all of the following:

Provides an outline of students can expect to learn in the course, including major topics, assignments, and activities

Describes the teacher's expectations for student behavior in class

Documents class policies, including late work, partial credit, attendance, and academic honesty

Describes exactly how the teacher will figure final grades

Informs students of how to contact their professor

Lists textbooks and other required materials

Suggests outside resources that may assist students

The syllabus is a good example of a writing genre, because its writer, audience, subject, and purpose is so clear. In fact, a genre might be defined as a category of texts that share distinct, common features and purposes.

Knowing the genre of a text can give you hints about what to expect as you read that text. For instance, in reading a syllabus you probably expect:

Straightforward, professional diction

Complete sentences that use standard syntax

An organized structure, using headings and sub-headings

Relatively few images, charts, or diagrams

Very few references to outside sources

Direct explanations

You also know what not to expect from a syllabus. You wouldn't expect action-packed narration, snappy dialogue, evocative description, or figurative language in a syllabus. That kind of writing belongs in the realm of storytelling. And you shouldn't expect detailed logic about class policies or scholarly evidence analysis about assignment design. That's the sort of writing that belongs in a research report or a textbook. In fact, when you do encounter narrative or argumentative writing in a syllabus, you probably sit up and pay attention. That's because you recognize that a genre convention is being broken.

Common Genres Encountered by College Students

"Genre" is a notoriously tricky term to define. Many students say, "I know it when I see it." One popular textbook suggests that genres are "formulas" or "traditions" (Ruszkiewicz 43-44). A  [link: http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/genre] common online dictionary defines genre as "a category...of composition characterized by a particular style, form, or content" (Merriam-Webster). [link: http://twp.duke.edu/twp-writing-studio/resources-students/genres]  Duke University's writing program web site argues that genres are "categories of written texts that have recognizable patterns, syntax, techniques, and/or conventions." And in this week's mini-lecture on rhetorical concepts, you will read read more about how genres are shaped by their social contexts.

Writing teacher John Ruszkiewicz argues that there are three common genres encountered by college students: narratives, reports, and arguments. Indeed, much of the reading you will be asked to complete for school falls into one of these broad categories. Each of these genres shares common features and purposes, and your job as a critical reader changes depending on the genre.

But texts aren't usually labeled as "Narrative" or "Report." Instead, one of your jobs as a critical reader is to place what you read within a genre, and use your understanding of that genre to analyze and evaluate the text. Use the chart below to understand how genres differ.

The critical questions to ask of each type of text are different, depending on the genre. Why? Because genres carry expectations. Readers expect different things from a narrative than from an argument. So it only makes sense to analyze a narrative differently than you would analyze an argument. In fact, if you were to judge a narrative personal essay by its persuasiveness and use of explicit reasoning, you would probably miss the point of the personal essay!

Of course, within these three broad genres are many, many sub-genres -- as you will discover as you read and write in this and other college courses. (Duke University's writing program web site lists  [link: http://twp.duke.edu/twp-writing-studio/resources-students/genres] 44 common college writing genres -- and it is not an exhaustive list!)

As you encounter readings in other classes or on the web, start by sorting your reading into one of Ruszkiewiecz's three categories, and use the critical reading questions above to better understand your reading.
