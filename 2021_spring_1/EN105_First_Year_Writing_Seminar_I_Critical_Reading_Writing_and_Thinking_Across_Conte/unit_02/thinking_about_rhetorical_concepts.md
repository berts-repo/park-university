Genre & Social Context

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on rhetorical concepts. This mini-lecture is designed to help you understand the theories and concepts behind the reading and writing strategies you are practicing. These rhetorical concepts are broadly applicable ideas that can help you succeed as a reader, writer, speaker, and communicator in many different contexts. This unit, you will explore further how genres are embedded in distinct social contexts.

Genre and Social Context

In this unit's mini-lecture on critical reading strategies, you learned to identify the genre of a work that you read by placing it in one of Ruszkiewicz's categories of narrative, report, or argument. Then, you learned how your job as a critical reader changes, depending on the genre of text you have encountered.

But understanding genre is so much more than placing a text in a pre-existing category. In fact, some scholars of rhetoric suggest that genres get their power from how they address social contexts.

Let's return to our example of the syllabus. In the earlier mini-lecture, you learned that the syllabus serves a particular social need, by setting mutual expectations among a group of students and a teacher. In fact, this social situation (a group of students enrolled in a class with a teacher) happens often, so often that the genre of the syllabus has evolved to meet that need. In other words, the genre of the syllabus addresses a recurring social situation.

The rhetorical scholar Carolyn Miller was one of the first to describe genres in this way. According to Miller, "what we learn when we learn a genre is not just a pattern of forms" (165). What we learn, Miller argues, is "to understand better the situations in which we find ourselves" (165). Miller's point is that when you understand a genre, you understand social context.

What does this mean for college students? It means that you should pay close attention to how what you read emerges from social context and serves a social purpose. Genres have evolved among groups of people to serve a purpose, achieve a goal, solve a problem, or address a recurring situation. The conventions of a genre (for example, structure, form, and style) are usually designed to meet that social purpose in a particular context.

You probably already understand that the way you evaluate a web news article should be very different from the way you evaluate a U.S government report. That's because the two genres address very different social contexts.

Smart readers do not expect news articles to be exhaustive reports on a subject matter. Nor do they expect sophisticated search engines or detailed charts in listicles intended for information. Instead, smart readers consider the social context when reading any particular work.

You probably don't find most college textbooks to be entertaining, humorous, or moving. That's because textbooks do not attempt to serve the social purpose of entertaining an audience. It would be silly to judge your textbooks for failing to serve a purpose that they does not even attempt to fulfill!

Genres & Language Communities

This unit's major writing assignment will ask you to describe a language community to which you belong. One way to understand your community is to understand the connection between the language of your community and your community's shared values.

Think about the language and genres that your community uses to "talk to itself." For instance, most professional workplace communities use genres like e-mails, memos, proposals, planning reports, action plans, standard operating procedure manuals, and PowerPoint presentations to communicate.

But how do these genres connect to shared values? Well, there is no rule dictating that workplaces must e-mail, etc., to communicate. Rather, e-mail and the other genres both serve a social need and express certain values. For instance, most workplaces put heavy emphasis on careful documentation of work processes. So, the genres of the workplace rely heavily on documentation, reflecting the values of clarity, detail, and organization. 

A different language community might use different language. Participants in many sports use highly detailed, specialized technical language that not only describes the sport, but also signals who is an "insider" in the community. Knowing the definition of a "6-4-3 double play" or being able to understand what's meant by "the yips" shows that you are a member of the baseball community. Likewise, most distance runners are able to define and use terms like "junk miles," "fartlek," "overpronate," and "bonk" -- terms that might be nonsense to people who are not members of the community. Sports terminology often signals who is "in" and who is "out," and creates a social bond among insiders. And anyone who has played team sports knows that community bonds are important for team success.

The point is that language and genre are deeply connected to community. Understanding that connection will be your goal in this unit's discussion and major writing assignment.
