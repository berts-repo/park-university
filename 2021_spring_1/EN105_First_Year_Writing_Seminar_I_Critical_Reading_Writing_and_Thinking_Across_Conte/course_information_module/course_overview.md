The Writing Program at Park University

English 105 is the first in a three-course writing program required of all students at Park University. EN105 focuses on critical reading, writing, and thinking practices. EN106 asks you to apply those skills to common academic tasks that involve integrating research into your writing. Finally, EN306 (or your department’s equivalent course) engages you in the particular work of writers in your discipline. The writing program reflects the University’s mission to “to think critically, communicate effectively, demonstrate a global perspective and engage in lifelong learning and service to others” ( [link: http://www.park.edu/about-park/mission-and-vision.html] About Park).

The faculty of the  [link: https://www.park.edu/academics/explore-majors-programs/english-degree/] Department of English & Modern Languages developed the following philosophy statement to articulate our beliefs about writing, writers, and writing instruction. These beliefs are reflected in the content of all EN courses.

The English Department at Park University believes that:

writing is a social and collaborative act;

writing is an act of exploration and discovery;

writing is thinking;

writing is a recursive and developmental process;

writing is a set of complex skills involving language;

writing is the work of all academic disciplines;

writing is a life-long learning endeavor.

Writing instruction should include:

one-to-one consultation;

peer response;

feedback and commentary on work in progress;

attention to purpose and audience;

emphasis on both process and product;

opportunity for student reflection.

Student writers need:

time to write;

positive encouragement;

freedom to choose a topic.

Writing Teachers must:

be writers themselves;

coach and mentor student writers with respect and humor;

engage in life-long learning of the craft of writing and of the teaching of writing.

Underlying these belief statements is a vision of writing as a process. That process, however, is different for each writer and cannot be codified, or mapped out in a linear formula. In EN105 we will discuss pre-writing, drafting, revising, and editing, however, we will do so with the recognition that those “stages” of writing as a process are enacted differently by every writer in every writing situation. Similarly, the art and science of writing is not something to be “mastered” in one eight-unit term, a college career, or even a lifetime. Our skill as writers is developed over time as we write for varied audiences and purposes, as we read, and as we learn from the feedback given to us by our readers.

 

The Curriculum of EN105

Contrary to the old adage, successful writers are made, not born—that is, writing is a learn-able, teach-able skill improved by practice over time. You are already a writer, with valuable experiences and skills. English 105 will give you the opportunity to continue your development as a writer through informal and formal writing assignments for a variety of audiences—all informed by our course readings.

Most of our time this term will be spent writing and responding to the writing of others. This is a discussion-based course, which means that all participants are expected to engage in discussions about the reading and writing assignments, will share their own work in progress, and will respond to the work of others.

English 105 focuses on those qualities common to writing in a variety of settings, academic and non-academic: process, focus, development, rhetorical awareness, and conventions/mechanics. In this course, we emphasize utilizing personal experience/observation as the basis for crafting a persuasive message.

 

Course Details

English 105: First Year Writing Seminar I: Critical Reading, Writing, and Thinking Across Contexts

Course Description: An introduction to the recursive processes of writing, the course will emphasize discovery and writing-as-thinking. Students will engage various personal and academic genres, with attention to analyzing the audience and purpose for different writing situations. Course readings expose students to a variety of genres and topics from a range of cultural contexts to promote critical thinking and dialogue. Peer response, reflection and revision are emphasized through a summative course portfolio.

Credit Hours: 3

Prerequisite Courses: None

Core Learning Outcomes

Process: Apply writing processes (e.g., pre-writing, drafting, revising, editing, proof-reading) and collaborative strategies (e.g., discussion, peer review, critique, conferencing) to a variety of writing tasks, including the use of writing as a tool for inquiry.

Focus: Develop a controlling idea/thesis for the personal essay.

Development: Through the close analysis of diverse model texts, identify strategies for supporting, developing, clarifying, and extending the focus of an essay, especially through the integration of cultural perspectives and personal experience.

Rhetorical Strategies: Analyze a variety of rhetorical situations to respond appropriately to diverse audiences, purposes, and genres.

Conventions: Identify common formats and conventions (e.g., structure, tone, mechanics) for different genres of writing, including electronic texts.

Course Assignments

This is an overview of the major assignment categories this term. More details on major assignments can be found on the Assignments page in this Course Information Module. For each assignment, you will receive detailed instructions and a grading rubric in the Unit module.

Readings
Each unit, you will complete several readings. Readings will come from Web resources and  [link: http://www.park.edu/library] Park University library holdings.

Mini-Lectures
Each unit, you will read several "mini-lectures." These mini-lectures are designed to match the three aspects of our course: critical reading, writing processes, and thinking about rhetorical concepts. Don't worry -- each mini-lecture is quite short! 

Quizzes
Most units, you will take a brief, self-graded quiz to gauge your understanding of your reading assignments and mini-lectures. 

Discussions
Most units, you will take part in class discussion. All discussions will take place in an asynchronous, threaded discussion format. You will be asked to make an initial post that responds to a question or prompt, and then engage in follow-up discussion. Specific details for each discussion's requirements are contained within the discussion prompt. 

Peer Reviews
Some units, you will engage in peer review of rough drafts of your major writing assignments. In general, peer reviews will ask you to read two classmates' drafts and provide a written response to each classmate, using a question guide. Specific instructions and grading rubrics are provided in each peer review assignment.

Writer's Journal
Each unit, you will make an entry into your Writer's Journal that responds to a prompt. Most Writer's Journal entries ask you to try out a new writing process activity or to reflect on your reading that unit. Writer's Journal entries are graded pass-fail: if you complete the prompt, you will receive full credit for the Writer's Journal entry.

Major Writing Assignments & Revision Projects
You will complete four major writing assignments this term, along with two revision projects. Each major writing assignment will addresses a particular question, problem, issue, or prompt. During Unit 1, you will tell a true story about a time you learned something important. During Unit 2, you will describe a community you belong to, focusing on the language habits used by members of your community. During Unit 3, you will explain a concept you know well to a group of classmates. During Unit 4, you will revise one of your first three major writing assignments, using the rhetorical concept of the three appeals to guide your work in revision. During Unit 5, you will enter a critical conversation around how the internet affects learning and cognition. During Units 6 and 7, you will engage in further revision, and begin work on your reflection for your Writing Portfolio. For each assignment, you will receive detailed instructions, including a grading rubric.

Writing Portfolio
Your major goal this term is to create a portfolio of your best writing from the class. To do so, you will choose your two best major writing assignments from this class and revise them further, using feedback from your instructor and your classmates to guide your work. You will also compile artifacts of your writing process, to help illustrate how you went about creating your writing assignments. Finally, you will compose a detailed reflection that describes your writing process and identifies specific lessons about writing you learned this term. 

Student Opinion of Teaching Survey

Background Knowledge & Technology Requirements

Prerequisite Knowledge: To be successful in English 105, you should have the following background knowledge:

Reading comprehension in English

Although this class will help you to develop your critical reading, writing, and thinking skills, you will need to have basic proficiency in reading English before entering this class.

Basic writing proficiency in English

You will learn a lot about writing this term, but to succeed, you will need to have basic proficiency in writing sentences and paragraphs in English before entering this class.

To succeed in this class, you will need internet access. Class discussions, correspondence with your instructor, and many assignments will require the use of Web/internet resources.

Ability to post to Canvas discussions
