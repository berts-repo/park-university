# Course Policies

Due Dates

In general, you have several standing due dates in the course:

In some units, by Tuesday at 11:59 pm (Central time), post a rough draft to the peer review discussion.

By Wednesday at 11:59 pm (Central time), take the reading quiz (if applicable) and post to the unit's discussion.

By Friday at 11:59 pm (Central time), post your Writer's Journal entry.

Most units, by Sunday at 11:59 pm (Central time), turn in either a major writing assignment or a revision project.

Depending on your instructor's policies about late work, you may not receive credit for assignments turned in after the due dates. In any case, it is important to keep track of due dates and make plans to meet them.

Response Time to Graded Assignments
In alignment with with Park University policies for online classes, your instructor will grade your "small-stakes" assignments (quizzes, discussions, peer reviews, and Writer's Journal entries) within 48 hours of the due date. Your major writing assignments will require more time for your instructor to provide substantive response and feedback. You can expect your instructor to respond to your major writing assignments within 6 days of the deadline. The instructor for your section of EN 105 may post an announcement with more details about responses to major writing assignments. 

File Formats
To allow your instructor the ability to post marginal commentary, you must submit your major writing assignments as .doc, .docx, or .pdf files. You can save files in these formats with most word processors, including Web-based programs like  [link: http://docs.google.com] Google Docs ,  [link: https://office.live.com/start/Word.aspx] Word Online, and  [link: https://www.openoffice.org/] Open Office . If you are using a web-based file storage system like  [link: https://drive.google.com] Google Drive or  [link: http://www.dropbox.com/] Dropbox , be sure to learn exactly how to transfer files from your storage system to Canvas.

Academic Honesty & Plagiarism

Since this is a writing class, one of the subjects you will consider this term is academic honesty in writing. Despite what you may have been told, academic honesty is a complex concept, and requires more than simply avoiding plagiarism. In fact, in this class, you will learn that "plagiarism" is a hotly debated concept among writers and writing scholars. You will see that standards for academic honesty in writing are sometimes difficult for students to understand. You will also learn that there are many points of view on what constitutes academic honesty and plagiarism. But you will, by the end of the class, come to a better understanding of the tricky concept of academic honesty in writing.

You are expected to do academically honest work from the beginning of class. Academic honesty in this class includes: 

Abiding by Park University's  [link: http://www.park.edu/park-distance-learning/academic-policies.html#academic-honesty] Academic Honesty Policy

Ensuring that all work you turn in for class is your own original work

Creating discussion board posts that reflect your own thoughts and responses

Being open and honest about the sources you consulted when completing your assignments

Providing clear attribution and documentation for all ideas and information that originate outside your own experience and analysis

Including quotation marks around any written material that originates in an outside source -- in other words, any material in your assignments that you did not write
