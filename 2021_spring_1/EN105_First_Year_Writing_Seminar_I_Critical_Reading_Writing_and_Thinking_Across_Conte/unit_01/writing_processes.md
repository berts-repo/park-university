# Unit 1: Writing Processes

Freewriting & Looping

Introduction

Welcome to your first mini-lecture on writing processes. Each mini-lecture is designed to introduce you to a writing process that will help you to develop ideas for your writing tasks. Each unit, you will read about a different writing process, and then practice that process in your Writer's Journal assignment. This unit, you will learn to use freewriting and looping to develop an idea.

Freewriting

Freewriting is a writing technique closely associated with the writing teacher and researcher Peter Elbow, who describes the process in his books Writing Without Teachers and Writing With Power. In a freewrite, your goal is to generate as much text as you possibly can in a short period of time. Later in your writing process, you will expand, develop, revise, or edit your freewriting into clear, readable prose -- but at first, your goal is to simply get words on paper (or screen).

To freewrite, first open a blank word processing document, or gather a blank sheet of paper and a pen or pencil. You will also need a timer or stopwatch. For your first freewrite, set your timer to five minutes. In those five minutes, you should write without stopping, without judgment, and, most importantly, without changing, editing, or correcting your writing.

Freewriting often starts with a prompt. In your Writer's Journal this unit, you will begin with this prompt: I'll never forget the time I... Here is an example of what your freewrite might look like:

EXAMPLE FREEWRITE: 

Learning experiences. The first time learned about well what exactly. First time i learned to drive i was 9 or 10 my father had me drive the van around block. Wasnt tall enough to reach the pedals and see over steering wheel at the same time and i didnt know u drove wtih one foot. Okay, boring. Another learnign experience -- time I realized you could see things from somebody elses point of view. Mr. Mair in history class, he had half the class argue in favor of declaring independence from English from ENGLAND, half the class argue in favor of staying united with England. This was 1760s 1770s, Revolutionary war times. I did the stay with England side even though I didnt really agree myself but I learned some about why people did. Trying to understanding ideas. I guess. 

Notice that the sentence structure, diction, punctuation, and even some of the spelling is incorrect! For a freewrite, mistakes are just fine. In a freewrite, your job is not to produce a perfectly polished piece of writing. Instead, your job is to generate ideas which you can later refine through rewriting and revision. 

A freewrite is where you are free to write anything that comes to mind. Freewrites may most useful to you at the beginning of your writing process, when you are searching for a topic, an argument, a position, or angle on your subject matter. In fact, in his books, Peter Elbow compares freewriting to growing plants -- the idea is to feed your writing and allow it to develop on its own.

At first, many students find freewriting intimidating, even irritating. But with practice, many students find it to be a useful, even indispensable tool for writing.

Looping

Looping is a strategy that takes a freewrite and deepens it. Your first step in a "loop" is to re-read your freewrite. As you read, underline, highlight, or circle any and all words, phrases, sentences, or ideas that strike you as worthy of closer attention. Hint: look for words and phrases that surprise you, or for ideas that you know you want to explore further.

I guess....

After highlighting those phrases, compose another freewrite in which you use your highlighted phrase as a prompt. Try to more deeply explore those ideas in your first loop. Set a timer for anywhere between 2 and 10 minutes for your first loop.

EXAMPLE FIRST LOOP

Seeing things from somebody elses point of view. The first time I learned to do that was in Mr. Mair's history class where he had the class do a debate on whether or not to secede from England. We were roleplaying, half the class in favor of staying united with England and half the class arguing in favor of declaring independence. Loyalists -- that's what it's called! I was in the loyalist camp, even though I didn't actually agree myself. We had three classes to research our side. Our loyalist team split up the different parts of the chapter and I had to read something by somebody maybe Burk was his name? Don't remember. Anyway...

Your first loop will probably be a better piece of writing than your initial freewrite: more developed, thoughtful, and textured. You can then use your first loop to compose a second loop: highlight the intriguing phrases, and use those as prompts for your second loop:

EXAMPLE SECOND LOOP

The Loyalists in the pre-Revolutionary War era seemed to favor order. They worried about mob rule, tar-and-featherings, and the loss of trade when ties with England were cut. There were even some black Loyalists who wanted to end slavery in the colonies. As I read more and more about the Loyalists' position on independence, the more I realized how complicated history was. It wasn't simply a game of good guys versus bad guys, or freedom overcoming oppression: history was the battle of competing ideas.

Freewriting and looping can be an effective and efficient technique for developing your ideas beyond your initial inspiration. As you'll learn this semester, good college writing is distinctive in its clarity of thought, its explicit arguments, and its development and depth. Freewriting and looping can help you develop all three important characteristics.
