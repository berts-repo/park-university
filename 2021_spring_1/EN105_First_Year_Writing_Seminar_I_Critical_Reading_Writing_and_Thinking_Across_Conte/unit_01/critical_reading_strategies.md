# Unit 1: Critical Reading Strategies

Identifying Writer and Audience

Introduction

Welcome to your first mini-lecture on critical reading strategies. Each critical reading strategies mini-lecture is designed to introduce you to techniques that will help you understand, interpret, and evaluate what you read, both in college and in the everyday world. This unit, you will focus on identifying how writer and audience can help you better understand a complex reading assignment.

Who's The Writer?

Read these texts. (In this class, "text" does not mean "text message;" instead, "text" refers to any piece of writing.)

Text 1
English teachers -- at least the ones I know -- want to churn out thinkers who wield power through language. We want them to love books, but also to survive. We want them to read a lease in 10 years and know what they’re getting into. We also want to turn out good citizens who practice in the streets and at the office what they identify as moral and good in class, people who do not cheat, manipulate, abuse, and unfairly judge others. English teachers, it seems, are in a unique position to impose some degree of emotional and moral rigor on the curriculum.

Text 2
The most important structural divisions among higher educational institutions in the United States are those due to selectivity and wealth, the highest degrees offered and the level of research intensity among those offering the doctorate, and the locus of governing authority. This assertion is supported by statistical analyses indicating that institutions defined by these structural characteristics tend to cluster together and the fact that presidents tend to identify with other colleges and universities that are similar to their own in terms of these criteria (Brint, Riddle, and Hanneman 2006; see also Reuf and Nag 2014). These divides also have parallels in the organization of the main higher education associations.

Even without any background knowledge, what might you guess about the author of each text? You might have guessed that the author of Text 1 is a teacher. The use of the word "we" suggests that the author is an English teacher. What's more, the author uses relatively straightforward language, and even colloquialisms; for instance, the writer uses phrases like "know what they're getting into," interjections like "at least the ones I know," and metaphorical verbs like "churn out." These phrases give the text a conversational quality. Based simply on the language that the writer uses, we can infer that this author is a teacher who cares deeply about students and student learning. 

You probably decided within five or six words that the author of Text 2 was a scholar or researcher. The writer uses specialized diction, or word choice. Words and phrases like "structural divisions," "locus," "assertion," and "statistical analyses" suggest that this writer is a specialist on higher education research. You probably also noticed that this writer has also used an academic style of source citation in order to document the sources of information he or she is relying on.

Texts don't just come into being. Texts are always written -- and that means that behind every text lies a writer. Careful readers make sure to identify the writer or writers of any given text, in order to better understand and analyze the meaning of a text.

Who's The Audience?

Now, think about the audience of each text. To whom do you imagine each writer is writing to? What sorts of readers has the writer imagined? And how do those imagined readers affect the writer's choices?

You probably determined that Andrew Simmons, the author of Text 1 ("Literature's Emotional Lessons"), was writing to a broad audience of people interested in high school education: perhaps students, teachers, and parents. His audience is probably limited to Americans, given his subject matter. His writing appeared in a general-interest magazine called The Atlantic, which tends to feature in-depth articles aimed at an educated, but general audience.

Knowing about Simmons's audience helps us understand some of his choices. For example, Simmons uses casual language not just because he wants to present himself as relatable, but also because he is trying to reach a broad audience. Conversational style can often spark a reader's interest in the subject matter.

Compare Simmons's audience with the audience of Steven Brint and Charles T. Clotfelter, authors of Text 2 ("U.S. Higher Education Effectiveness"). When you clicked through to the original source, you probably saw that the writers were professors at prestigious universities, and that they published their writing in something called RSF: The Russell Sage Foundation Journal of the Social Sciences. Right away, you can guess that Brint and Clotfelter intend their work to be read by other specialists and researchers -- social scientists, public policy experts, and so on.

Once again, knowing about Brint and Clotfelter's audience helps us understand their writing choices. Because they assume that their readers are trained in the methods of social science research, Brint and Clotfelter can use specialized vocabulary without defining every term. Likewise, Brint and Clotfelter are careful to cite the sources of their information, because they know that academic audiences expect careful, clear documentation of every piece of evidence. You may have had a difficult time understanding Text 2, but now that you understand the writers' intended audience, you can better understand the writers' choices.

Why is it important to identify a writer's intended audience? Because a smart interpretation of any text takes into account how well the writer is likely to have reached the intended audience. Understanding audience helps us to understand why a writer made the choices he or she did, and helps us to better interpret and evaluate the message of a text.

In this week's mini-lecture on rhetorical concepts, you will learn a little more about how writing theorists have defined writer, audience, and subject. In this week's discussion, you will practice identifying writer, audience, and subject in your readings.
