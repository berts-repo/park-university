# Unit 1: Thinking About Rhetorical Concepts

The Rhetorical Triangle

Introduction

Welcome to your first mini-lecture on rhetorical concepts. Each mini-lecture is designed to help you understand the theories and concepts behind the reading and writing strategies you are practicing. These rhetorical concepts are broadly applicable ideas that can help you succeed as a reader, writer, speaker, and communicator in many different contexts. This unit, you will focus on what writing researchers sometimes call the rhetorical triangle.

The Rhetorical Triangle

Think back to this week's mini-lecture on identifying writer and audience, and to the reading you have done from Ruszkiewicz. You are probably starting to understand that to understand a text, you must not simply read the words, but also analyze the background of the text: writer, audience, and subject.

Some writing researchers propose that different types of texts gravitate toward one point on the rhetorical triangle or another. For instance, writing that seems to focus mostly on the subject matter tends to be informative writing. Lab reports, news stories, expository writing assignments, chapter summaries, nurses' notes, and classroom assignments are all examples of writing that is primarily informative. Informative writing seeks to convey a message about a subject as clearly and precisely as possible.

You are probably already thinking about writing that gravitates to other points on the triangle. Writing that centers on the writer tends to be expressive writing. Journals, diaries, personal narratives, therapeutic writing, even many tweets and Facebook posts generally try to focus on the writer more than anything else. If you write for yourself, as a hobby or as an escape, that writing is probably expressive in nature. 

Writing that focuses on the audience of readers tends to be persuasive in nature. Newspaper editorials, public speeches, sales pitches, most business correspondence, argumentative research papers, law briefs, and many other types of writing fit this category. In fact, most of the writing that you will do in college is persuasive in nature, and therefore focuses primarily on addressing a particular audience. That is why you will spend much of your time in this course composing persuasive writing that makes an argument.

The writing scholar James Kinneavy even suggested that there was a fourth category of writing: writing that focuses on the text itself. This sort of writing, according to Kinneavy, was literary or artistic writing. This is writing that seeks to be appreciated on its own terms, for its own sake. This category of literary writing includes most poetry, literary fiction, many personal essays, and any other form of writing that seeks to be appreciated for its own formal, artistic, or literary qualities.

Understanding the rhetorical triangle can help you to better understand not only what you are reading, but also what you are writing. You will use the rhetorical triangle all term to understand your assigned readings, your own major writing assignments, the writing you encounter on the web, and even some of your instructor's communications.
