# Unit 4: Writing Processes

Reflecting on Peer Review

Introduction

Welcome to this unit's mini-lecture on writing processes. This unit, you will learn about using peer review as a process for improving your own writing and that of your classmates.

Response & Feedback

Learning to give and receive feedback is a crucial step in developing a strong writing practice. The process of responding to your classmates' writing gives you valuable practice in critical reading and can help you to learn important lessons about audience and rhetoric. This mini-lecture will guide you in the process of reading, annotating, discussing, and writing about your classmates' drafts for this unit's peer review.

So, What Is Peer Review?

Peer review is a key aspect of academic writing. In the "real world" of academic publishing, academic writers publish their work only after receiving the advice, suggestions, and feedback of expert readers, who are called "peer reviewers." Peer reviewers provide response to...well, almost everything, including:

the writer's overall argument

the writer's use of logic

the interpretive models the writer uses to analyze data

the writer's connections to current critical conversations on the subject

the accuracy of the writer's use of outside sources

Usually, academic peer reviewers provide a recommendation to the publisher: publish, publish with revisions, or reject. 

After receiving anonymous peer reviews, academic writers usually revise their work significantly and re-submit for further review and, eventually, publication. (Very few academic writers publish work without performing significant revisions after peer review.)

One outcome of the peer review process is that readers of the final version of the writer's work can be reasonably assured that it has been picked apart, checked for reliability, and revised into its best possible shape. (For more on academic peer review, see the sections on "Reviewing and Improving The Draft" in most chapters of Reading Critically, Writing Well.)

But peer review is not just for professional scholars! In fact, peer review is a key tool also to good writing in the everyday world. As you have learned this term, "good writing" is writing that achieves its goals with real-life readers. So, what better way to gauge the success of your writing than to hear from those real-life readers?

Our Approach to Peer Review

Donald Murray, a scholar of teaching writing, outlined a useful approach to peer review in his book The Craft of Revision. In this course, you will use an adapted version of Murray's approach to peer review.

The first step in peer review is to set the agenda. Murray suggests that all writers have a few basic questions for readers to consider. These include:

What works?

What needs work?

As you can see, these are basic questions that are important for any reader to know: what is good about a draft, and what is not yet good. The responses that you give as a peer reviewer will help your classmates focus their revision efforts. Writers need feedback on these questions -- feedback that, in this course, will come via written letters. Peer reviewers will pinpoint precise aspects of your classmates' work, using posted agenda questions as a guide.

For instance, in this unit's peer review, you will be asked to place your classmates' draft into a genre, to analyze the writer's assumed audience, and to discuss the writer's use of ethos, pathos, and logos. In future peer reviews, peer reviewers may focus on evidence and argument, overall structure and organization, and use of sources.

As a peer reviewer, you will be asked to closely read, annotate, and respond to your classmates' drafts, using a posted set of agenda questions and your classmate's individual requests. You will write a brief letter, to your classmate, that answers the questions as they apply to his or her posted draft. This letter, you should address both the posted peer review questions and the writer's individual concerns. Here is the beginning of a sample peer review letter; read it, and notice how the peer reviewer provides honest responses while also suggesting possible edits or revisions.

Sample Peer Review Letter

First of all, thank you for letting me read your draft. It was fun to learn a little more about you and I think if you do some more work in revising, you will have something great on your hands.

First, what words and what doesn’t. I think your topic is somewhat clear: this essay is going to be about an experience that inspired you to pursue a career in law enforcement. That’s good, because many people don’t realize the passion and values that lie behind career choices. I'll be curious to see if your experience was negative or positive.

As far as what doesn't work, your introduction is a little bit disorganized. You start by describing criminal justice as an academic discipline, and then start talking about how many people distrust police, and finally start telling the story that you seem to want to tell. It’s a little confusing, and I wonder if you might be able to just start by telling your story. I was most interested starting at the sentence beginning, "For me, the thing that made me think differently about police was when my mother's car broke down on a road trip." This provides a good emotional appeal because it sounds like YOU writing. Your voice as a writer is what I respond to most.

You asked about ideas for expansion. Here's what I think…. 

You should note that this peer reviewer has not provided any kind of feedback on punctuation, spelling, or grammar. While those aspects of writing are very important, at early stages of the writing process, it is more worthwhile to pay attention to the overall message and organization of a draft, as well as its use of the three rhetorical appeals. Later in the writing process, editing can be useful -- and you will work on editing in the final units of the course.

One Last Thought

Learning to do good peer review takes practice. You may find it difficult at first to provide excellent peer review responses, but with practice in this course and future writing courses, you will hone your ability to use this writing process tool.
