White-water boating is a major new sport in America, and it involves using canoes or kayaks.  Both canoes and kayaks have unique American histories; in fact, canoes are sleek boats, and they were developed by Native Americans of the Northeast and Great Lakes region.  Originally canoes were made of wood their hulls, and had a deep V-shape;  later, a frame of strong wooden ribs was made, and then a covering of scraped bark was laid over the frame.  Although modern canoes are made of aluminum or fiberglass, yet the design has never really changed.  A canoe's hull will carry mountains of equipment, but the boat itself is light enough to be carried by one or two people. Canoes are versatile, consequently, they can be used on lakes or in mountain rapids.

            Kayaks were developed by the Eskimos of northern Canada and Greenland. The  original kayaks had  wood or bone frames with seal skin stretched over them; therefor, kayaks are even more lightweight than canoes. Today's kayaks may be made of plastic, aluminum, or fiberglass, like modern canoes, they are designed to hold only one or two people. The passengers sit flat on the bottom of a kayak, and a watertight flap seals them into the craft.  Today's kayak is watertight and almost impossible to sink, and if it turns upside down; the pilot can turn it back upright with a twist of the hips, and this maneuver is called "the Eskimo roll," to honor its inventors.  Kayak pilots carry a single oar with two blades, for they can use this oar to paddle rapidly or to fend off rocks and obstacles.
